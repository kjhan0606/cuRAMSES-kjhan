\begin{abstract}
Cosmological AMR simulations employ a \emph{holdback} mechanism that
progressively releases refinement levels as the universe expands,
preventing premature over-refinement at high redshift.  However, this
mechanism blocks \emph{all} refinement criteria simultaneously, including
the Jeans length criterion, which is a numerical stability requirement
rather than merely a resolution preference.  When a holdback level is
finally released, a large number of cells suddenly satisfy the
refinement condition, triggering an abrupt starburst.  We implement a
\code{jeans\_bypass\_holdback} option that allows the Jeans criterion to
operate independently of the holdback, with a configurable level limit
(\code{n\_jeans\_bypass\_levels}) to prevent uncontrolled refinement
cascades.  We discuss the interplay between the Jeans criterion, the
polytropic equation of state, and the density threshold
\code{d\_jeans\_thre}, showing that the polytropic temperature floor
provides a natural self-regulation mechanism for the bypass.
\end{abstract}

\section{AMR Refinement in \ramses}
\label{sec:amr_refine}
%======================================================================

In \ramses, each cell at level $\ell < \nlevelmax$ is tested for
refinement at every coarse time-step.  The decision to subdivide a
cell into $\twotondim$ children is governed by a combination of
physical, geometric, and cosmological criteria.

\subsection{Refinement Logic}
\label{sec:refine_logic}

The overall refinement flag for a cell is determined by the following
Boolean expression:
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{R} = \lnot\,\mathcal{H}
  \;\land\; \mathcal{G}
  \;\land\; \bigl(
    \mathcal{M} \lor \mathcal{S} \lor \mathcal{D} \lor \mathcal{P}
    \lor \mathcal{U} \lor \mathcal{J}
  \bigr),
\label{eq:refine_flag}
\end{equation}
where each symbol is defined below.

\subsubsection{Physical Criteria (OR)}

\begin{enumerate}

\item \textbf{Quasi-Lagrangian mass criterion ($\mathcal{M}$).}
  The cell is flagged if its gas density exceeds a
  level-dependent threshold:
  \begin{equation}
    \rho_{\rm cell} \;\ge\; m_{\rm refine}(\ell)
      \;\frac{m_{\rm sph}}{V_{\rm cell}},
  \end{equation}
  where $m_{\rm sph}$ is the effective SPH kernel mass and
  $V_{\rm cell} = (\dx_\ell)^3$.
  This criterion ensures that regions containing roughly
  $m_{\rm refine}$ or more particles per cell are refined,
  providing a quasi-Lagrangian resolution that tracks gravitational
  collapse.  Controlled by the namelist array
  \code{m\_refine(1:nlevelmax)}.

\item \textbf{Sink particle forcing ($\mathcal{S}$).}
  If \code{sink\_refine=.true.}, any cell containing a sink cloud
  particle (identified by $\code{idp} < 0$ and $\code{tp} = 0$) is
  unconditionally flagged up to $\nlevelmax$, guaranteeing maximum
  resolution around each accretion region.

\item \textbf{Density gradient ($\mathcal{D}$).}
  The normalised density gradient between a cell and each of its
  $2N_{\rm dim}$ face-neighbours is computed as
  \begin{equation}
    \epsilon_\rho = 2\,\max\!\left(
      \frac{|\rho_+ - \rho_0|}{|\rho_+ + \rho_0| + \rho_{\rm floor}},\;
      \frac{|\rho_0 - \rho_-|}{|\rho_0 + \rho_-| + \rho_{\rm floor}}
    \right).
  \end{equation}
  If $\epsilon_\rho > \code{err\_grad\_d}$ along any axis, the cell
  is flagged.

\item \textbf{Pressure gradient ($\mathcal{P}$).}
  Analogous to the density gradient but applied to the thermal
  pressure:
  \begin{equation}
    \epsilon_P = 2\,\max\!\left(
      \frac{|P_+ - P_0|}{|P_+ + P_0| + P_{\rm floor}},\;
      \frac{|P_0 - P_-|}{|P_0 + P_-| + P_{\rm floor}}
    \right),
  \end{equation}
  flagging when $\epsilon_P > \code{err\_grad\_p}$.

\item \textbf{Velocity gradient ($\mathcal{U}$).}
  A normalised velocity jump relative to the local sound speed:
  \begin{equation}
    \epsilon_v = 2\,\max\!\left(
      \frac{|v_+ - v_0|}{c_{s,+} + c_{s,0} + |v_+| + |v_0|
                          + v_{\rm floor}},\;
      \frac{|v_0 - v_-|}{c_{s,0} + c_{s,-} + |v_0| + |v_-|
                          + v_{\rm floor}}
    \right),
  \end{equation}
  flagging when $\epsilon_v > \code{err\_grad\_u}$ in any component.

\item \textbf{Jeans length ($\mathcal{J}$).}
  When \code{jeans\_refine($\ell$)} $= N_J > 0$, the cell is flagged if
  the local Jeans length is shorter than $N_J$ cell widths:
  \begin{equation}
    \lJ = \sqrt{\frac{\pi\,c_s^2}{G\,\rho}}
    < N_J\,\dx_\ell,
    \label{eq:jeans_crit}
  \end{equation}
  implementing the \citet{Truelove1997} condition to prevent artificial
  fragmentation in self-gravitating gas.  An additional density
  threshold \code{d\_jeans\_thre} restricts this criterion to cells
  with $n_{\rm H} \ge \code{d\_jeans\_thre}$.

\end{enumerate}

\subsubsection{Geometry Mask (AND)}

\textbf{Ellipsoidal region ($\mathcal{G}$).}
When \code{r\_refine($\ell$)} $> -1$, only cells inside a generalised
ellipsoidal region are permitted to refine.  The test is
\begin{equation}
  \left(
    \frac{2|\Delta x|}{r}
  \right)^{e_r}
  + \left(
    \frac{2|\Delta y|}{a\,r}
  \right)^{e_r}
  + \left(
    \frac{2|\Delta z|}{b\,r}
  \right)^{e_r}
  < 1,
\end{equation}
where $r = \code{r\_refine}$, $a = \code{a\_refine}$,
$b = \code{b\_refine}$, and $e_r = \code{exp\_refine}$.
Cells outside this region are \emph{un-flagged} regardless of
physical criteria, restricting high resolution to a zoom-in target.

\subsubsection{Cosmological Holdback (NOT)}

\textbf{Resolution holdback ($\mathcal{H}$).}
In cosmological runs with cooling, overly eager refinement at high
redshift can exhaust memory before the epoch of interest.
When \code{q\_refine\_holdback=.true.} and the physical cell size
$\dx_\ell < 4^{1/N_{\rm dim}}\,\dx_{\nlevelmax}/a_{\rm exp}$
for levels $\ell > \code{nlevelmax\_part} + 3$, the \emph{entire}
level is blocked from further refinement:
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{H}(\ell) = \bigl[\ell > \ell_{\rm part} + \ell_{\rm collapse}\bigr]
  \;\wedge\;
  \bigl[\dx_\ell < 4^{1/N_{\rm dim}}\,\dx_{\rm min}/a\bigr],
  \label{eq:holdback}
\end{equation}
where $\ell_{\rm part}$ is the particle initial-condition level
(typically $\ell_{\rm min}$) and $\ell_{\rm collapse}=3$.
The first holdback level $\ell_{\rm hb}$ is the smallest $\ell$
satisfying Equation~\eqref{eq:holdback}:
\begin{equation}
  \ell_{\rm hb} = \min\bigl\{\ell > \ell_{\rm part}+\ell_{\rm collapse}
  \;:\; \dx_\ell < 4^{1/N_{\rm dim}}\,\dx_{\rm min}/a\bigr\}.
  \label{eq:lhb}
\end{equation}

\subsection{Buffer Zones}

After applying Equation~(\ref{eq:refine_flag}), flagged cells are
expanded by \code{nexpand($\ell$)} cell widths in each direction via a
dilation operator (\code{smooth\_fine}).
This cubic buffer ensures smooth refinement boundaries, preventing
the Godunov solver from encountering abrupt level transitions.


%======================================================================
\section{The Starburst Problem}
\label{sec:starburst}
%======================================================================

The holdback mechanism blocks \emph{all} refinement criteria at levels
$\ell \ge \ell_{\rm hb}$, including the Jeans criterion.  This creates
two related problems:

\paragraph{Artificial fragmentation.}
The Jeans length criterion $\mathcal{J}$ differs fundamentally from
the other refinement triggers: it is a \emph{numerical stability}
requirement \citep{Truelove1997}, not merely a resolution preference.
Violating the Jeans condition leads to artificial fragmentation ---
a qualitatively wrong result --- regardless of epoch.  Yet in the
standard \ramses\ logic (Equation~\ref{eq:refine_flag}), the
cosmological holdback $\mathcal{H}$ blocks $\mathcal{J}$ uniformly
with all other criteria.  At high redshift, dense self-gravitating
regions may therefore be held at insufficient resolution, producing
spurious fragmentation that propagates into lower-redshift structure.

\paragraph{Abrupt SFR transition.}
When the holdback releases level $\ell$ (i.e.\ $\mathcal{H}(\ell)$
transitions from true to false), all accumulated criteria fire
simultaneously.  Many cells that have been gravitationally collapsing
during the holdback period now suddenly satisfy the refinement
condition, leading to a burst of new cells and an abrupt spike in
the star formation rate.

\section{Jeans Bypass: Design and Implementation}
\label{sec:bypass}
%======================================================================

\subsection{Modified Refinement Logic}

The key observation is that the Jeans length criterion is
\emph{cell-local}: it depends only on the density and temperature of the
cell itself (\code{uold}), with no neighbor information required.  This
allows a lightweight evaluation even when the full refinement machinery
(which requires neighbor gathering for $\mathcal{D}$, $\mathcal{P}$,
$\mathcal{U}$) is skipped by the holdback.

With \code{jeans\_bypass\_holdback=.true.}, the refinement logic becomes
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{R}' = \Bigl[
  \lnot\,\mathcal{H} \;\land\; \mathcal{G} \;\land\;
  \bigl(\mathcal{M} \lor \mathcal{S} \lor \mathcal{D}
        \lor \mathcal{P} \lor \mathcal{U} \lor \mathcal{J}\bigr)
\Bigr]
\;\lor\;
\Bigl[ \mathcal{H} \;\land\; \mathcal{J} \;\land\;
  (\ell < \ell_{\rm hb} + n_{\rm bypass}) \Bigr],
\label{eq:refine_bypass}
\end{equation}
where $n_{\rm bypass} \equiv$ \code{n\_jeans\_bypass\_levels}
(default 1).  The geometry mask $\mathcal{G}$ still applies through the
non-holdback branch, preserving zoom-in spatial restrictions.  Only the
temporal holdback $\mathcal{H}$ is bypassed for $\mathcal{J}$, and only
within a limited number of levels.

This modification is analogous to the existing treatment of sink
particles: $\mathcal{S}$ is already evaluated inside the
holdback-protected block, but unlike $\mathcal{J}$ it only triggers in
cells that already host a sink.  Extending the same exemption to
$\mathcal{J}$ ensures numerical integrity for gas that is
\emph{about to} form a sink.


\subsection{Level Limitation: Preventing Cascades}
\label{sec:cascade}

Without a level limit ($n_{\rm bypass}\to\infty$), the Jeans bypass
creates a catastrophic cascade.  When a cell at the holdback level
$\ell_{\rm hb}$ is flagged by the Jeans criterion, child cells are
created at level $\ell_{\rm hb}+1$.  Since level $\ell_{\rm hb}+1$ is
also in the holdback zone, the bypass applies again, flagging cells at
$\ell_{\rm hb}+2$, and so on until $\ell_{\rm max}$.  This rapidly
exhausts available memory.

The cascade occurs because the Jeans length in radiatively cooling gas
(below the star-formation density threshold) shrinks without bound as
the temperature drops.  There is no physical mechanism to halt the
Jeans criterion from triggering at every successive level --- only the
polytropic temperature floor (Section~\ref{sec:polytropic}) or a hard
level limit can stop it.

without level limit.

creating cells at level 15 but no further.  This provides a controlled
pre-refinement before the holdback releases.

\section{Role of the Polytropic Temperature Floor}
\label{sec:polytropic}
%======================================================================

\subsection{Jeans Length in the Polytropic Regime}

In \ramses, the star-formation module enforces a polytropic equation
of state above the density threshold $\nstar$:
\begin{equation}
  T(n) = \Tstar\,\Bigl(\frac{n}{\nstar}\Bigr)^{\gstar - 1},
  \qquad n \ge \nstar,
  \label{eq:polytropic}
\end{equation}
with default parameters $\Tstar = 750\,{\rm K}$,
$\nstar = 0.1\,{\rm cm}^{-3}$, $\gstar = 5/3$.  This floor is
applied in the cooling routine and is already reflected in \code{uold},
so the Jeans length computation in \code{jeans\_length\_refine()}
automatically includes the polytropic stabilisation.

Substituting Equation~\eqref{eq:polytropic} into
Equation~\eqref{eq:jeans_crit}:
\begin{equation}
  \lJ \propto \sqrt{\frac{T}{\rho}} \propto
  \sqrt{\rho^{\gstar-1}/\rho} = \rho^{(\gstar-2)/2}.
  \label{eq:jeans_poly}
\end{equation}
For $\gstar = 5/3$: $\lJ \propto \rho^{-1/6}$.  The Jeans length
\emph{decreases very slowly} with density --- one or two additional
levels of refinement are typically sufficient to resolve it.

In contrast, for gas below $\nstar$ (no polytropic floor), radiative
cooling can drive $T$ to very low values while $\rho$ remains moderate.
This yields $\lJ \ll \dx_\ell$ at many levels, causing the cascade.

\subsection{Self-regulation via \code{d\_jeans\_thre}}

The density threshold parameter \code{d\_jeans\_thre} (in units of
$n_{\rm H}\,[{\rm cm}^{-3}]$) restricts the Jeans criterion to cells
above a given density:
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{J}(\text{cell}) =
  \begin{cases}
    N_J\,\dx_\ell \ge \lJ & \text{if } n \ge
    \code{d\_jeans\_thre}, \\
    \text{false} & \text{otherwise}.
  \end{cases}
\end{equation}

Setting $\code{d\_jeans\_thre} \approx \nstar$ restricts the Jeans
criterion to the polytropic regime.  The cascade then self-limits
through the following mechanism:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item At level $\ell_{\rm hb}$: only cells with $n \ge \nstar$ are
    checked.  The polytropic floor guarantees $T \ge \Tstar$, so $\lJ$
    is relatively large.
  \item If $N_J\,\dx_{\ell_{\rm hb}} > \lJ$, the cell is flagged and
    children at $\ell_{\rm hb}+1$ are created.
  \item At level $\ell_{\rm hb}+1$: $\dx$ is halved, density is
    approximately unchanged, $\lJ$ is approximately unchanged.
  \item $N_J\,\dx_{\ell_{\rm hb}+1} = N_J\,\dx_{\ell_{\rm hb}}/2
    < \lJ$ $\Rightarrow$ Jeans criterion is \emph{satisfied}
    $\Rightarrow$ no further flagging $\Rightarrow$ cascade stops
    naturally.
\end{enumerate}

This provides a \emph{physics-based} self-regulation, complementary to
the \emph{hard limit} imposed by \code{n\_jeans\_bypass\_levels}.

\begin{deluxetable}{lll}
\tablecaption{Two complementary mechanisms for preventing the bypass
cascade.\label{tab:regulators}}
\tablehead{
\colhead{Mechanism} & \colhead{Type} & \colhead{Behaviour}
}
\startdata
\code{n\_jeans\_bypass\_levels} & Hard limit &
  Caps extra levels regardless of physics \\
\code{d\_jeans\_thre} $\approx \nstar$ & Physics-based &
  Polytropic floor limits $\lJ$ decrease \\
\enddata
\end{deluxetable}

When \code{d\_jeans\_thre}$\,=0$ (default), \emph{all} cells are
checked, including low-density gas where cooling produces arbitrarily
small $\lJ$.  This is the root cause of the unlimited cascade observed
in Section~\ref{sec:cascade}.

\section{Implementation Details}
\label{sec:implementation}
%======================================================================

\subsection{Code Structure}

The implementation modifies two source files (plus parameter declaration
and namelist reading).

\paragraph{\code{flag\_utils.kjhan.f90} --- \code{userflag\_fine()}}
The holdback early-return is modified from a simple \code{return} to a
conditional block:

\begin{lstlisting}
if(prevent_refine .and. q_refine_holdback) then
   if(jeans_bypass_holdback .and. hydro &
      .and. n_jeans_bypass_levels > 0) then
      ! Find the first holdback level
      dx_min = (0.5D0**nlevelmax)*scale
      holdback_threshold = &
        (4d0**(1d0/ndim))*(dx_min/aexp)
      holdback_first = nlevelmax + 1
      do jlevel = nlevelmax_part &
         + nlevel_collapse+1, nlevelmax
         dx_test = (0.5d0**jlevel)*scale
         if(dx_test < holdback_threshold) then
            holdback_first = jlevel
            exit
         endif
      end do
      ! Only bypass within allowed range
      if(ilevel < holdback_first &
         + n_jeans_bypass_levels) then
         call jeans_only_flag(ilevel)
      endif
   endif
   return
endif
\end{lstlisting}

\paragraph{\code{hydro\_flag.kjhan.f90} --- \code{jeans\_only\_flag()}}
A new lightweight subroutine that:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item Loops over active grids with OpenMP parallelism.
  \item Computes cell indices without neighbor gathering.
  \item Calls the existing \code{jeans\_length\_refine()} subroutine
    (no code duplication).
  \item Sets \code{flag1} for flagged cells and updates \code{nflag}.
\end{enumerate}

This is possible because \code{jeans\_length\_refine()} only accesses
cell-local data (\code{uold(ind\_cell,...)}), unlike the gradient-based
criteria $\mathcal{D}$, $\mathcal{P}$, $\mathcal{U}$.

\subsection{Safety Properties}

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Default off}: With
    \code{jeans\_bypass\_holdback=.false.} (default), the new code path
    is never entered.  Bit-identical to the unmodified code.
  \item \textbf{No neighbor access}: \code{jeans\_only\_flag()} is
    safe to call at any point in the refinement cycle.
  \item \textbf{OMP safety}: \code{flag1} writes are grid-partitioned;
    \code{nflag} uses reduction.
  \item \textbf{Hard level limit}: \code{n\_jeans\_bypass\_levels}
    provides a fail-safe even if \code{d\_jeans\_thre} is misconfigured.
\end{itemize}

\section{Test Results}
\label{sec:tests}
%======================================================================

All tests use the HR5 medium-box setup: $\ell_{\rm min}=9$,
$\ell_{\rm max}=17$, 12 MPI ranks, OMP=5, restarting from output 5
($a=0.164$, $z\approx 5.1$, nstep\_coarse=241).

\subsection{Bit-identical Verification}

With \code{jeans\_bypass\_holdback=.false.} (default) and no
\code{jeans\_refine}, the new binary produces results identical to
the reference run: econs=$6.13\times10^{-4}$ at step 242,
econs=$6.71\times10^{-4}$ at step 243.

\subsection{Unlimited Bypass}

With \code{jeans\_refine=9*4.},
\code{jeans\_bypass\_holdback=.true.}, \code{d\_jeans\_thre=0}, and
no level limit: cascade refinement from level 14 through 17 within a
single coarse step.  Memory grew from 81\% to 88.5\% before OOM crash.
Fine-step timestep decreased by $8\times$ due to subcycling.

\subsection{Limited Bypass ($n_{\rm bypass}=1$)}

With \code{n\_jeans\_bypass\_levels=1}: only level 14 is bypassed,
cells at level 15 may be created but no further cascade.

\subsection{Future: $\code{d\_jeans\_thre}=\nstar$}

A test with \code{d\_jeans\_thre=0.1} will verify the physics-based
self-regulation, potentially allowing $n_{\rm bypass} > 1$ without
cascade, since only gas in the polytropic regime would trigger the
Jeans criterion.

\section{Cooling Module: Limitations and Alternatives}
\label{sec:cooling}
%======================================================================

The cooling module is a critical component of the simulation.  It
determines the equilibrium temperature of the gas, which in turn
controls the Jeans length $\lJ \propto \sqrt{T/\rho}$ and hence the
refinement decisions through the Jeans criterion~$\mathcal{J}$.  Errors
in the cooling calculation --- whether from the cooling rate tables, the
UV background model, or the numerical integration scheme --- propagate
directly into errors in $\lJ$ and may trigger (or suppress) refinement
at the wrong density and temperature.

In this section we first analyse the limitations of the standard
\ramses\ cooling solver, then compare its underlying cooling rates
against the Grackle equilibrium chemistry library
\citep{Smith2017}, and finally describe an alternative exact
integration scheme based on the Townsend (2009) Y-function method
\citep{Townsend2009}.


\subsection{The Standard \ramses\ Cooling Solver}
\label{sec:ramses_cooling}

The \ramses\ cooling module solves the energy equation
\begin{equation}
  \frac{dT_2}{dt} = -\frac{2 X_{\rm H}}{3 k_{\rm B}}\,
  \frac{\Lambda_{\rm net}(n_{\rm H}, T_2)}{n_{\rm H}},
  \label{eq:cooling_ode}
\end{equation}
where $T_2 \equiv T/\mu$ is the temperature per mean molecular weight
and $\Lambda_{\rm net}$ is the net volumetric cooling rate
(cooling $-$ heating) in $\rm erg\,cm^{-3}\,s^{-1}$.

The solver uses a semi-implicit Newton--Raphson iteration.  At each
sub-step, the temperature is updated as
\begin{equation}
  T_2^{(n+1)} = T_2^{(n)}\,
  \frac{1 + \Lambda'/w_{\rm cool} - \Lambda/(T_2^{(n)}\,w_{\rm cool})}
       {1 + \Lambda'/w_{\rm cool}},
  \label{eq:NR_update}
\end{equation}
where $\Lambda' \equiv d\Lambda_{\rm net}/dT_2$ and the adaptive step
control parameter is
$w_{\rm cool} = \max\bigl(|\Lambda|/(T_2\,\Delta_{\rm var}),\;
w_{\rm max},\;{-\Lambda'\,\Delta_{\rm var}}\bigr)$
with $\Delta_{\rm var} = 4$.

The cooling rates $\Lambda_{\rm net}$ and their derivatives are
interpolated from a pre-computed 2D table
$\Lambda(n_{\rm H}, T_2)$, built at the start of the simulation for the
current expansion factor $a$.  The table contains separate contributions
from:
\begin{itemize}
  \item H/He collisional cooling (ionisation, recombination,
    bremsstrahlung, excitation) computed from collisional ionisation
    equilibrium (CIE);
  \item UV photo-heating from the \citet{Courty2004} background model;
  \item Compton cooling/heating off the CMB;
  \item metal cooling from the Cloudy~07 table
    \citep{Ploeckinger2015}, scaled linearly with metallicity~$Z$.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Overshooting Near Thermal Equilibrium}
\label{sec:overshoot}

The Newton--Raphson scheme (Equation~\ref{eq:NR_update}) is designed
for monotonic cooling (or heating).  However, near the thermal
equilibrium temperature $T_{\rm eq}$ where
$\Lambda_{\rm net}(T_{\rm eq}) = 0$, the net rate changes sign and the
update can overshoot.  Specifically:

\paragraph{Sign reversal.}
Consider a cell at temperature $T > T_{\rm eq}$ that is cooling
($\Lambda_{\rm net} > 0$).  A Newton--Raphson step decreases $T_2$.
If the step is large enough to cross $T_{\rm eq}$, the cell enters the
heating regime ($\Lambda_{\rm net} < 0$).  On the next iteration, the
solver increases $T_2$, potentially overshooting back above $T_{\rm eq}$.
The result is an oscillation around $T_{\rm eq}$ that converges only
after many sub-steps, or in the worst case does not converge within the
500-iteration limit.

\paragraph{Steep cooling function.}
The problem is exacerbated where the cooling function has a steep slope
--- in particular, near the hydrogen Lyman-$\alpha$ peak at
$T \sim 10^4\,{\rm K}$ and the metal cooling peak at
$T \sim 10^{4.5}$--$10^{5.5}\,{\rm K}$.  In these temperature ranges,
$|\Lambda'|$ is large and the linearised N-R step can dramatically
overshoot.  The adaptive step control $w_{\rm cool}$ mitigates this by
limiting $\Delta T_2 / T_2 \le \Delta_{\rm var}$, but for cells
undergoing rapid cooling (large $|\Lambda|/T_2$), the effective
sub-step can still be too large to track the equilibrium accurately.

\paragraph{Cumulative error.}
Because $T_2$ enters the next hydrodynamic time-step as the updated
thermal energy, any overshoot at step $n$ feeds back into the pressure
and hence the Jeans length at step $n{+}1$.  A cell that overshoots to
$T < T_{\rm eq}$ will have an artificially low Jeans length, potentially
triggering spurious refinement.  Conversely, a cell that undershoots
(remains at $T > T_{\rm eq}$) may fail to trigger the Jeans criterion
when it should.

\paragraph{Time-step coupling.}
The maximum sub-step size within \code{solve\_cooling} is determined by
the cooling time $t_{\rm cool} = (3/2)\,n k_{\rm B} T / |\Lambda_{\rm net}|$.
When $\Lambda_{\rm net}$ is large, the solver takes many small sub-steps
and is accurate.  But near equilibrium, $|\Lambda_{\rm net}|$ is small,
$t_{\rm cool}$ is large, and the solver takes fewer, larger steps ---
precisely where accuracy matters most for determining the equilibrium
temperature.

\subsubsection{Limitations of the 2D Table}
\label{sec:2d_table}

Beyond the numerical integration, the cooling \emph{rates} themselves
have systematic biases:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{No metallicity axis.}
    The \ramses\ table is 2D: $(n_{\rm H}, T_2)$.  Metal cooling is
    stored as a separate 1D function $\Lambda_Z(T)$ at solar
    metallicity, then scaled as $Z \cdot \Lambda_Z$.  This linear scaling
    is correct for CIE but neglects the density-dependent ionisation
    corrections that become important at $n_{\rm H} > 1\,{\rm cm}^{-3}$,
    where photo-ionisation by the UV background cannot penetrate and the
    gas is self-shielded.

  \item \textbf{CIE assumption.}
    The mean molecular weight $\mu$ is computed assuming collisional
    ionisation equilibrium at each $(n_{\rm H}, T)$ point.  This neglects
    time-dependent ionisation (important during rapid cooling) and
    photo-ionisation at low temperatures (important in the diffuse IGM
    at $T < 10^4\,{\rm K}$), where the UV background maintains a
    residual ionisation fraction that CIE would predict to be negligible.

  \item \textbf{UV background model.}
    The default Courty model is a polynomial fit calibrated against
    Cloudy~07.  More recent UV background models
    \citep[e.g.][]{HM2012,FG2020} differ significantly at $z > 3$,
    particularly in the He~{\sc ii} reionisation epoch ($z \sim 3$--$4$)
    and at the earliest epochs where the UV intensity rises steeply.

  \item \textbf{Temperature variable $T_2 = T/\mu$.}
    The solver works with $T_2$ rather than $T$.  Since $\mu$ changes
    across the ionisation transition ($\mu \approx 1.22$ for neutral gas,
    $\mu \approx 0.59$ for fully ionised), a small change in $T_2$ maps
    to a factor-of-two change in physical temperature $T$ across the
    transition.  This amplifies interpolation errors near
    $T \sim 10^4\,{\rm K}$.
\end{enumerate}


\subsection{Comparison on the Density--Temperature Plane}
\label{sec:cool_compare}

To quantify the systematic differences, we compare the \ramses\ net
cooling rate against the Grackle equilibrium cooling library
\citep{Smith2017}, which uses the Haardt \& Madau (2012) UV background
\citep{HM2012} and includes density-dependent metal cooling with full
non-LTE ionisation corrections.  We compute
$\Lambda_{\rm net}(n_{\rm H}, T)$ on a grid of $60 \times 256$ points
spanning $n_{\rm H} = 10^{-8}$--$10^{4}\,{\rm cm}^{-3}$ and
$T = 10$--$10^{9}\,{\rm K}$ at $z = 5.1$ (the redshift of the HR5 test
restart).

\paragraph{Low metallicity ($Z = 10^{-3}\,Z_\odot$).}
Figure~\ref{fig:cool_diff_lowZ} compares the two models at the
simulation average metallicity ($\code{z\_ave} = 10^{-3}$).  At this
low metallicity, the net rate is dominated by H/He processes and UV
photo-heating.

The lower-left panel shows the log ratio of the cooling rates,
$\log_{10}(|\Lambda_{\rm RAMSES}|/|\Lambda_{\rm Grackle}|)$.
Contour lines mark the thermal equilibrium ($\Lambda_{\rm net} = 0$)
for each model: the yellow solid line for RAMSES and the green dashed
line for Grackle.  Three features are apparent:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item The \emph{equilibrium temperature} differs by up to
    $\Delta\log T \sim 0.3$--$0.5$ at low densities
    ($n_{\rm H} \lesssim 10^{-2}\,{\rm cm}^{-3}$), reflecting the
    different UV backgrounds.  Since $\lJ \propto T^{1/2}$, this
    translates to a $\sim 40$--$80\%$ difference in the Jeans length
    at the same density.
  \item At intermediate temperatures ($10^4$--$10^5\,{\rm K}$),
    the RAMSES rate is systematically \emph{stronger} (redder in the
    ratio panel) by up to an order of magnitude.  This arises because
    the CIE assumption overpredicts the neutral hydrogen fraction at
    these temperatures for low-density gas where the UV background
    maintains significant photo-ionisation.
  \item At $T > 10^7\,{\rm K}$, both models converge to the
    bremsstrahlung limit $\Lambda \propto T^{1/2}\,n_{\rm H}^2$ and
    the ratio approaches unity, as expected.
\end{enumerate}

The lower-right panel shows the mean molecular weight difference
$\Delta\mu = \mu_{\rm RAMSES} - \mu_{\rm Grackle}$.  The RAMSES CIE
$\mu$ is systematically higher than the Grackle non-LTE value at
$T > 10^5\,{\rm K}$.  At intermediate temperatures
($T \sim 10^4$--$10^5\,{\rm K}$), the Grackle $\mu$ shows a strong
\emph{density dependence} due to the UV background maintaining partial
ionisation at low densities --- a feature entirely absent from the
RAMSES model, where $\mu(T_2)$ is independent of density.  This
density-dependent $\mu$ has direct implications for the $T_2 \to T$
conversion and hence for the Jeans length evaluation.

Figure~\ref{fig:cool_diff_Zsol} shows the same comparison at near-solar

\begin{itemize}
  \item The Grackle table includes density-dependent ionisation
    corrections for metal species, leading to \emph{up to
    $\sim 10\times$ stronger cooling} in the

\subsection{The Townsend Exact Integration Scheme}
\label{sec:townsend}

To address the overshooting problem of the N-R solver
(Section~\ref{sec:overshoot}), we implement the exact integration
method of \citet{Townsend2009}, which transforms the cooling ODE into
an analytically invertible form using the $Y$-function.

\subsubsection{Y-Function Formulation}

For a piecewise power-law cooling function
$\Lambda(T) \propto T^{\alpha_j}$ in each temperature bin
$[T_j, T_{j+1}]$, the integrated cooling variable is
\begin{equation}
  Y(T) = \int_{T_{\rm ref}}^{T} \frac{dT'}{T'\,\hat{\Lambda}(T')}
  = \sum_{k} \Delta Y_k,
  \label{eq:Y_func}
\end{equation}
where $\hat{\Lambda} = \Lambda/\Lambda_{\rm ref}$ is the normalised
cooling rate.  Each segment contributes
\begin{equation}
  \Delta Y_j = \begin{cases}
    \dfrac{T_j^{-\alpha_j} - T_{j+1}^{-\alpha_j}}
          {\alpha_j\,\hat{\Lambda}_j\,T_j^{-\alpha_j}}
    & (\alpha_j \ne 0), \\[6pt]
    \dfrac{\ln(T_j/T_{j+1})}{\hat{\Lambda}_j}
    & (\alpha_j = 0).
  \end{cases}
  \label{eq:deltaY}
\end{equation}

The exact temperature after a time-step $\Delta t$ is obtained by
inverting the relation
\begin{equation}
  Y(T^{n+1}) = Y(T^n) + \Lambda_{\rm ref}\,\frac{n_{\rm H}}{E_0}\,\Delta t,
  \label{eq:Y_advance}
\end{equation}
where $E_0 = (3/2)\,n_{\rm tot}\,k_{\rm B}$ is the thermal energy per
unit temperature.  The inversion is performed by binary search on the
cumulative $Y$-table and local analytic inversion within the identified
segment.

\subsubsection{Advantages Over Newton--Raphson}

The Y-function scheme has several fundamental advantages:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{No overshooting.}  The method integrates the cooling
    equation \emph{exactly} for piecewise power-law rates.  The
    temperature evolves monotonically toward the equilibrium; it cannot
    oscillate across $T_{\rm eq}$.
  \item \textbf{No sub-stepping.}  A single evaluation of
    Equation~\eqref{eq:Y_advance} advances the solution by the full
    hydrodynamic time-step, regardless of $t_{\rm cool}/\Delta t$.  The
    N-R solver requires up to 500 sub-steps when $\Delta t \gg t_{\rm cool}$.
  \item \textbf{Correct equilibrium.}  When $\Delta t \to \infty$,
    $Y \to Y_{\rm eq}$ and the cell relaxes exactly to $T_{\rm eq}$.
    The N-R solver only reaches $T_{\rm eq}$ asymptotically through
    iteration, and may settle on the wrong side if the sub-step
    structure interacts with the sign change.
  \item \textbf{Deterministic.}  The result depends only on
    $(T^n, n_{\rm H}, Z, \Delta t)$ and the cooling table, with no
    sensitivity to iteration count, convergence tolerance, or adaptive
    step heuristics.
\end{enumerate}

The cost is a table lookup and a $\log/\exp$ evaluation per cell
($\sim$few $\mu$s), comparable to a single N-R sub-step.  Since the N-R
solver typically requires 5--50 sub-steps, the exact scheme is faster
for strongly cooling gas.

\subsubsection{Implementation: Eunha Cooling Module}

The exact integrator is implemented as a Fortran module
(\code{eunha\_cooling\_mod.f90}) that reads a 3D Grackle equilibrium
cooling table $\Lambda_{\rm net}(n_{\rm H}, Z, T)$ and pre-computes
the cumulative $Y$-table.  The module provides three runtime modes
selected by the namelist parameter \code{cooling\_method}:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \code{'original'} --- standard \ramses\ N-R solver (default,
    no overhead);
  \item \code{'exact'} --- Townsend exact integration replaces the N-R
    solver;
  \item \code{'compare'} --- both solvers run; the N-R result is used
    for physics while per-step diagnostics report the difference
    $\Delta T_2^{\rm orig} - \Delta T_2^{\rm exact}$.
\end{itemize}

The Grackle table adds a third axis (metallicity $Z$) with 40 bins
spanning $10^{-6}$--$10\,Z_\odot$, compared to the 2D RAMSES table
with linear $Z$ scaling.  The mean molecular weight $\mu(n_{\rm H}, Z, T)$
is also tabulated from Grackle's non-LTE equilibrium, replacing the
RAMSES CIE calculation.  The $T_2 \leftrightarrow T$ conversion uses an
iterative scheme: $\mu \to T = T_2\,\mu \to \mu'(n_{\rm H}, Z, T) \to
T' = T_2\,\mu'$, converging to $<0.1\%$ in 3 iterations.


\subsection{Implications for the Jeans Criterion}
\label{sec:cool_jeans}

The combined effect of the cooling rate differences
(Figures~\ref{fig:cool_diff_lowZ}--\ref{fig:cool_diff_Zsol}) and the
integration scheme impacts the Jeans refinement criterion through
three channels:

\paragraph{1. Equilibrium temperature.}
The Jeans length at thermal equilibrium is
$\lJ^{\rm eq} \propto (T_{\rm eq}/\rho)^{1/2}$.  Since
$T_{\rm eq}^{\rm RAMSES} \ne T_{\rm eq}^{\rm Grackle}$ (bottom-left
panels), the two solvers will flag different cells.  At low densities,
the RAMSES equilibrium is systematically \emph{higher}
($T_{\rm eq} \sim 2$--$3\times$ warmer), yielding a larger Jeans length
and less refinement.  This means the standard solver may
\emph{underestimate} the refinement needed in filaments and the
circumgalactic medium.

\paragraph{2. Cooling overshoot near $T_{\rm eq}$.}
The N-R solver's tendency to overshoot $T_{\rm eq}$
(Section~\ref{sec:overshoot}) creates transient temperature errors that
affect the \emph{instantaneous} Jeans length even if the time-averaged
temperature is correct.  A cell that overshoots to $T < T_{\rm eq}$
will have a transiently small $\lJ$, triggering refinement that may
not be needed.  Conversely, a cell that oscillates above $T_{\rm eq}$
will have a transiently large $\lJ$, suppressing refinement that
\emph{is} needed.  The exact integrator eliminates this noise entirely.

\paragraph{3. Mean molecular weight.}
The $T_2 = T/\mu$ representation amplifies errors in $\mu$.  With the
RAMSES CIE model, $\mu$ is a function of $T_2$ alone (density-independent).
The Grackle non-LTE $\mu$ depends on both $n_{\rm H}$ and $T$, with
$|\Delta\mu| \sim 0.3$--$0.6$ in the transition zone
($T \sim 10^4$--$10^5\,{\rm K}$; bottom-right panels).  Since $T =
T_2\,\mu$, a 50\% error in $\mu$ produces a 50\% error in $T$ and
hence a $\sim 22\%$ error in $\lJ$, comparable to the factor-of-two
ratio that separates adjacent refinement levels.

\section{Discussion}

\subsection{Physical Motivation}

The holdback mechanism was designed to prevent over-refinement from the
mass criterion at early times.  However, it inadvertently blocks the
Jeans criterion, which has a different physical motivation: resolving
the Jeans length prevents artificial fragmentation and ensures
numerically converged star formation.

By allowing the Jeans criterion to operate ahead of the holdback
release, we expect:
\begin{itemize}
  \item Dense gas that will form stars is pre-refined before the
    holdback releases.
  \item When the holdback releases, the transition is smoother because
    the densest regions are already at the appropriate resolution.
  \item The SFR curve shows a gradual onset rather than an abrupt step.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Interplay of the Three Parameters}

The bypass is controlled by three parameters that form a layered
defence:

\paragraph{Layer 1: \code{jeans\_bypass\_holdback} (on/off)}
Master switch.  When off (default), the system is identical to
standard \ramses.

\paragraph{Layer 2: \code{d\_jeans\_thre} (physics-based regulation)}
Restricts the Jeans check to dense gas where the polytropic floor
operates.  In this regime, $\lJ \propto \rho^{-1/6}$ varies slowly,
and 1--2 extra refinement levels suffice to resolve it.  Provides
self-consistent, physics-driven cascade termination.

\paragraph{Layer 3: \code{n\_jeans\_bypass\_levels} (hard safety limit)}
Absolute cap on the number of extra levels.  Prevents OOM regardless
of physics configuration.  Essential when
\code{d\_jeans\_thre=0} (all cells checked).
