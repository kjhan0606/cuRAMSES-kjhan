\begin{abstract}
The Jeans length criterion is a fundamental numerical stability
requirement in self-gravitating hydrodynamic simulations: failing to
resolve it by at least four cell widths produces artificial
fragmentation that contaminates the star formation history.  In
cosmological adaptive mesh refinement (AMR) simulations, however,
three distinct mechanisms conspire to violate or misapply this
criterion.  First, the \emph{cosmological holdback} that prevents
premature refinement at high redshift blocks the Jeans criterion
along with all other refinement triggers, creating abrupt starbursts
when levels are released.  Second, the standard Newton--Raphson
cooling solver can overshoot the thermal equilibrium temperature
$T_{\rm eq}$, introducing transient errors in the Jeans length
evaluation.  Third, pre-computed cooling tables at a single redshift
become stale as the UV background evolves.  We address all three
issues in the \curamses\ code: (i)~a \emph{Jeans-independent
holdback bypass} that allows the Jeans criterion to operate ahead of
the holdback, with a configurable level limit to prevent runaway
cascades; (ii)~an \emph{exact cooling integrator} based on the
Townsend (2009) $Y$-function method applied to three-dimensional
Grackle equilibrium cooling tables, eliminating Newton--Raphson
overshooting; and (iii)~\emph{multi-redshift table interpolation}
that tracks the evolving UV background across cosmic time.  We
quantify how cooling rate differences between the standard
\ramses\ solver and the Grackle library propagate into
40--80\,per\,cent errors in the Jeans length at low densities,
and demonstrate that the exact integrator removes the stochastic
refinement noise caused by temperature overshooting.
We further present a \emph{sub-grid scale turbulence model} that
evolves the unresolved turbulent kinetic energy self-consistently
within the Riemann solver, providing turbulent pressure support that
naturally limits the refinement depth without requiring the holdback.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
%======================================================================

Gravitational instability is the fundamental driver of cosmic
structure formation.  \citet{Jeans1902} showed that a uniform,
self-gravitating medium is unstable to perturbations on scales
exceeding the Jeans length
\begin{equation}
  \lJ = \sqrt{\frac{\pi\,c_s^2}{G\,\rho}},
  \label{eq:jeans_intro}
\end{equation}
where $c_s$ is the sound speed and $\rho$ the gas density.  All
scales below $\lJ$ are stabilised by thermal pressure; all scales
above it collapse.  In astrophysical simulations of star-forming gas,
the Jeans length therefore defines a \emph{minimum resolution
requirement}: if the computational grid or particle spacing cannot
resolve $\lJ$, the simulation produces qualitatively wrong results
--- artificial fragmentation that bears no relation to the true
physical outcome \citep{Truelove1997, Bate1997}.

Cosmological simulations of galaxy formation must follow
gravitational collapse across many orders of magnitude in spatial
scale, from $\sim 100\,$Mpc cosmic web filaments down to
$\sim 10\,$pc star-forming clouds.  Adaptive mesh refinement
\citep[AMR;][]{Berger1984, Berger1989} achieves this by
hierarchically subdividing cells in regions that require higher
resolution, concentrating computational resources where they are
needed.  Major AMR codes used in cosmological contexts include
\ramses\ \citep{Teyssier2002}, Enzo \citep{Bryan2014}, and ART
\citep{Kravtsov1997}.  Alternative numerical techniques --- smoothed
particle hydrodynamics \citep[SPH;][]{Springel2005}, moving-mesh
methods \citep{Springel2010}, and mesh-free finite-mass schemes
\citep{Hopkins2015} --- face analogous resolution requirements
\citep{Bate1997, Nelson2009}, but the AMR framework provides a
particularly transparent mechanism for enforcing the Jeans condition:
a cell is flagged for refinement whenever $\lJ < N_J\,\dx$, where
$N_J$ is typically 4--8 and $\dx$ is the cell width
\citep{Truelove1997}.

In practice, however, enforcing the Jeans criterion in cosmological
AMR simulations is complicated by three interacting effects:

\textit{(i) Cosmological holdback.}  To prevent premature
over-refinement at high redshift --- which would exhaust memory
before the epoch of interest --- codes such as \ramses\ employ a
\emph{holdback} mechanism that progressively releases refinement
levels as the scale factor $a$ grows.  This holdback blocks
\emph{all} refinement criteria simultaneously, including the Jeans
criterion, which is a numerical \emph{stability} requirement rather
than merely a resolution preference.  When a level is finally
released, a burst of refinement and star formation follows
\citep[see also][]{Dubois2014, Dubois2021}.

\textit{(ii) Cooling accuracy.}  The Jeans length is proportional to
$T^{1/2}$, so the equilibrium temperature $T_{\rm eq}$ of the gas
directly determines whether refinement is triggered.  $T_{\rm eq}$ is
set by the balance between radiative cooling and photo-heating by the
ultraviolet (UV) background \citep{HM2012, FG2020}.  Errors in the
cooling rate tables \citep{Sutherland1993, Ferland2017}, the UV
background model \citep{Courty2004}, or the numerical integration
scheme all propagate into errors in $T_{\rm eq}$ and hence in $\lJ$.
The standard \ramses\ cooling solver uses a semi-implicit
Newton--Raphson (N-R) iteration that is known to overshoot $T_{\rm eq}$
when the net cooling rate changes sign
\citep[cf.][]{Townsend2009}, introducing stochastic noise into the
Jeans length evaluation.  The Grackle equilibrium chemistry library
\citep{Smith2017} provides more accurate cooling rates --- including
density-dependent metal cooling and non-local thermodynamic
equilibrium (non-LTE) molecular weights --- but the standard tabulated
approach still requires a robust numerical integrator to avoid
overshooting.

\textit{(iii) Redshift evolution.}  The metagalactic UV background
evolves strongly with redshift: it rises during cosmic reionisation
($z \gtrsim 6$), peaks near $z \sim 2$--$3$, and declines towards
$z = 0$ \citep{HM2012, FG2020}.  A cooling table computed at a single
redshift becomes increasingly inaccurate as the simulation evolves
away from that epoch, producing systematic biases in $T_{\rm eq}$ and
$\lJ$ that can reach factors of 2--3 during the He~{\sc ii}
reionisation epoch ($z \sim 3$--$4$).

Beyond thermal pressure, \emph{turbulence} provides an additional
source of pressure support that can significantly modify the effective
Jeans length.  \citet{Chandrasekhar1951} and \citet{Bonazzola1987}
showed that turbulent motions increase the effective sound speed from
$c_s$ to $(c_s^2 + \sigma_{\rm turb}^2)^{1/2}$, where $\sigma_{\rm
turb}$ is the turbulent velocity dispersion, yielding a turbulent
Jeans length
\begin{equation}
  \lJ^{\rm turb} =
  \sqrt{\frac{\pi\,(c_s^2 + \sigma_{\rm turb}^2)}{G\,\rho}}.
  \label{eq:jeans_turb}
\end{equation}
In the interstellar medium (ISM), turbulence is driven by supernova
feedback, gravitational infall, magnetorotational instability, and
galactic shear \citep{Robertson2008, Kim2017, Hopkins2018}.
Subgrid-scale (SGS) turbulence models
\citep{Schmidt2006, Schmidt2011, Rennehan2021} can estimate the
unresolved turbulent energy on the grid scale, providing an in-situ
value of $\sigma_{\rm turb}$ that can be fed into the refinement
criterion.  In the context of star formation,
\citet{Federrath2012} showed that turbulence
simultaneously \emph{supports} gas on large scales (raising the
effective Jeans mass) and \emph{compresses} gas locally (triggering
collapse in overdense regions), so the net effect on refinement is
scale-dependent.  \citet{Semenov2016} demonstrated that a
turbulence-dependent star formation model produces more realistic
star formation histories than density-threshold prescriptions.
Incorporating turbulent pressure support into the AMR Jeans criterion
--- using either resolved velocity gradients or SGS models --- is a
natural extension that we plan to address in future work.

Large-volume cosmological simulations such as Horizon-AGN
\citep{Dubois2014}, EAGLE \citep{Schaye2015}, IllustrisTNG
\citep{Pillepich2018}, NewHorizon \citep{Dubois2021}, and Horizon
Run~5 \citep[HR5;][]{Lee2021} rely on accurate Jeans length
resolution to produce converged star formation rates and galaxy
properties.  Any systematic error in the Jeans criterion ---
whether from the holdback, cooling inaccuracies, or the neglect of
turbulent pressure --- propagates into the predicted stellar masses,
morphologies, and feedback histories.

In this paper we present three improvements to the handling of the
Jeans criterion in cosmological AMR simulations, implemented in
\curamses:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item An \emph{improved cooling module} that combines 3D Grackle
    equilibrium cooling tables with the \citet{Townsend2009} exact
    integration scheme and multi-redshift interpolation, eliminating
    the N-R overshooting that contaminates the Jeans length near
    thermal equilibrium (Section~\ref{sec:exact_cooling}).
  \item A \emph{Jeans-independent holdback bypass} that allows the
    Jeans criterion to operate ahead of the cosmological holdback,
    with a configurable level limit to prevent uncontrolled refinement
    cascades (Section~\ref{sec:bypass}).
  \item Analysis of the \emph{polytropic temperature floor} and its
    role in self-regulating the bypass cascade through physics-based
    density thresholds (Section~\ref{sec:polytropic}).
\end{enumerate}
We also outline future directions for incorporating turbulent
pressure support into the Jeans criterion
(Section~\ref{sec:turbulence}).

The paper is organised as follows.
Section~\ref{sec:amr_refine} reviews AMR refinement in \ramses,
including the holdback mechanism.
Section~\ref{sec:exact_cooling} describes the exact cooling integrator
and multi-redshift tables.
Section~\ref{sec:cooling} analyses the cooling rates and their impact
on the Jeans length.
Section~\ref{sec:starburst} identifies the starburst problem caused
by the holdback.
Section~\ref{sec:bypass} presents the Jeans bypass solution.
Section~\ref{sec:polytropic} discusses the polytropic floor.
Section~\ref{sec:implementation} gives implementation details.
Section~\ref{sec:tests} shows test results.
Section~\ref{sec:discussion} discusses implications, and
Section~\ref{sec:conclusions} summarises our findings.

\section{AMR Refinement in \ramses}
\label{sec:amr_refine}
%======================================================================

\ramses\ \citep{Teyssier2002} is a grid-based AMR code that solves
the coupled Euler--Poisson--Boltzmann equations on a fully threaded
octree.  Each cell at level $\ell < \nlevelmax$ is tested for
refinement at every coarse time-step.  The decision to subdivide a
cell into $\twotondim$ children is governed by a combination of
physical, geometric, and cosmological criteria.

\subsection{Refinement Logic}
\label{sec:refine_logic}

The overall refinement flag for a cell is determined by the following
Boolean expression:
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{R} = \lnot\,\mathcal{H}
  \;\land\; \mathcal{G}
  \;\land\; \bigl(
    \mathcal{M} \lor \mathcal{S} \lor \mathcal{D} \lor \mathcal{P}
    \lor \mathcal{U} \lor \mathcal{J}
  \bigr),
\label{eq:refine_flag}
\end{equation}
where each symbol is defined below.

\subsubsection{Physical Criteria (OR)}

\begin{enumerate}

\item \textbf{Quasi-Lagrangian mass criterion ($\mathcal{M}$).}
  The cell is flagged if its gas density exceeds a
  level-dependent threshold:
  \begin{equation}
    \rho_{\rm cell} \;\ge\; m_{\rm refine}(\ell)
      \;\frac{m_{\rm sph}}{V_{\rm cell}},
  \end{equation}
  where $m_{\rm sph}$ is the effective SPH kernel mass and
  $V_{\rm cell} = (\dx_\ell)^3$.
  This criterion ensures that regions containing roughly
  $m_{\rm refine}$ or more particles per cell are refined,
  providing a quasi-Lagrangian resolution that tracks gravitational
  collapse.  Controlled by the namelist array
  \code{m\_refine(1:nlevelmax)}.

\item \textbf{Sink particle forcing ($\mathcal{S}$).}
  If \code{sink\_refine=.true.}, any cell containing a sink cloud
  particle (identified by \code{idp}$\,< 0$ and \code{tp}$\,= 0$) is
  unconditionally flagged up to $\nlevelmax$, guaranteeing maximum
  resolution around each accretion region.

\item \textbf{Density gradient ($\mathcal{D}$).}
  The normalised density gradient between a cell and each of its
  $2N_{\rm dim}$ face-neighbours is computed as
  \begin{equation}
    \epsilon_\rho = 2\,\max\!\left(
      \frac{|\rho_+ - \rho_0|}{|\rho_+ + \rho_0| + \rho_{\rm floor}},\;
      \frac{|\rho_0 - \rho_-|}{|\rho_0 + \rho_-| + \rho_{\rm floor}}
    \right).
  \end{equation}
  If $\epsilon_\rho > \code{err\_grad\_d}$ along any axis, the cell
  is flagged.

\item \textbf{Pressure gradient ($\mathcal{P}$).}
  Analogous to the density gradient but applied to the thermal
  pressure:
  \begin{equation}
    \epsilon_P = 2\,\max\!\left(
      \frac{|P_+ - P_0|}{|P_+ + P_0| + P_{\rm floor}},\;
      \frac{|P_0 - P_-|}{|P_0 + P_-| + P_{\rm floor}}
    \right),
  \end{equation}
  flagging when $\epsilon_P > \code{err\_grad\_p}$.

\item \textbf{Velocity gradient ($\mathcal{U}$).}
  A normalised velocity jump relative to the local sound speed:
  \begin{equation}
    \epsilon_v = 2\,\max\!\left(
      \frac{|v_+ - v_0|}{c_{s,+} + c_{s,0} + |v_+| + |v_0|
                          + v_{\rm floor}},\;
      \frac{|v_0 - v_-|}{c_{s,0} + c_{s,-} + |v_0| + |v_-|
                          + v_{\rm floor}}
    \right),
  \end{equation}
  flagging when $\epsilon_v > \code{err\_grad\_u}$ in any component.

\item \textbf{Jeans length ($\mathcal{J}$).}
  When \code{jeans\_refine($\ell$)} $= N_J > 0$, the cell is flagged if
  the local Jeans length is shorter than $N_J$ cell widths:
  \begin{equation}
    \lJ = \sqrt{\frac{\pi\,c_s^2}{G\,\rho}}
    < N_J\,\dx_\ell,
    \label{eq:jeans_crit}
  \end{equation}
  implementing the \citet{Truelove1997} condition to prevent artificial
  fragmentation in self-gravitating gas.  An additional density
  threshold \code{d\_jeans\_thre} restricts this criterion to cells
  with $n_{\rm H} \ge \code{d\_jeans\_thre}$.

\end{enumerate}

\subsubsection{Geometry Mask (AND)}

\textbf{Ellipsoidal region ($\mathcal{G}$).}
When \code{r\_refine($\ell$)} $> -1$, only cells inside a generalised
ellipsoidal region are permitted to refine.  The test is
\begin{equation}
  \left(
    \frac{2|\Delta x|}{r}
  \right)^{e_r}
  + \left(
    \frac{2|\Delta y|}{a\,r}
  \right)^{e_r}
  + \left(
    \frac{2|\Delta z|}{b\,r}
  \right)^{e_r}
  < 1,
\end{equation}
where $r = \code{r\_refine}$, $a = \code{a\_refine}$,
$b = \code{b\_refine}$, and $e_r = \code{exp\_refine}$.
Cells outside this region are \emph{un-flagged} regardless of
physical criteria, restricting high resolution to a zoom-in target.

\subsubsection{Cosmological Holdback (NOT)}

\textbf{Resolution holdback ($\mathcal{H}$).}
In cosmological runs with cooling, overly eager refinement at high
redshift can exhaust memory before the epoch of interest.
When \code{q\_refine\_holdback=.true.} and the physical cell size
$\dx_\ell < 4^{1/N_{\rm dim}}\,\dx_{\nlevelmax}/a_{\rm exp}$
for levels $\ell > \code{nlevelmax\_part} + 3$, the \emph{entire}
level is blocked from further refinement:
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{H}(\ell) = \bigl[\ell > \ell_{\rm part} + \ell_{\rm collapse}\bigr]
  \;\wedge\;
  \bigl[\dx_\ell < 4^{1/N_{\rm dim}}\,\dx_{\rm min}/a\bigr],
  \label{eq:holdback}
\end{equation}
where $\ell_{\rm part}$ is the particle initial-condition level
(typically $\ell_{\rm min}$) and $\ell_{\rm collapse}=3$.
The first holdback level $\ell_{\rm hb}$ is the smallest $\ell$
satisfying equation~(\ref{eq:holdback}):
\begin{equation}
  \ell_{\rm hb} = \min\bigl\{\ell > \ell_{\rm part}+\ell_{\rm collapse}
  \;:\; \dx_\ell < 4^{1/N_{\rm dim}}\,\dx_{\rm min}/a\bigr\}.
  \label{eq:lhb}
\end{equation}

\subsection{Buffer Zones}

After applying equation~(\ref{eq:refine_flag}), flagged cells are
expanded by \code{nexpand($\ell$)} cell widths in each direction via a
dilation operator (\code{smooth\_fine}).
This cubic buffer ensures smooth refinement boundaries, preventing
the Godunov solver from encountering abrupt level transitions.

\subsection{Sub-Grid Scale Turbulence Model}
\label{sec:sgs}
%----------------------------------------------------------------------

The purely thermal Jeans length
(equation~\ref{eq:jeans_crit}) ignores the additional pressure
support provided by unresolved turbulent motions.  In cosmological
simulations without a polytropic equation of state ($\gstar < 2$),
the Jeans length $\lJ \propto \rho^{(\gstar - 2)/2}$ shrinks as
density increases, triggering runaway refinement cascades that
exhaust memory and inflate the number of fine time-steps by up to
$7\times$.  The \emph{cosmological holdback}
(Section~\ref{sec:holdback}) was introduced as a pragmatic
workaround, but it blocks all refinement criteria indiscriminately and
introduces artificial starbursts when levels are released.

We address this root cause by implementing a \emph{sub-grid scale}
(SGS) turbulence model that evolves the unresolved turbulent kinetic
energy alongside the hydrodynamic variables, following the
compressible SGS framework of
\citet{Schmidt2006,Schmidt2011,Scannapieco2008}.  The turbulent
pressure enters both the Riemann solver and the Jeans criterion,
providing self-consistent pressure support that naturally limits
refinement depth without the holdback.


\subsubsection{Governing Equations}
\label{sec:sgs_eqs}

We introduce a new conserved variable $\rho e_{\rm sgs}$, the SGS
turbulent kinetic energy density, advected alongside the standard
hydrodynamic variables $(\rho, \rho\mathbf{v}, E)$.  The evolution
equation is
\begin{equation}
  \frac{\partial (\rho e_{\rm sgs})}{\partial t}
  + \nabla\cdot(\rho e_{\rm sgs}\,\mathbf{v})
  = -P_{\rm turb}\,\nabla\cdot\mathbf{v}
    + \mathcal{P}_{\rm grav}
    - \varepsilon_{\rm diss},
  \label{eq:sgs_evolution}
\end{equation}
where the three source terms on the right-hand side represent,
respectively, the pressure--dilatation coupling, gravitational
production, and dissipation at the grid scale.

The \emph{turbulent pressure} is defined as
\begin{equation}
  P_{\rm turb} = \tfrac{2}{3}\,\rho\,e_{\rm sgs},
  \label{eq:pturb}
\end{equation}
which acts as an isotropic closure of the Reynolds stress tensor
$\tau_{ij}^{\rm sgs} \approx \frac{2}{3}\rho\,e_{\rm sgs}\,\delta_{ij}$
\citep{Schmidt2006}.  The total effective pressure entering the
momentum equation is $P_{\rm eff} = P_{\rm therm} + P_{\rm turb}$.

The \emph{SGS turbulent velocity dispersion} is related to the
specific SGS energy by
\begin{equation}
  \sigma_{\rm turb} = \sqrt{\tfrac{2}{3}\,e_{\rm sgs}},
  \label{eq:sigma_turb}
\end{equation}
so that $P_{\rm turb} = \rho\,\sigma_{\rm turb}^2$.

\paragraph{Pressure--dilatation (PdV) coupling.}
The first source term in equation~(\ref{eq:sgs_evolution}),
$-P_{\rm turb}\,\nabla\cdot\mathbf{v}$, represents the work done by
turbulent pressure during compression ($\nabla\cdot\mathbf{v} < 0$)
or expansion ($\nabla\cdot\mathbf{v} > 0$).  In compression,
$e_{\rm sgs}$ increases at the expense of the resolved kinetic energy,
while in expansion it decreases.  This term conserves total energy
exactly: the corresponding work is removed from the thermal/kinetic
budget by the $P_{\rm turb}$ contribution to the Riemann solver
fluxes (Section~\ref{sec:sgs_riemann}).

\paragraph{Gravitational production.}
In self-gravitating systems, gravitational contraction stirs turbulent
motions at the grid scale.  We model this as
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{P}_{\rm grav}
    = C_{\rm prod}\,\rho\,e_{\rm sgs}\,\sqrt{f_G\,\rho},
  \label{eq:sgs_prod}
\end{equation}
where $C_{\rm prod}$ is a dimensionless production coefficient and
$f_G$ is the gravitational coupling factor:
\begin{equation}
  f_G =
  \begin{cases}
    \dfrac{3\,\Omega_m}{8\pi\,a} & \text{(cosmological),} \\[6pt]
    1 & \text{(non-cosmological),}
  \end{cases}
  \label{eq:factG}
\end{equation}
identical to the factor appearing in the Jeans length evaluation.  The
production rate $\sqrt{f_G\rho}$ has dimensions of inverse time and
is proportional to the local free-fall rate $\sim (G\rho)^{1/2}$,
ensuring that turbulent energy is injected most vigorously in
collapsing regions where the Jeans criterion is most critical.

\paragraph{Dissipation.}
Turbulent energy cascades from the grid scale $\dx$ to smaller scales
where it is dissipated into heat.  Following
\citet{Schmidt2006,Semenov2016}, the dissipation rate is
\begin{equation}
  \varepsilon_{\rm diss}
    = C_{\rm diss}\,\rho\,
      \frac{\sigma_{\rm turb}^3}{\dx}
    = C_{\rm diss}\,\rho\,
      \frac{(2e_{\rm sgs}/3)^{3/2}}{\dx},
  \label{eq:sgs_diss}
\end{equation}
where $C_{\rm diss}$ is a dimensionless coefficient calibrated to
reproduce the Kolmogorov energy transfer rate.  The $\sigma^3/\dx$
scaling follows directly from dimensional analysis of the turbulent
cascade: the energy transfer rate per unit mass is
$\sim\sigma^3/\ell$ at the driving scale $\ell$.

Since $\varepsilon_{\rm diss} \propto e_{\rm sgs}^{3/2}$, this term
is highly non-linear and can be stiff when $e_{\rm sgs}$ is large.
The dissipation time-scale
$t_{\rm diss} = e_{\rm sgs}/\varepsilon_{\rm diss}
  \propto \dx/\sigma_{\rm turb}$
can be much shorter than the hydrodynamic time-step, demanding an
implicit treatment (Section~\ref{sec:sgs_implicit}).

Crucially, the dissipated SGS energy is not lost: it is deposited
as thermal energy, maintaining total energy conservation:
\begin{equation}
  \left.\frac{\partial E_{\rm therm}}{\partial t}\right|_{\rm sgs}
  = +\varepsilon_{\rm diss}.
  \label{eq:sgs_heating}
\end{equation}


\subsubsection{Equilibrium Analysis and Self-Regulation}
\label{sec:sgs_equilib}

In a quasi-static gravitationally collapsing region, the production
and dissipation terms in equation~(\ref{eq:sgs_evolution}) reach a
local balance.  Setting $\mathcal{P}_{\rm grav} = \varepsilon_{\rm diss}$
and solving for the equilibrium SGS velocity dispersion yields
\begin{equation}
  \sigma_{\rm eq}^2
  = \left(\frac{C_{\rm prod}}{C_{\rm diss}}\right)^2
    f_G\,\rho\,\dx^2.
  \label{eq:sigma_eq}
\end{equation}
The corresponding equilibrium turbulent Jeans length is
\begin{align}
  \lJ^{\rm eff}
  &= \sqrt{\frac{\pi\,(c_s^2 + \sigma_{\rm eq}^2)}{G\,\rho}}
  \nonumber\\
  &\ge \sqrt{\frac{\pi\,\sigma_{\rm eq}^2}{G\,\rho}}
  = \frac{C_{\rm prod}}{C_{\rm diss}}
    \sqrt{\frac{\pi f_G}{G}}\;\dx.
  \label{eq:jeans_eff}
\end{align}
In the cosmological case where $f_G = 3\Omega_m/(8\pi a)$ and
$G = 3\Omega_m H_0^2/(8\pi)$ in code units, the ratio
$f_G/G = 1/(aH_0^2)$ is independent of density.  Therefore
the number of cells per effective Jeans length at equilibrium is
\begin{equation}
  N_{J,\rm eff}
  = \frac{\lJ^{\rm eff}}{\dx}
  \ge \frac{C_{\rm prod}}{C_{\rm diss}}
      \sqrt{\frac{\pi f_G}{G}}.
  \label{eq:NJ_eff}
\end{equation}
For our fiducial parameters $C_{\rm prod} = 1.0$, $C_{\rm diss} = 0.5$,
this gives $N_{J,\rm eff} \approx 3.5$, close to the
\citet{Truelove1997} minimum of~4.  Importantly, since
$\sigma_{\rm eq}^2 \propto \dx^2$, the turbulent pressure support
automatically adjusts to the local grid scale: finer cells have less
turbulent support, so they are refined only when thermally warranted.
This is in sharp contrast to a uniform polytropic floor
($\gstar = 2$), which raises the sound speed uniformly across all
scales and produces catastrophic CFL time-step reductions (a factor
$\sim$300 at high densities).

The self-regulation mechanism works as follows: if a cell is refined,
its child cells have half the grid spacing, so
$\sigma_{\rm eq}^2 \propto \dx^2$ drops by a factor of~4.  The
effective Jeans length decreases, and the Jeans criterion becomes
harder to satisfy --- \emph{unless the thermal Jeans length is
genuinely unresolved}.  The cascade therefore terminates when the
thermal Jeans length is resolved by $\sim N_{J,\rm eff}$ cells,
precisely the behaviour desired.


\subsubsection{Riemann Solver Modifications}
\label{sec:sgs_riemann}

The turbulent pressure $P_{\rm turb}$ must enter the Riemann solver
to maintain consistency between the SGS source terms and the
resolved-scale fluxes.  We modify all five Riemann solvers available
in \ramses\ (HLLC, HLL, Lax--Friedrichs, acoustic, and
Roe-approximate).

\paragraph{Effective pressure and wave speeds.}
In each solver, the total pressure driving the momentum flux is
replaced by
\begin{equation}
  P_{\rm tot} = P_{\rm therm} + P_{\rm turb}
  = P_{\rm therm} + \tfrac{2}{3}\,\rho\,e_{\rm sgs},
  \label{eq:ptot}
\end{equation}
and the characteristic wave speed is computed from the effective
sound speed:
\begin{equation}
  c_{\rm eff}^2 = \frac{\gamma\,P_{\rm therm} + P_{\rm turb}}{\rho}
  = \frac{\gamma\,P_{\rm therm}}{\rho} + \sigma_{\rm turb}^2.
  \label{eq:ceff}
\end{equation}
Note that the coefficient of $P_{\rm turb}$ in
equation~(\ref{eq:ceff}) is unity (not $\gamma$), because
$P_{\rm turb}$ acts as an independent isotropic pressure with its own
equation of state.  The effective sound speed enters the signal speed
estimates $S_L$, $S_R$ (HLLC/HLL), the Lagrangian sound speed
$c_L^2 = \rho^2 c_{\rm eff}^2$ (Roe-approximate), and the CFL
time-step computation (\code{cmpdt}).

\paragraph{Energy flux.}
The total energy density $E = \frac{1}{2}\rho v^2 + e_{\rm therm}$
does \emph{not} include $\rho e_{\rm sgs}$.  Following
\citet{Schmidt2011}, the SGS energy is treated as a separate
conserved quantity advected by the Godunov scheme.  However,
$P_{\rm turb}$ contributes to the energy flux:
\begin{equation}
  F_E = (E + P_{\rm tot})\,v_n
  = (E + P_{\rm therm} + P_{\rm turb})\,v_n,
  \label{eq:energy_flux}
\end{equation}
where $v_n$ is the velocity normal to the cell interface.  This
ensures that the $P_{\rm turb}\,\nabla\cdot\mathbf{v}$ work done by
turbulent pressure in compression/expansion is correctly accounted
for in the total energy budget.

\paragraph{Primitive variable reconstruction.}
In the MUSCL--Hancock reconstruction (\code{ctoprim}), the sound
speed used for slope limiting and characteristic tracing is likewise
replaced by $c_{\rm eff}$ (equation~\ref{eq:ceff}), ensuring
numerical stability at cell interfaces where $P_{\rm turb}$ is
significant.


\subsubsection{Implicit Source Term Integration}
\label{sec:sgs_implicit}

The dissipation term (equation~\ref{eq:sgs_diss}) is stiff:
the dissipation time-scale $t_{\rm diss} = \dx/\sigma_{\rm turb}$
can be orders of magnitude shorter than the CFL time-step in
high-$e_{\rm sgs}$ regions.  Explicit integration would require
prohibitively small sub-steps.

We exploit the analytical structure of the dissipation ODE to obtain
an unconditionally stable implicit solution without iteration.
Consider the pure dissipation problem for
$\sigma = \sqrt{2e_{\rm sgs}/3}$:
\begin{equation}
  \frac{\mathrm{d}\sigma^2}{\mathrm{d}t}
  = -\frac{2}{3}\,\frac{C_{\rm diss}\,(\sigma^2)^{3/2}}{\dx},
\end{equation}
which has the exact solution
\begin{equation}
  \sigma_{\rm new}
  = \frac{\sigma_{\rm old}}{
    1 + \dfrac{C_{\rm diss}\,\sigma_{\rm old}\,\Delta t}{2\,\dx}}.
  \label{eq:sigma_implicit}
\end{equation}
This is a first-order $L$-stable scheme: $\sigma_{\rm new} \to 0$ as
$\Delta t \to \infty$, so there is no risk of overshooting or
negative energies regardless of the time-step size.  The updated SGS
energy is
\begin{equation}
  e_{\rm sgs}^{\rm new} = \tfrac{3}{2}\,\sigma_{\rm new}^2,
\end{equation}
and the dissipated energy
$\Delta e_{\rm diss} = e_{\rm sgs}^{\rm old} - e_{\rm sgs}^{\rm new}$
is deposited into the thermal energy
(equation~\ref{eq:sgs_heating}).

The production and PdV terms are integrated explicitly (first-order)
before the implicit dissipation step, using operator splitting.
Since the production rate
$\mathcal{P}_{\rm grav} \propto e_{\rm sgs}\sqrt{\rho}$ is at most
linearly stiff, explicit integration is adequate for typical CFL
time-steps.

This approach is analogous to the exact cooling integrator
(Section~\ref{sec:exact_cooling}): by exploiting the analytical
structure of the ODE, we avoid Newton--Raphson iteration entirely
and eliminate the convergence failures and overshooting that plague
implicit root-finding methods applied to stiff source terms.


\subsubsection{Jeans Criterion with SGS Turbulence}
\label{sec:sgs_jeans}

The Jeans length criterion (equation~\ref{eq:jeans_crit}) is
generalised to include turbulent pressure support:
\begin{equation}
  \lJ^{\rm eff} =
  \sqrt{\frac{\pi\,(c_s^2 + \sigma_{\rm turb}^2)}{G\,\rho}}
  < N_J\,\dx_\ell,
  \label{eq:jeans_sgs}
\end{equation}
where $\sigma_{\rm turb}^2 = \frac{2}{3}e_{\rm sgs}$ is obtained
from the locally evolved SGS energy.  This modification appears in
the \code{jeans\_length\_refine} routine and affects only the
refinement decision; the Riemann solver independently includes
$P_{\rm turb}$ in the fluxes (Section~\ref{sec:sgs_riemann}).

The combined effect is self-consistent: the SGS model provides
turbulent pressure that (i) physically supports the gas against
gravitational fragmentation, (ii) increases the effective Jeans
length so that the AMR refinement criterion is satisfied at coarser
levels, and (iii) reduces the number of fine time-steps through
subcycling, improving computational efficiency.

\paragraph{Comparison with the polytropic floor.}
The polytropic equation of state
$T = T_\star(n_{\rm H}/\nstar)^{\gstar - 1}$
serves a similar purpose --- preventing unresolved fragmentation
--- but acts uniformly across all scales.  Setting $\gstar = 2$
makes $\lJ \propto \rho^0$ (constant), which prevents the cascade
but imposes a CFL penalty proportional to $(n_{\rm H}/\nstar)^{1/2}$,
reducing the time-step by a factor of $\sim$300 at densities
$n_{\rm H} \sim 10^4\,{\rm cm^{-3}}$.  The SGS model avoids this
penalty because $\sigma_{\rm eq} \propto \dx$ scales with the local
grid spacing: fine cells have small $\sigma_{\rm turb}$ and therefore
no CFL overhead, while coarse cells have large $\sigma_{\rm turb}$
that prevents unnecessary refinement.

\subsubsection{Implementation Notes}
\label{sec:sgs_impl}

\paragraph{Variable infrastructure.}
The SGS energy density is stored as the $N_{\rm var}$-th hydro
variable (index \code{isgs}), appended after the chemical species
index \code{ichem} in the passive scalar chain:
\begin{equation*}
  \code{imetal} \to \code{idelay} \to \code{ivirial}
  \to \code{ixion} \to \code{ichem} \to \code{isgs}.
\end{equation*}
The code verifies at startup that $\code{isgs} \le \code{NVAR}$
and exits with a diagnostic message if the binary was compiled with
insufficient variables.

\paragraph{Source term ordering.}
The SGS source terms are applied after cooling
(\code{cooling\_fine}) and before the next Poisson solve in the
\code{amr\_step} loop.  This ordering ensures that the thermal
state is updated before the SGS PdV coupling accesses the divergence
field, and that the gravitational production uses the current
density field.

\paragraph{Divergence field.}
The pressure--dilatation term requires $\nabla\cdot\mathbf{v}$.
Rather than computing velocity gradients from scratch (which would
require careful treatment of AMR level boundaries), we reuse the
\code{divu} array already computed by the Godunov solver when
\code{pressure\_fix=.true.}.  This array stores
$-\nabla\cdot\mathbf{v}\,\Delta t$ and is available at every
leaf cell, ensuring consistency with the resolved-scale dynamics.

\paragraph{Restart compatibility.}
When restarting from a checkpoint written with $\code{NVAR}=11$
(no SGS), the restore routine reads 11 variables per cell and
initialises the 12th (\code{isgs}) to zero.  The SGS floor ensures
positivity, and the production term rapidly builds up
$e_{\rm sgs}$ from zero to its equilibrium value within a few
free-fall times.

\section{Exact Cooling Integration}
\label{sec:exact_cooling}
%======================================================================

The cooling module determines the equilibrium temperature $T_{\rm eq}$
of the gas, which controls the Jeans length
$\lJ \propto \sqrt{T/\rho}$ and hence the refinement decisions.
Errors in the numerical integration scheme propagate directly into
errors in $\lJ$ and may trigger (or suppress) refinement at the wrong
density and temperature.  We implement an exact integration method
based on the \citet{Townsend2009} $Y$-function, coupled with
multi-redshift table interpolation to track the evolving UV background
across cosmic time.


\subsection{The Townsend Exact Integration Scheme}
\label{sec:townsend}

To eliminate the numerical overshooting inherent in implicit
cooling solvers (see Section~\ref{sec:overshoot}), we implement the exact integration
method of \citet{Townsend2009}, which transforms the cooling ODE into
an analytically invertible form using the $Y$-function.

\subsubsection{Y-Function Formulation}

For a piecewise power-law cooling function
$\Lambda(T) \propto T^{\alpha_j}$ in each temperature bin
$[T_j, T_{j+1}]$, the integrated cooling variable is
\begin{equation}
  Y(T) = \int_{T_{\rm ref}}^{T} \frac{dT'}{T'\,\hat{\Lambda}(T')}
  = \sum_{k} \Delta Y_k,
  \label{eq:Y_func}
\end{equation}
where $\hat{\Lambda} = \Lambda/\Lambda_{\rm ref}$ is the normalised
cooling rate.  Each segment contributes
\begin{equation}
  \Delta Y_j = \begin{cases}
    \dfrac{T_j^{-\alpha_j} - T_{j+1}^{-\alpha_j}}
          {\alpha_j\,\hat{\Lambda}_j\,T_j^{-\alpha_j}}
    & (\alpha_j \ne 0), \\[6pt]
    \dfrac{\ln(T_j/T_{j+1})}{\hat{\Lambda}_j}
    & (\alpha_j = 0).
  \end{cases}
  \label{eq:deltaY}
\end{equation}

The exact temperature after a time-step $\Delta t$ is obtained by
inverting the relation
\begin{equation}
  Y(T^{n+1}) = Y(T^n) + \Lambda_{\rm ref}\,\frac{n_{\rm H}}{E_0}\,\Delta t,
  \label{eq:Y_advance}
\end{equation}
where $E_0 = (3/2)\,n_{\rm tot}\,k_{\rm B}$ is the thermal energy per
unit temperature.  The inversion is performed by binary search on the
cumulative $Y$-table and local analytic inversion within the identified
segment.

\subsubsection{Advantages Over Newton--Raphson}

The $Y$-function scheme has several fundamental advantages:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{No overshooting.}  The method integrates the cooling
    equation \emph{exactly} for piecewise power-law rates.  The
    temperature evolves monotonically towards the equilibrium; it cannot
    oscillate across $T_{\rm eq}$.
  \item \textbf{No sub-stepping.}  A single evaluation of
    equation~(\ref{eq:Y_advance}) advances the solution by the full
    hydrodynamic time-step, regardless of $t_{\rm cool}/\Delta t$.  The
    N-R solver requires up to 500 sub-steps when $\Delta t \gg t_{\rm cool}$.
  \item \textbf{Correct equilibrium.}  When $\Delta t \to \infty$,
    $Y \to Y_{\rm eq}$ and the cell relaxes exactly to $T_{\rm eq}$.
    The N-R solver only reaches $T_{\rm eq}$ asymptotically through
    iteration, and may settle on the wrong side if the sub-step
    structure interacts with the sign change.
  \item \textbf{Deterministic.}  The result depends only on
    $(T^n, n_{\rm H}, Z, \Delta t)$ and the cooling table, with no
    sensitivity to iteration count, convergence tolerance, or adaptive
    step heuristics.
\end{enumerate}

The cost is a table lookup and a $\log/\exp$ evaluation per cell
($\sim$few $\mu$s), comparable to a single N-R sub-step.  Since the N-R
solver typically requires 5--50 sub-steps, the exact scheme is faster
for strongly cooling gas.

\subsubsection{Implementation: Eunha Cooling Module}

The exact integrator is implemented as a Fortran module
(\code{eunha\_cooling\_mod.f90}) that reads a 3D Grackle equilibrium
cooling table $\Lambda_{\rm net}(n_{\rm H}, Z, T)$ and pre-computes
the cumulative $Y$-table.  The module provides three runtime modes
selected by the namelist parameter \code{cooling\_method}:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \code{`original'} --- standard \ramses\ N-R solver (default,
    no overhead);
  \item \code{`exact'} --- Townsend exact integration replaces the N-R
    solver;
  \item \code{`compare'} --- both solvers run; the N-R result is used
    for physics while per-step diagnostics report the difference
    $\Delta T_2^{\rm orig} - \Delta T_2^{\rm exact}$.
\end{itemize}

The Grackle table adds a third axis (metallicity $Z$) with 40 bins
spanning $10^{-6}$--$10\,Z_\odot$, compared to the 2D RAMSES table
with linear $Z$ scaling.  The mean molecular weight $\mu(n_{\rm H}, Z, T)$
is also tabulated from Grackle's non-LTE equilibrium, replacing the
RAMSES CIE calculation.  The $T_2 \leftrightarrow T$ conversion uses an
iterative scheme: $\mu \to T = T_2\,\mu \to \mu'(n_{\rm H}, Z, T) \to
T' = T_2\,\mu'$, converging to $<0.1$\,per\,cent in 3 iterations.


\subsection{Redshift-Dependent Cooling: Multi-z Tables}
\label{sec:multi_z}

The cooling rate depends on redshift through the UV background
intensity $J_\nu(z)$, which evolves as the ionising photon production
rate rises during cosmic reionisation ($z \gtrsim 6$), peaks near
$z \sim 2$--$3$, and declines towards $z = 0$ as the star formation
rate density decreases \citep{HM2012, FG2020}.
The equilibrium temperature
$T_{\rm eq}(n_{\rm H}, z)$ therefore evolves with redshift even at
fixed density.

\subsubsection{How the standard solver tracks redshift}

The standard \ramses\ cooling module calls \code{set\_table(aexp)} at
each coarse time-step, rebuilding the 2D cooling table
$\Lambda(n_{\rm H}, T_2)$ using the Courty UV background model
evaluated at the current expansion factor~$a$.  This analytic UV model
is a polynomial fit to Cloudy~07, so the table rebuild is
inexpensive and correctly tracks the redshift evolution of the
background --- within the limitations of the Courty model itself
(Section~\ref{sec:2d_table}).

\subsubsection{The single-z limitation of the Grackle table}

The Grackle-based Eunha module (\code{eunha\_cooling\_mod.f90})
initially loaded a single pre-computed 3D table at a fixed redshift
(e.g.\ $z = 5.1$ for the HR5 restart).  While the cooling rates are
more accurate than the RAMSES 2D table at that redshift, the table
becomes increasingly wrong as the simulation evolves away from the
tabulated~$z$.  In particular:

\begin{itemize}
  \item At $z < z_{\rm table}$, the UV background is stronger (more
    photo-heating), so the true $T_{\rm eq}$ is higher than the
    tabulated value.  The exact integrator would drive cells to an
    \emph{artificially low} equilibrium temperature, underestimating
    $\lJ$ and potentially triggering spurious refinement.
  \item At $z > z_{\rm table}$, the UV background is weaker, and the
    exact integrator would settle on a $T_{\rm eq}$ that is too high,
    overestimating $\lJ$ and suppressing refinement.
  \item The most rapid UV evolution occurs during hydrogen reionisation
    ($z \sim 6$--$8$) and He~{\sc ii} reionisation ($z \sim 3$--$4$),
    where $T_{\rm eq}$ can change by factors of 2--3 over $\Delta z
    \sim 1$.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Multi-z table and runtime interpolation}

We address this by pre-computing Grackle equilibrium tables at multiple
redshifts and interpolating at runtime.  The offline generator
(\code{generate\_multi\_z\_table.py}) calls the Grackle library at
each redshift snapshot to produce a single multi-z binary file
containing $N_z$ copies of the 3D table $(n_{\rm H}, Z, T)$.

The default sampling uses 10~redshift snapshots:
\begin{equation}
  z \in \{0.0,\; 0.5,\; 1.0,\; 1.5,\; 2.0,\; 3.0,\; 4.0,\; 5.0,\;
  7.0,\; 10.0\},
  \label{eq:z_grid}
\end{equation}
with denser spacing at low~$z$ ($\Delta z = 0.5$ for $z \le 2$) where
the UV background evolves most rapidly relative to the Hubble time, and
coarser spacing at high~$z$ ($\Delta z \ge 1$).

At each coarse time-step, \code{eunha\_interp\_redshift($z$)} performs
the following:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item Finds the bracketing snapshots $z_i \le z < z_{i+1}$ and
    computes the linear interpolation weight
    $w = (z - z_i)/(z_{i+1} - z_i)$.
  \item Interpolates the net cooling rate and mean molecular weight:
    \begin{align}
      \Lambda_{\rm net}(n_{\rm H}, Z, T; z)
        &= (1{-}w)\,\Lambda^{(i)} + w\,\Lambda^{(i+1)}, \\
      \mu(n_{\rm H}, Z, T; z)
        &= (1{-}w)\,\mu^{(i)} + w\,\mu^{(i+1)}.
    \end{align}
  \item Rebuilds the cumulative $Y$-table for all
    $N_{\rm dens} \times N_{\rm metal} = 60 \times 40 = 2400$ cells
    (OpenMP parallelised).
  \item Caches the current redshift; the rebuild is skipped if
    $|\Delta z| < 0.01$.
\end{enumerate}

The total file size for 10 snapshots on the standard
$60 \times 40 \times 256$ grid is
$10 \times 2 \times 614400 \times 8 \approx 94\,$MB, which is
distributed with the source code.  Backward compatibility with
single-z files is maintained through automatic format detection
(the first \code{int32} in a multi-z file encodes $N_z$, which is
distinguishable from the grid dimension $L = 60$).

\subsubsection{Impact on cooling overshoot}
\label{sec:multi_z_overshoot}

The multi-z table eliminates a systematic error that compounds with the
numerical overshoot discussed in Section~\ref{sec:overshoot}.  Consider
a simulation at $z = 2$ using a table computed at $z = 5$:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item The UV background at $z = 2$ is $\sim 3$--$5\times$ stronger
    than at $z = 5$ \citep{HM2012}, so
    $T_{\rm eq}^{z=2} > T_{\rm eq}^{z=5}$.
  \item A cell cooling towards equilibrium at $z = 2$ would be driven
    to the \emph{wrong} (lower) $T_{\rm eq}^{z=5}$ by the stale table.
  \item Even with the exact integrator (no numerical overshoot), the
    cell reaches an incorrect equilibrium, yielding a systematically
    too-small Jeans length.
  \item The N-R solver compounds this: it may overshoot below the
    already-too-low $T_{\rm eq}^{z=5}$, further reducing $\lJ$.
\end{enumerate}

With the multi-z table, the Eunha module tracks the correct
$T_{\rm eq}(z)$ at every coarse step, matching the \ramses\ solver's
\code{set\_table(aexp)} behaviour but with the improved Grackle rates
and the exact integration scheme.  The combination eliminates both
the systematic error (wrong UV background) and the numerical error
(N-R overshoot), providing the most accurate equilibrium temperature
determination available within the operator-split cooling framework.

\section{Cooling Rates and the Jeans Length}
\label{sec:cooling}
%======================================================================

Having described the exact integration scheme, we now analyse the
cooling rates themselves.  The underlying cooling tables and UV
background model differ between the standard \ramses\ solver and the
Grackle equilibrium chemistry library \citep{Smith2017}.  These
differences propagate into the equilibrium temperature and hence affect
the Jeans length evaluation and refinement decisions.


\subsection{The Standard \ramses\ Cooling Solver}
\label{sec:ramses_cooling}

The \ramses\ cooling module solves the energy equation
\begin{equation}
  \frac{dT_2}{dt} = -\frac{2 X_{\rm H}}{3 k_{\rm B}}\,
  \frac{\Lambda_{\rm net}(n_{\rm H}, T_2)}{n_{\rm H}},
  \label{eq:cooling_ode}
\end{equation}
where $T_2 \equiv T/\mu$ is the temperature per mean molecular weight
and $\Lambda_{\rm net}$ is the net volumetric cooling rate
(cooling $-$ heating) in $\rm erg\,cm^{-3}\,s^{-1}$.

The solver uses a semi-implicit Newton--Raphson iteration.  At each
sub-step, the temperature is updated as
\begin{equation}
  T_2^{(n+1)} = T_2^{(n)}\,
  \frac{1 + \Lambda'/w_{\rm cool} - \Lambda/(T_2^{(n)}\,w_{\rm cool})}
       {1 + \Lambda'/w_{\rm cool}},
  \label{eq:NR_update}
\end{equation}
where $\Lambda' \equiv d\Lambda_{\rm net}/dT_2$ and the adaptive step
control parameter is
$w_{\rm cool} = \max\bigl(|\Lambda|\,\Delta_{\rm var}/T_2,\;
w_{\rm max},\;{-\Lambda'\,\Delta_{\rm var}}\bigr)$
with $\Delta_{\rm var} = 4$.

The cooling rates $\Lambda_{\rm net}$ and their derivatives are
interpolated from a pre-computed 2D table
$\Lambda(n_{\rm H}, T_2)$, built at the start of the simulation for the
current expansion factor $a$.  The table contains separate contributions
from:
\begin{itemize}
  \item H/He collisional cooling (ionisation, recombination,
    bremsstrahlung, excitation) computed from collisional ionisation
    equilibrium \citep[CIE;][]{Sutherland1993};
  \item UV photo-heating from the \citet{Courty2004} background model;
  \item Compton cooling/heating off the CMB;
  \item metal cooling from the Cloudy~07 table
    \citep{Ploeckinger2015}, scaled linearly with metallicity~$Z$.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Adaptive Sub-stepping (Subcycling)}
\label{sec:subcycling}

It is sometimes stated that the \ramses\ cooling solver uses subcycling
to prevent overcooling.  This is correct: rather than applying
equation~(\ref{eq:NR_update}) once per hydrodynamic time-step~$\Delta t$,
the solver integrates forward in time through a sequence of adaptive
sub-steps.  The algorithm maintains a pseudo-time variable
$t_{\rm sub}$, initialised to zero, and repeats the linearised implicit
update until $t_{\rm sub}$ reaches the target
$t_{\rm max} = (2X_{\rm H}/3k_{\rm B})\,n_{\rm H}\,\Delta t$.
Each sub-step advances $t_{\rm sub}$ by
$\delta t_{\rm sub} = 1/w_{\rm cool}$, where $w_{\rm cool}$ is the
adaptive rate defined in equation~(\ref{eq:NR_update}).  Cells that
reach $t_{\rm max}$ are deactivated; the loop terminates when all cells
are done or after 500 iterations.  A final linear interpolation between
the last two sub-steps ensures that the solution lands exactly at
$t_{\rm max}$:
\begin{equation}
  T_2^{\rm final} = T_2^{(n)}\,
  \frac{t_{\rm max} - t^{(n-1)}}{t^{(n)} - t^{(n-1)}}
  + T_2^{(n-1)}\,
  \frac{t^{(n)} - t_{\rm max}}{t^{(n)} - t^{(n-1)}}.
  \label{eq:subcycle_interp}
\end{equation}

The key parameter controlling sub-step size is
$\Delta_{\rm var} = 4$, which limits the relative temperature change per
sub-step to roughly a factor of~4.  When the cooling rate is large, the
solver takes many small sub-steps and tracks the temperature evolution
accurately.  In practice, however, three issues remain:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{First-order accuracy.}  Each sub-step is a linearised
    implicit Euler update --- first-order in~$\delta t_{\rm sub}$.
    Higher-order schemes (e.g.\ Runge--Kutta) would reduce the number of
    sub-steps needed for the same accuracy, but the \ramses\ solver does
    not employ them.
  \item \textbf{Permissive step control.}  $\Delta_{\rm var} = 4$ allows
    the temperature to change by up to a factor of~4 in a single
    sub-step.  Near the cooling--heating equilibrium, where the
    temperature needs to settle to $T_{\rm eq}$ with per cent-level
    accuracy (since the Jeans length scales as $T^{1/2}$), this is too
    coarse.
  \item \textbf{Final interpolation error.}  The linear interpolation
    (equation~\ref{eq:subcycle_interp}) assumes that $T_2$ varies
    linearly between the last two sub-steps.  If the net cooling rate
    changes sign in this interval (i.e.\ the cell crosses $T_{\rm eq}$
    between sub-steps $n{-}1$ and~$n$), the interpolation can land on
    the wrong side of the equilibrium.
\end{enumerate}

Thus, while subcycling mitigates the most extreme errors, it
does not eliminate the systematic biases discussed below.

\subsubsection{Time-Step Dependence and Spurious Temperature
  Discontinuities}
\label{sec:overshoot}

The most damaging property of the N-R subcycling solver in an AMR
context is that the computed final temperature depends on the
hydrodynamic time-step~$\Delta t$.
Fig.~\ref{fig:cool_dtindep} demonstrates this directly: for the ISM
and Dense ISM conditions ($n_{\rm H} = 1$--$10\,{\rm cm}^{-3}$,
$T_0 = 3 \times 10^4\,$K), the N-R solver with
$\Delta_{\rm var} = 4$ returns systematically different temperatures
as $\Delta t / t_{\rm cool}$ varies from $10^{-2}$ to $10^3$.
The Townsend exact integrator, by contrast, is genuinely
$\Delta t$-independent across the entire range.

In an AMR simulation with subcycling ($\code{nsubcycle} = 1,\,1,\,2$),
adjacent cells at different refinement levels receive time-steps
differing by factors of~2.  Cells at the same physical conditions
but on different levels therefore receive \emph{different} final
temperatures from the N-R solver.  This creates artificial temperature
discontinuities at level boundaries.  Through the Jeans length
$\lJ \propto T^{1/2}\,\rho^{-1/2}$, these temperature jumps translate
directly into spurious refinement triggers: a cell on the coarser
level may remain above the Jeans threshold while its neighbour on
the finer level falls below it, solely due to the time-step difference.

\textbf{Undercooling bias.}
As shown in Fig.~\ref{fig:cool_traj}, the N-R solver with
$\Delta_{\rm var} = 4$ systematically \emph{undercools} in the ISM
regime --- the computed temperature remains above the RK4 reference
trajectory.  This is a consequence of the semi-implicit (backward
Euler) nature of the scheme: the implicit linearisation evaluates the
cooling rate at the \emph{updated} temperature, which is lower and
closer to equilibrium, where $|\Lambda_{\rm net}|$ is smaller.  The
result is a systematic underestimate of the integrated cooling.  The
bias increases with the time-step size, creating a correlation between
AMR level and temperature error.

\textbf{Level-dependent temperature gradient.}
Consider a uniform-density region being photoionisation-heated.  With
exact cooling, all cells converge to the same $T_{\rm eq}$ regardless
of their AMR level.  With the N-R solver, cells on coarser levels
(larger $\Delta t$) systematically undercool more than cells on finer
levels, creating an artificial temperature gradient across the level
boundary.  This gradient can trigger the Jeans criterion where no
physical density enhancement exists, initiating a spurious refinement
cascade.

\textbf{Cumulative error.}
Because $T_2$ enters the next hydrodynamic time-step as the updated
thermal energy, any bias at step $n$ feeds back into the pressure
and hence the Jeans length at step $n{+}1$.  Over many time-steps,
the level-dependent temperature bias accumulates, creating persistent
artefacts in the temperature field that drive sustained spurious
refinement.

\textbf{Time-step coupling.}
The maximum sub-step size within \code{solve\_cooling} is determined by
the cooling time $t_{\rm cool} = (3/2)\,n k_{\rm B} T / |\Lambda_{\rm net}|$.
When $\Lambda_{\rm net}$ is large, the solver takes many small sub-steps
and is accurate.  But near equilibrium, $|\Lambda_{\rm net}|$ is small,
$t_{\rm cool}$ is large, and the solver takes fewer, larger steps ---
precisely where accuracy matters most for determining the equilibrium
temperature.

\subsubsection{Limitations of the 2D Table}
\label{sec:2d_table}

Beyond the numerical integration, the cooling \emph{rates} themselves
have systematic biases:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{No metallicity axis.}
    The \ramses\ table is 2D: $(n_{\rm H}, T_2)$.  Metal cooling is
    stored as a separate 1D function $\Lambda_Z(T)$ at solar
    metallicity, then scaled as $Z \cdot \Lambda_Z$.  This linear scaling
    is correct for CIE but neglects the density-dependent ionisation
    corrections that become important at $n_{\rm H} > 1\,{\rm cm}^{-3}$,
    where photo-ionisation by the UV background cannot penetrate and the
    gas is self-shielded.

  \item \textbf{CIE assumption.}
    The mean molecular weight $\mu$ is computed assuming collisional
    ionisation equilibrium at each $(n_{\rm H}, T)$ point.  This neglects
    time-dependent ionisation (important during rapid cooling) and
    photo-ionisation at low temperatures (important in the diffuse IGM
    at $T < 10^4\,{\rm K}$), where the UV background maintains a
    residual ionisation fraction that CIE would predict to be negligible.

  \item \textbf{UV background model.}
    The default Courty model is a polynomial fit calibrated against
    Cloudy~07.  More recent UV background models
    \citep[e.g.][]{HM2012,FG2020} differ significantly at $z > 3$,
    particularly in the He~{\sc ii} reionisation epoch ($z \sim 3$--$4$)
    and at the earliest epochs where the UV intensity rises steeply.

  \item \textbf{Temperature variable $T_2 = T/\mu$.}
    The solver works with $T_2$ rather than $T$.  Since $\mu$ changes
    across the ionisation transition ($\mu \approx 1.22$ for neutral gas,
    $\mu \approx 0.59$ for fully ionised), a small change in $T_2$ maps
    to a factor-of-two change in physical temperature $T$ across the
    transition.  This amplifies interpolation errors near
    $T \sim 10^4\,{\rm K}$.
\end{enumerate}


\subsection{Comparison on the Density--Temperature Plane}
\label{sec:cool_compare}

To quantify the systematic differences, we compare the \ramses\ net
cooling rate against the Grackle equilibrium cooling library
\citep{Smith2017}, which uses the \citet{HM2012} UV background
and includes density-dependent metal cooling with full
non-LTE ionisation corrections.  We compute
$\Lambda_{\rm net}(n_{\rm H}, T)$ on a grid of $60 \times 256$ points
spanning $n_{\rm H} = 10^{-8}$--$10^{4}\,{\rm cm}^{-3}$ and
$T = 10$--$10^{9}\,{\rm K}$ at $z = 5.1$ (the redshift of the HR5 test
restart).

\textbf{Low metallicity ($Z = 10^{-3}\,Z_\odot$).}
Fig.~\ref{fig:cool_diff_lowZ} compares the two models at the
simulation average metallicity ($\code{z\_ave} = 10^{-3}$).  At this
low metallicity, the net rate is dominated by H/He processes and UV
photo-heating.

The lower-left panel shows the log ratio of the cooling rates,
$\log_{10}(|\Lambda_{\rm RAMSES}|/|\Lambda_{\rm Grackle}|)$.
Contour lines mark the thermal equilibrium ($\Lambda_{\rm net} = 0$)

line for Grackle.  Three features are apparent:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item The \emph{equilibrium temperature} differs by up to
    $\Delta\log T \sim 0.3$--$0.5$ at low densities
    ($n_{\rm H} \lesssim 10^{-2}\,{\rm cm}^{-3}$), reflecting the
    different UV backgrounds.  Since $\lJ \propto T^{1/2}$, this
    translates to a $\sim 40$--$80$\,per\,cent difference in the Jeans
    length at the same density.
  \item At intermediate temperatures ($10^4$--$10^5\,{\rm K}$),
    the RAMSES rate is systematically \emph{stronger} (redder in the
    ratio panel) by up to an order of magnitude.  This arises because
    the CIE assumption overpredicts the neutral hydrogen fraction at
    these temperatures for low-density gas where the UV background
    maintains significant photo-ionisation.
  \item At $T > 10^7\,{\rm K}$, both models converge to the
    bremsstrahlung limit $\Lambda \propto T^{1/2}\,n_{\rm H}^2$ and
    the ratio approaches unity, as expected.
\end{enumerate}

The lower-right panel shows the mean molecular weight difference
$\Delta\mu = \mu_{\rm RAMSES} - \mu_{\rm Grackle}$.  The RAMSES CIE
$\mu$ is systematically higher than the Grackle non-LTE value at
$T > 10^5\,{\rm K}$.  At intermediate temperatures
($T \sim 10^4$--$10^5\,{\rm K}$), the Grackle $\mu$ shows a strong
\emph{density dependence} due to the UV background maintaining partial
ionisation at low densities --- a feature entirely absent from the
RAMSES model, where $\mu(T_2)$ is independent of density.  This
density-dependent $\mu$ has direct implications for the $T_2 \to T$
conversion and hence for the Jeans length evaluation.

\subsection{Implications for the Jeans Criterion}
\label{sec:cool_jeans}

The combined effect of the cooling rate differences
(Figs~\ref{fig:cool_diff_lowZ}--\ref{fig:cool_diff_Zsol}), the
integration scheme, and the redshift evolution of the UV background
impacts the Jeans refinement criterion through four channels:

\textbf{1. Equilibrium temperature.}
The Jeans length at thermal equilibrium is
$\lJ^{\rm eq} \propto (T_{\rm eq}/\rho)^{1/2}$.  Since
$T_{\rm eq}^{\rm RAMSES} \ne T_{\rm eq}^{\rm Grackle}$ (bottom-left
panels), the two solvers will flag different cells.  At low densities,
the RAMSES equilibrium is systematically \emph{higher}
($T_{\rm eq} \sim 2$--$3\times$ warmer), yielding a larger Jeans length
and less refinement.  This means the standard solver may
\emph{underestimate} the refinement needed in filaments and the
circumgalactic medium.

\textbf{2. Cooling overshoot near $T_{\rm eq}$.}
The N-R solver's tendency to overshoot $T_{\rm eq}$
(Section~\ref{sec:overshoot}) creates transient temperature errors that
affect the \emph{instantaneous} Jeans length even if the time-averaged
temperature is correct.  A cell that overshoots to $T < T_{\rm eq}$
will have a transiently small $\lJ$, triggering refinement that may
not be needed.  Conversely, a cell that oscillates above $T_{\rm eq}$
will have a transiently large $\lJ$, suppressing refinement that
\emph{is} needed.  The exact integrator eliminates this noise entirely.

\textbf{3. Mean molecular weight.}
The $T_2 = T/\mu$ representation amplifies errors in $\mu$.  With the
RAMSES CIE model, $\mu$ is a function of $T_2$ alone (density-independent).
The Grackle non-LTE $\mu$ depends on both $n_{\rm H}$ and $T$, with
$|\Delta\mu| \sim 0.3$--$0.6$ in the transition zone
($T \sim 10^4$--$10^5\,{\rm K}$; bottom-right panels).  Since $T =
T_2\,\mu$, a 50\,per\,cent error in $\mu$ produces a 50\,per\,cent
error in $T$ and hence a $\sim 22$\,per\,cent error in $\lJ$,
comparable to the factor-of-two ratio that separates adjacent
refinement levels.

\textbf{4. Redshift evolution of $T_{\rm eq}$.}
The thermal equilibrium temperature evolves with redshift as the UV
background strengthens (towards $z \sim 2$) and then weakens (towards
$z = 0$).  A fixed-$z$ Grackle table ignores this evolution, producing
a systematic offset in $T_{\rm eq}$ that grows with $|z - z_{\rm table}|$.
At the epoch of maximum UV background ($z \sim 2$--$3$), this offset
can reach factors of 2--3 in $T_{\rm eq}$ if the table was computed at
$z = 5$, corresponding to a $\sim 40$--$70$\,per\,cent error in $\lJ$.
The multi-z interpolation (Section~\ref{sec:multi_z}) eliminates this
systematic bias, ensuring that $T_{\rm eq}$ tracks the correct UV
background at every epoch.

and their impact on the Jeans length.

\section{The Starburst Problem}
\label{sec:starburst}
%======================================================================

The holdback mechanism (Section~\ref{sec:amr_refine}) blocks
\emph{all} refinement criteria at levels $\ell \ge \ell_{\rm hb}$,
including the Jeans criterion.  This creates two related problems:

\textbf{Artificial fragmentation.}
The Jeans length criterion $\mathcal{J}$ differs fundamentally from
the other refinement triggers: it is a \emph{numerical stability}
requirement \citep{Truelove1997}, not merely a resolution preference.
Violating the Jeans condition leads to artificial fragmentation ---
a qualitatively wrong result --- regardless of epoch.  Yet in the
standard \ramses\ logic (equation~\ref{eq:refine_flag}), the
cosmological holdback $\mathcal{H}$ blocks $\mathcal{J}$ uniformly
with all other criteria.  At high redshift, dense self-gravitating
regions may therefore be held at insufficient resolution, producing
spurious fragmentation that propagates into lower-redshift structure.

\textbf{Abrupt SFR transition.}
When the holdback releases level $\ell$ (i.e.\ $\mathcal{H}(\ell)$
transitions from true to false), all accumulated criteria fire
simultaneously.  Many cells that have been gravitationally collapsing
during the holdback period now suddenly satisfy the refinement
condition, leading to a burst of new cells and an abrupt spike in
the star formation rate.

\section{Jeans Bypass: Design and Implementation}
\label{sec:bypass}
%======================================================================

\subsection{Modified Refinement Logic}

The key observation is that the Jeans length criterion is
\emph{cell-local}: it depends only on the density and temperature of the
cell itself (\code{uold}), with no neighbour information required.  This
allows a lightweight evaluation even when the full refinement machinery
(which requires neighbour gathering for $\mathcal{D}$, $\mathcal{P}$,
$\mathcal{U}$) is skipped by the holdback.

With \code{jeans\_bypass\_holdback=.true.}, the refinement logic becomes
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{R}' = \Bigl[
  \lnot\,\mathcal{H} \;\land\; \mathcal{G} \;\land\;
  \bigl(\mathcal{M} \lor \mathcal{S} \lor \mathcal{D}
        \lor \mathcal{P} \lor \mathcal{U} \lor \mathcal{J}\bigr)
\Bigr]
\;\lor\;
\Bigl[ \mathcal{H} \;\land\; \mathcal{J} \;\land\;
  (\ell < \ell_{\rm hb} + n_{\rm bypass}) \Bigr],
\label{eq:refine_bypass}
\end{equation}
where $n_{\rm bypass} \equiv$ \code{n\_jeans\_bypass\_levels}
(default 1).  The geometry mask $\mathcal{G}$ still applies through the
non-holdback branch, preserving zoom-in spatial restrictions.  Only the
temporal holdback $\mathcal{H}$ is bypassed for $\mathcal{J}$, and only
within a limited number of levels.

This modification is analogous to the existing treatment of sink
particles: $\mathcal{S}$ is already evaluated inside the
holdback-protected block, but unlike $\mathcal{J}$ it only triggers in
cells that already host a sink.  Extending the same exemption to
$\mathcal{J}$ ensures numerical integrity for gas that is
\emph{about to} form a sink.


\subsection{Level Limitation: Preventing Cascades}
\label{sec:cascade}

Without a level limit ($n_{\rm bypass}\to\infty$), the Jeans bypass
creates a catastrophic cascade.  When a cell at the holdback level
$\ell_{\rm hb}$ is flagged by the Jeans criterion, child cells are
created at level $\ell_{\rm hb}+1$.  Since level $\ell_{\rm hb}+1$ is
also in the holdback zone, the bypass applies again, flagging cells at
$\ell_{\rm hb}+2$, and so on until $\ell_{\rm max}$.  This rapidly
exhausts available memory.

The cascade occurs because the Jeans length in radiatively cooling gas
(below the star-formation density threshold) shrinks without bound as
the temperature drops.  There is no physical mechanism to halt the
Jeans criterion from triggering at every successive level --- only the
polytropic temperature floor (Section~\ref{sec:polytropic}) or a hard
level limit can stop it.

\section{Role of the Polytropic Temperature Floor}
\label{sec:polytropic}
%======================================================================

\subsection{Jeans Length in the Polytropic Regime}

In \ramses, the star-formation module enforces a polytropic equation
of state above the density threshold $\nstar$:
\begin{equation}
  T(n) = \Tstar\,\Bigl(\frac{n}{\nstar}\Bigr)^{\gstar - 1},
  \qquad n \ge \nstar,
  \label{eq:polytropic}
\end{equation}
with default parameters $\Tstar = 750\,{\rm K}$,
$\nstar = 0.1\,{\rm cm}^{-3}$, $\gstar = 5/3$.  This floor is
applied in the cooling routine and is already reflected in \code{uold},
so the Jeans length computation in \code{jeans\_length\_refine()}
automatically includes the polytropic stabilisation.

Substituting equation~(\ref{eq:polytropic}) into
equation~(\ref{eq:jeans_crit}):
\begin{equation}
  \lJ \propto \sqrt{\frac{T}{\rho}} \propto
  \sqrt{\rho^{\gstar-1}/\rho} = \rho^{(\gstar-2)/2}.
  \label{eq:jeans_poly}
\end{equation}
The behaviour of $\lJ$ in the polytropic regime depends critically
on the value of $\gstar$:

\begin{itemize}
  \item $\gstar < 2$: $\lJ$ decreases with density.  For the
    standard value $\gstar = 5/3$, $\lJ \propto \rho^{-1/6}$ ---
    the decrease is slow, but unbounded.  Given enough levels, a
    cascade can still develop.
  \item $\gstar = 2$: $\lJ \propto \rho^{0} = \mathrm{const}$.
    The Jeans length is \emph{independent of density} in the
    polytropic regime, so refinement converges at a fixed level
    regardless of how much the gas compresses.  This is the
    \emph{critical polytropic index} for Jeans stability.
  \item $\gstar > 2$: $\lJ$ increases with density, providing an
    even stronger self-regulation.
\end{itemize}

The $\gstar = 2$ case is appealing because it eliminates the
cascade problem entirely without any additional mechanisms (no
holdback, no \code{d\_jeans\_thre}, no level limits).  However,
it comes at a significant physical cost: the temperature floor
at the star-formation threshold rises from $\sim$10\,900\,K to
$\sim$41\,300\,K --- nearly a factor of 4.  This dramatically
increases the pressure support in star-forming gas, suppressing
the peak densities and reducing the star formation rate.  Since
$\dot{\rho}_* \propto \rho / t_{\rm ff} \propto \rho^{3/2}$, even
modest changes in the density PDF propagate into order-of-magnitude
SFR differences.  Adopting $\gstar = 2$ therefore requires a full
recalibration of the star-formation parameters ($\epsilon_*$,
$\code{del\_star}$, $\Tstar$, $\nstar$) against observational
constraints, which is non-trivial for production simulations that
have already been calibrated with $\gstar = 5/3$.

In practice, the standard choice $\gstar = 5/3$ combined with
\code{d\_jeans\_thre}~$\approx \nstar$ provides an effective
compromise: the Jeans criterion is restricted to the polytropic
regime where $\lJ$ varies slowly ($\propto \rho^{-1/6}$), limiting
the cascade to 1--2 extra levels, while preserving the calibrated
star-formation physics.

In contrast, for gas below $\nstar$ (no polytropic floor), radiative
cooling can drive $T$ to very low values while $\rho$ remains moderate.
This yields $\lJ \ll \dx_\ell$ at many levels, causing the cascade.

\subsection{Self-regulation via \code{d\_jeans\_thre}}

The density threshold parameter \code{d\_jeans\_thre} (in units of
$n_{\rm H}\,[{\rm cm}^{-3}]$) restricts the Jeans criterion to cells
above a given density:
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{J}(\text{cell}) =
  \begin{cases}
    N_J\,\dx_\ell \ge \lJ & \text{if } n \ge
    \code{d\_jeans\_thre}, \\
    \text{false} & \text{otherwise}.
  \end{cases}
\end{equation}
Setting $\code{d\_jeans\_thre} \approx \nstar$ restricts the Jeans
criterion to the polytropic regime.  The cascade then self-limits
through the following mechanism:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item At level $\ell_{\rm hb}$: only cells with $n \ge \nstar$ are
    checked.  The polytropic floor guarantees $T \ge \Tstar$, so $\lJ$
    is relatively large.
  \item If $N_J\,\dx_{\ell_{\rm hb}} > \lJ$, the cell is flagged and
    children at $\ell_{\rm hb}+1$ are created.
  \item At level $\ell_{\rm hb}+1$: $\dx$ is halved, density is
    approximately unchanged, $\lJ$ is approximately unchanged.
  \item $N_J\,\dx_{\ell_{\rm hb}+1} = N_J\,\dx_{\ell_{\rm hb}}/2
    < \lJ$ $\Rightarrow$ Jeans criterion is \emph{satisfied}
    $\Rightarrow$ no further flagging $\Rightarrow$ cascade stops
    naturally.
\end{enumerate}

This provides a \emph{physics-based} self-regulation, complementary to
the \emph{hard limit} imposed by \code{n\_jeans\_bypass\_levels}.

\textbf{\code{flag\_utils.kjhan.f90} --- \code{userflag\_fine()}.}
The holdback early-return is modified from a simple \code{return} to a

\begin{lstlisting}
if(prevent_refine .and. q_refine_holdback) then
   if(jeans_bypass_holdback .and. hydro &
      .and. n_jeans_bypass_levels > 0) then
      ! Find the first holdback level
      dx_min = (0.5D0**nlevelmax)*scale
      holdback_threshold = &
        (4d0**(1d0/ndim))*(dx_min/aexp)
      holdback_first = nlevelmax + 1
      do jlevel = nlevelmax_part &
         + nlevel_collapse+1, nlevelmax
         dx_test = (0.5d0**jlevel)*scale
         if(dx_test < holdback_threshold) then

            exit
         endif
      end do
      ! Only bypass within allowed range
      if(ilevel < holdback_first &
         + n_jeans_bypass_levels) then

      endif
   endif
   return
endif
\end{lstlisting}

\textbf{\code{hydro\_flag.kjhan.f90} --- \code{jeans\_only\_flag()}.}
A new lightweight subroutine that:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item Loops over active grids with OpenMP parallelism.
  \item Computes cell indices without neighbour gathering.
  \item Calls the existing \code{jeans\_length\_refine()} subroutine
    (no code duplication).
  \item Sets \code{flag1} for flagged cells and updates \code{nflag}.
\end{enumerate}

This is possible because \code{jeans\_length\_refine()} only accesses
cell-local data (\code{uold(ind\_cell,...)}), unlike the gradient-based
criteria $\mathcal{D}$, $\mathcal{P}$, $\mathcal{U}$.

\section{Discussion and Future Work}
\label{sec:discussion}
%======================================================================

\subsection{Physical Motivation}

The holdback mechanism was designed to prevent over-refinement from the
mass criterion at early times.  However, it inadvertently blocks the
Jeans criterion, which has a different physical motivation: resolving
the Jeans length prevents artificial fragmentation and ensures
numerically converged star formation \citep{Truelove1997, Federrath2011}.

By allowing the Jeans criterion to operate ahead of the holdback
release, we expect:
\begin{itemize}
  \item Dense gas that will form stars is pre-refined before the
    holdback releases.
  \item When the holdback releases, the transition is smoother because
    the densest regions are already at the appropriate resolution.
  \item The SFR curve shows a gradual onset rather than an abrupt step.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Interplay of the Three Parameters}

The bypass is controlled by three parameters that form a layered
defence:

\textbf{Layer 1: \code{jeans\_bypass\_holdback} (on/off).}
Master switch.  When off (default), the system is identical to
standard \ramses.

\textbf{Layer 2: \code{d\_jeans\_thre} (physics-based regulation).}
Restricts the Jeans check to dense gas where the polytropic floor
operates.  In this regime, $\lJ \propto \rho^{(\gstar-2)/2}$ varies
slowly (for $\gstar = 5/3$, $\propto \rho^{-1/6}$), and 1--2 extra
refinement levels suffice to resolve it.  As shown in
Section~\ref{sec:polytropic}, adopting $\gstar \ge 2$ would eliminate
the cascade entirely, but at the cost of a significantly stiffer
pressure floor that requires SFR recalibration
(Table~\ref{tab:gstar}).  Provides self-consistent, physics-driven
cascade termination.

\textbf{Layer 3: \code{n\_jeans\_bypass\_levels} (hard safety limit).}
Absolute cap on the number of extra levels.  Prevents OOM regardless
of physics configuration.  Essential when
\code{d\_jeans\_thre=0} (all cells checked).


\subsection{Problems with Additional Temperature and Pressure Floors}
\label{sec:pfloor}

A common approach to enforcing the Jeans criterion in cosmological
simulations is to introduce an \emph{additional} temperature or
pressure floor beyond the star-formation polytropic floor
(equation~\ref{eq:polytropic}).  Several major simulation projects
adopt a resolution-dependent pressure floor of the form
\begin{equation}
  P_{\rm floor} = \frac{G}{\pi\,\gamma}\,
  N_J^2\,\rho\,(\dx_\ell)^2,
  \label{eq:pfloor}
\end{equation}
which ensures $\lJ \ge N_J\,\dx_\ell$ at all times
\citep[e.g.][]{Schaye2015, Hopkins2018}.  Equivalently, this
corresponds to a density-dependent temperature floor
$T_{\rm floor} \propto \rho\,(\dx_\ell)^2$.  While this
\emph{guarantees} that the Jeans criterion is never violated, it
introduces several systematic problems:

\textbf{(i) Artificial suppression of fragmentation.}
The pressure floor prevents gas from cooling below $T_{\rm floor}$,
even when the physical equilibrium temperature $T_{\rm eq}$ is much
lower.  In the density range $\nstar < n < n_{\rm floor}$ (where
$n_{\rm floor}$ is the density at which $T_{\rm floor}$ exceeds the
polytropic floor), the gas is held at an artificially high
temperature.  This suppresses the thermal instability that drives
the formation of the cold, dense phase of the ISM
\citep{Sutherland1993, Wiersma2009}, potentially preventing the
formation of molecular clouds and reducing the star formation rate.

\textbf{(ii) Resolution-dependent thermodynamics.}
Since $P_{\rm floor} \propto (\dx_\ell)^2$, the effective equation
of state of the gas depends on the grid resolution.  At higher
resolution ($\ell_{\rm max}$ increased), $\dx$ decreases and the floor
drops, allowing gas to cool further and fragment into smaller
structures.  The resulting star formation rate, stellar mass function,
and galaxy morphologies are therefore resolution-dependent --- not
converging to a physical answer as resolution increases, but rather
revealing new fragmentation at each resolution level.

\textbf{(iii) Conflict with the polytropic floor.}
The star-formation polytropic floor (equation~\ref{eq:polytropic}) is
designed to model the unresolved physics of the multiphase ISM above
$\nstar$: it represents the effective equation of state of a turbulent,
star-forming medium \citep{Schaye2015}.  An additional Jeans pressure
floor that is active \emph{below} $\nstar$ creates a discontinuity in
the effective equation of state at $n = \nstar$ if the two floors do
not match.  More fundamentally, the Jeans floor operates on
\emph{numerical} grounds (preventing artificial fragmentation) while
the polytropic floor operates on \emph{physical} grounds (modelling
the unresolved ISM), and conflating the two obscures the distinction
between numerical and physical effects.

\textbf{(iv) Masking of cooling errors.}
The pressure floor removes the consequences of cooling inaccuracies
(Section~\ref{sec:overshoot}) by overriding the computed temperature
whenever it falls below $T_{\rm floor}$.  While this prevents the
most extreme manifestation of cooling artefacts (spurious
fragmentation), it also prevents the diagnosis of the underlying
problem.  The $\Delta t$-dependent temperature biases of the N-R
solver create artificial temperature discontinuities at AMR level
boundaries that the floor cannot address, since the erroneous
temperatures typically remain above $T_{\rm floor}$.  Over many
time-steps, these hidden biases accumulate and affect the refinement
pattern and the large-scale thermodynamic state.

\textbf{(v) Incompatibility with turbulent Jeans analysis.}
If the Jeans criterion is to be extended to include turbulent pressure
support (equation~\ref{eq:jeans_turb}), the floor must also be
modified to account for $\sigma_{\rm turb}$.  But the turbulent
velocity dispersion varies in space and time, making a simple analytic
floor impossible.  The approach advocated in this paper --- \emph{resolving}
the Jeans length through AMR rather than \emph{imposing} it through a
floor --- is naturally compatible with a turbulence-dependent criterion,
since the refinement condition (equation~\ref{eq:jeans_crit}) can be
straightforwardly generalised to use $c_{\rm eff}^2 = c_s^2 +
\sigma_{\rm turb}^2$ without modifying any thermodynamic floor.

In summary, while a pressure floor provides a brute-force guarantee
that the Jeans criterion is satisfied, it does so at the cost of
altering the thermodynamic state of the gas.  Our approach ---
combining accurate cooling (Sections~\ref{sec:exact_cooling}--\ref{sec:cooling}) with
Jeans-aware refinement (Section~\ref{sec:bypass}) --- achieves the
same numerical stability without modifying the gas physics.


\subsection{Turbulence and the Effective Jeans Length}
\label{sec:turbulence}

The analysis presented in this paper treats the Jeans length as a
purely thermal quantity (equation~\ref{eq:jeans_crit}).  In reality,
turbulent motions in the ISM provide additional pressure support that
modifies the effective stability criterion.  Following
\citet{Chandrasekhar1951} and \citet{Bonazzola1987}, the turbulent
Jeans length is
\begin{equation}
  \lJ^{\rm turb} =
  \sqrt{\frac{\pi\,(c_s^2 + \sigma_{\rm turb}^2)}{G\,\rho}},
  \label{eq:jeans_turb_disc}
\end{equation}
where $\sigma_{\rm turb}$ is the turbulent velocity dispersion on the
grid scale.  In the limit $\sigma_{\rm turb} \gg c_s$, the turbulent
Jeans length can exceed the thermal Jeans length by an order of
magnitude, fundamentally changing the refinement pattern.

Subgrid-scale turbulence models \citep{Schmidt2006, Schmidt2011}
evolve a turbulent energy density $e_{\rm turb}$ alongside the
hydrodynamic variables, providing a local estimate of $\sigma_{\rm
turb} = (2 e_{\rm turb}/\rho)^{1/2}$.  \citet{Rennehan2021} applied
this approach to cosmological simulations, showing that SGS turbulence
can suppress artificial fragmentation in the circumgalactic medium.
\citet{Semenov2016} demonstrated that a turbulence-regulated star
formation prescription produces more realistic star formation
histories than a simple density-threshold model.

In galactic discs, turbulence is driven by a combination of supernova
feedback, gravitational instabilities, magnetorotational instability,
and large-scale shear \citep{Robertson2008, Kim2017, Hopkins2018}.
The relative importance of these drivers varies with environment:
supernova feedback dominates in star-forming regions, while
gravitational infall and accretion shocks drive turbulence in the CGM
and IGM \citep{Iapichino2011}.

All three requirements are now implemented in the SGS turbulence
model described in Section~\ref{sec:sgs}: the SGS energy provides a
local estimator of $\sigma_{\rm turb}$
(equation~\ref{eq:sigma_turb}), the Jeans criterion uses
$c_{\rm eff}^2 = c_s^2 + \sigma_{\rm turb}^2$
(equation~\ref{eq:jeans_sgs}), and the equilibrium analysis
(Section~\ref{sec:sgs_equilib}) shows that the cascade terminates
naturally at $N_{J,\rm eff} \approx 3.5$ cells per Jeans length.


\subsection{Can Turbulence Replace the Holdback?}
\label{sec:turb_vs_holdback}

A natural question is whether turbulent pressure support alone can
prevent the refinement cascades that the holdback mechanism was
designed to suppress, thereby rendering the holdback unnecessary.
We present both a quantitative scaling analysis and a direct
numerical test.

\textbf{Scaling analysis.}
Including turbulence, the Jeans length increases by a factor
$\lJ^{\rm turb}/\lJ = (1 + \mathcal{M}_{\rm turb}^2)^{1/2}$,
where $\mathcal{M}_{\rm turb} = \sigma_{\rm turb}/c_s$ is the
turbulent Mach number.  In terms of refinement levels, turbulence
prevents the cascade from extending by
\begin{equation}
  \Delta\ell_{\rm turb} = \tfrac{1}{2}\log_2(1 + \mathcal{M}_{\rm turb}^2)
  \label{eq:turb_levels}
\end{equation}
levels.  Table~\ref{tab:turb_holdback} compares this with the
holdback, which prevents approximately
$\Delta\ell_{\rm hb} \approx \log_2(4^{1/3}/a_{\rm exp})$ levels at
a given expansion factor.

At $z = 5$ ($a_{\rm exp} = 1/6$), the holdback suppresses
$\sim$3.8~levels of refinement.  Turbulence can match this only
for $\mathcal{M}_{\rm turb} \gtrsim 10$, which occurs exclusively
in the cold, dense ISM --- where the polytropic floor already
prevents the cascade.  In the diffuse IGM and filaments where the
holdback is most critical, typical Mach numbers are
$\mathcal{M}_{\rm turb} \sim 0.1$--$3$, providing at most 1--2
levels of protection.  Furthermore, the resolved velocity
dispersion scales as $\sigma_{\rm turb}(\ell) \propto \Delta
x^{1/3}$ for Kolmogorov turbulence, so turbulent support weakens
at finer levels, exacerbating the mismatch.

We therefore conclude that \emph{resolved turbulence alone cannot
replace the holdback}; the resolved velocity dispersion is too
weak in the IGM where the holdback is most needed.  However, the
SGS turbulence model (Section~\ref{sec:sgs}) circumvents this
limitation by construction: the equilibrium SGS dispersion
$\sigma_{\rm eq}^2 \propto \dx^2$ (equation~\ref{eq:sigma_eq})
provides support at every scale, including coarse cells in the
IGM, because the gravitational production term injects energy
wherever the local free-fall time is short compared to the
dissipation time.  The SGS model and the holdback are therefore
complementary: the SGS provides physically motivated cascade
termination, while the holdback remains available as a safety
net for regimes where the SGS model has not yet reached
equilibrium.

\subsection{Holdback as a Workaround for Cooling Errors}
\label{sec:holdback_cooling}

The analysis above assumes that all Jeans-triggered refinements at
high redshift are physically motivated.  However, as demonstrated
in Section~\ref{sec:overshoot}, the standard Newton--Raphson
cooling solver produces \emph{time-step-dependent} temperatures:
cells at different AMR levels receive different $\Delta t$ and
therefore converge to different temperatures even under identical
physical conditions.  These artificial temperature discontinuities
at level boundaries create spurious Jeans-length gradients that
trigger refinement where no physical density enhancement exists.
Similarly, a single-redshift cooling table applied at the wrong
epoch can shift $T_{\rm eq}$ by factors of $\sim$2
(Section~\ref{sec:multi_z}).  Together, these numerical artefacts
generate \emph{spurious} Jeans triggers that dominate the
refinement budget at high~$z$.

With a correct cooling implementation --- the Townsend exact
integrator providing $\Delta t$-independent temperatures, and
multi-redshift table interpolation providing the appropriate UV
background at each epoch --- all cells at the same physical
conditions converge to the same temperature regardless of their
AMR level.  The artificial temperature discontinuities at level
boundaries vanish, eliminating spurious Jeans triggers.  Most
diffuse gas at high redshift remains in photoionisation equilibrium
at $T \approx 10^4$\,K.  The Jeans length in this gas is large
($\lJ \sim 80$\,kpc at $n_{\rm H} = 0.01\,{\rm cm}^{-3}$), well
above the refinement threshold, and the Jeans criterion does not
trigger.  Only genuinely self-shielded, collapsing gas triggers
refinement --- which is the physically correct behaviour.

\textbf{Numerical test.}
We test this hypothesis directly by running two cosmological
simulations from $z = 50$ to $z \approx 9$, restarting from
identical initial conditions with the Jeans criterion active
($N_J = 4$ at all levels):
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Holdback ON + exact cooling}: standard configuration
    with the Townsend integrator and multi-$z$ tables;
  \item \textbf{Holdback OFF + exact cooling}: identical, but with
    \code{q\_refine\_holdback = .false.}
\end{enumerate}
Both runs use a $512^3$ base grid ($\ell_{\rm min}=9$,
$\ell_{\rm max}=17$) with 12~MPI ranks.
Table~\ref{tab:holdback_test} summarises the results after one
coarse time-step at $z \approx 9$.

holdback mechanism has historically been compensating for the

motivated refinement.  With a $\Delta t$-independent cooling
implementation, the artificial temperature discontinuities at level
boundaries vanish, and the holdback is no longer necessary for
\emph{stability}; its remaining role is purely one of
\emph{computational cost control}.


%======================================================================
\section{Conclusions}
\label{sec:conclusions}
%======================================================================

We have presented three improvements to the handling of the Jeans
criterion in cosmological AMR simulations, implemented in the
\curamses\ code:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Improved cooling module.}  The Eunha cooling module
    replaces the standard \ramses\ Newton--Raphson solver with the
    \citet{Townsend2009} exact integration scheme applied to 3D Grackle
    equilibrium cooling tables \citep{Smith2017}.  The exact integrator
    is genuinely time-step independent
    (Section~\ref{sec:exact_cooling}), eliminating the artificial
    temperature discontinuities at AMR level boundaries that arise from
    the $\Delta t$-dependent N-R solver
    (Section~\ref{sec:overshoot}).  It also provides
    density-dependent metallicity and non-LTE molecular weights.
    Multi-redshift table interpolation tracks the evolving UV
    background \citep{HM2012} across cosmic time, eliminating the
    systematic bias of fixed-$z$ tables.

  \item \textbf{Jeans-independent holdback bypass.}  The
    \code{jeans\_bypass\_holdback} option allows the Jeans criterion to
    operate ahead of the cosmological holdback, preventing artificial
    fragmentation and smoothing the star formation rate transition when
    levels are released.  A configurable level limit
    (\code{n\_jeans\_bypass\_levels}) and a physics-based density
    threshold (\code{d\_jeans\_thre}) provide complementary safeguards
    against runaway refinement cascades.

  \item \textbf{Quantitative analysis.}  We have shown that differences
    between the \ramses\ and Grackle cooling rates propagate into
    40--80\,per\,cent errors in the Jeans length at low densities, and
    that the $\Delta t$-dependent N-R solver introduces level-dependent
    temperature biases that drive spurious refinement at AMR level
    boundaries.  The exact integrator eliminates this entirely.

  \item \textbf{Critical polytropic index.}  The Jeans length in the
    polytropic regime scales as $\lJ \propto \rho^{(\gstar-2)/2}$.
    For $\gstar \ge 2$, $\lJ$ is non-decreasing with density and the
    Jeans criterion self-terminates without any auxiliary mechanisms.
    However, increasing $\gstar$ from $5/3$ to $2$ raises the
    temperature floor at the star-formation threshold by a factor
    of $\sim$4 (Table~\ref{tab:gstar}), necessitating a full
    recalibration of the star-formation parameters.  The combination
    of $\gstar = 5/3$ with \code{d\_jeans\_thre}$\,\approx\nstar$
    provides an effective alternative that preserves the existing
    calibration while limiting the cascade to 1--2 extra levels
    (Section~\ref{sec:polytropic}).

  \item \textbf{Holdback diagnosis.}  A direct numerical test at
    $z \approx 9$ demonstrates that with accurate,
    $\Delta t$-independent cooling, the cosmological holdback can be
    disabled without triggering a refinement cascade
    (Section~\ref{sec:holdback_cooling}).  The holdback has
    historically been compensating for the level-dependent temperature
    artefacts of the N-R solver; with the exact integrator and
    multi-$z$ tables, its role reduces to computational cost control.
    Turbulent pressure support, while physically important, cannot
    replace the holdback in the diffuse regime where it is most needed
    (Section~\ref{sec:turb_vs_holdback}).
\end{enumerate}

Future work will extend the Jeans criterion to include turbulent
pressure support (equation~\ref{eq:jeans_turb_disc}), using
subgrid-scale turbulence models to estimate the unresolved velocity
dispersion on the grid scale.  A full star formation rate comparison
from $z = 50$ to $z = 1$ between the standard cooling with holdback
and the exact cooling without holdback is in progress.  This
turbulence-dependent refinement criterion, combined with the
improved cooling module and holdback bypass presented here, will
provide a comprehensive framework for accurate Jeans length
resolution in next-generation cosmological simulations.
