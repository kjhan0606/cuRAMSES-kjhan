% ABSTRACT
% ===================================================================
\begin{abstract}
We present extensions to the \curamses\ adaptive mesh refinement (AMR)
code that enable cosmological hydrodynamic simulations with eleven
classes of non-standard physics beyond $\Lambda$CDM.  The first group
operates within the Poisson solver framework: (i)~CPL dark energy with
dynamical equation of state $w(a)=w_0+w_a(1-a)$ and density
perturbations via a table-based linear response method for arbitrary
sound speed $\cst$; (ii)~massive neutrino linear response using
precomputed \camb\ transfer function ratios $\Rnu(k,a)$.  The second
group addresses dark matter phenomenology: (iii)~self-interacting dark
matter (SIDM) Monte Carlo scattering with constant,
velocity-dependent, anisotropic, and inelastic cross-sections;
(iv)~Modified Newtonian Dynamics in QUMOND and AQUAL formulations with
external field effect (EFE) support.  The third group implements
scalar field modified gravity theories via Newton--Gauss--Seidel
relaxation on AMR levels: (v)~$\fR$~Hu--Sawicki gravity with
chameleon screening; (vi)~nDGP braneworld gravity with Vainshtein
screening; (vii)~Symmetron gravity with spontaneous symmetry breaking;
(viii)~environmentally dependent Dilaton gravity; (ix)~cubic Galileon
gravity reusing the Vainshtein operator.  Finally, two additional
modules modify forces and background cosmology: (x)~Coupled Dark
Energy with an enhanced effective gravitational constant
$\Geff = G(1+2\betacde^2)$ for dark matter; and (xi)~Early Dark
Energy in the Doran--Robbers parameterisation, which modifies the
Friedmann equation at high redshift.  All scalar field models (f(R),
nDGP, MOND, Symmetron, Dilaton, Galileon) are mutually exclusive,
while coupled DE and early DE can combine freely with any model.  Each
module is controlled by dedicated namelist blocks and is verified to
recover $\Lambda$CDM results when disabled.  We describe the
algorithms, screening mechanisms, unit conversions, and numerical
validation, providing a self-contained reference for non-standard
cosmological simulations with AMR codes.
\end{abstract}

\section{INTRODUCTION}
\label{sec:intro}

The $\Lambda$CDM concordance model, with a cosmological constant
$\Lambda$ and cold, collisionless dark matter, provides an excellent
fit to a wide range of cosmological observations
\citep{PlanckCollaboration2020}.  Yet several fundamental questions
remain open.  The nature of the accelerated expansion is unknown: the
cosmological constant may be replaced by a dynamical dark energy field
whose equation of state evolves with redshift
\citep{Chevallier2001,Linder2003}.  Neutrino oscillation experiments
have established non-zero neutrino masses
\citep{Lesgourgues2006,Lattanzi2017}, which suppress the growth of
structure on small scales.  On the dark matter side, persistent
small-scale tensions --- the core--cusp, missing satellites, and
too-big-to-fail problems --- have motivated self-interacting dark
matter (SIDM) models in which DM particles scatter elastically or
inelastically with non-negligible cross-sections
\citep{Spergel2000,Tulin2018}.  An alternative explanation of these
small-scale phenomena, as well as of galaxy rotation curves without
dark matter, is provided by Modified Newtonian Dynamics (MOND;
\citealt{Milgrom1983}), in which the gravitational acceleration is
enhanced below a critical threshold $\azero \approx 1.2 \times
10^{-8}\,\mathrm{cm\,s^{-2}}$.  The quasi-linear formulation of MOND
(QUMOND; \citealt{Milgrom2010}) recasts this modification as a
modified Poisson equation that can be solved with standard numerical
methods.

Beyond these phenomenological approaches, a rich landscape of
gravitational theories has emerged that modify general relativity (GR)
on cosmological scales.  $\fR$ gravity
\citep{HuSawicki2007,Sotiriou2010} introduces a scalar degree of
freedom through a nonlinear function of the Ricci scalar, with the
Hu--Sawicki model providing a chameleon screening mechanism that
recovers GR in high-density environments.  The Dvali--Gabadadze--Porrati
(DGP) braneworld model \citep{DGP2000} explains cosmic acceleration
through gravitational leakage into an extra dimension; the normal
branch (nDGP) exhibits Vainshtein screening
\citep{Vainshtein1972,Nicolis2009}, whereby nonlinear derivative
interactions suppress the scalar force near massive sources.  The
Symmetron mechanism \citep{Hinterbichler2010,Hinterbichler2011}
employs a scalar field with a spontaneous symmetry-breaking potential
whose vacuum expectation value (VEV) vanishes in high-density regions,
effectively decoupling the fifth force.  The environmentally dependent
Dilaton model \citep{Brax2010,Brax2011} achieves a similar screening
via the Damour--Polyakov mechanism in which the coupling function has
a minimum at the cosmological field value.  Cubic Galileon gravity
\citep{Nicolis2009,Deffayet2009a,Deffayet2009b} features
shift-symmetric scalar field interactions with second-order equations
of motion, screened by the Vainshtein mechanism.

Several $N$-body codes have implemented subsets of these models,
notably \textsc{ecosmog} \citep{Li2012,Li2013}, \textsc{isis}
\citep{Llinares2014}, \textsc{mg-gadget} \citep{Puchwein2013}, and
the \ramses-based solver of \citet{Llinares2014}.  The
Newton--Gauss--Seidel (N-GS) relaxation method with red--black
ordering has proven robust for the nonlinear scalar field equations
arising in all these theories \citep{Li2012,Llinares2014}.

Beyond modifications to gravity, the dark energy sector itself may
couple directly to dark matter.  Coupled dark energy (CDE) models
\citep{Amendola2000,Amendola2004,Pettorino2008} introduce an
interaction term in the dark sector Lagrangian, yielding an effective
gravitational constant $\Geff = G(1+2\betacde^2)$ for DM particles.
Independently, the coincidence problem and Hubble tension have
motivated Early Dark Energy (EDE) models
\citep{DoranRobbers2006,Poulin2019,Hill2020,Smith2020} in which a
non-negligible dark energy component exists at early times
($z \sim 3000$) before decaying rapidly, modifying the expansion
history and sound horizon.

Precision cosmological surveys such as DESI \citep{DESI2024}, Euclid
\citep{EuclidCollaboration2024}, and the Vera C.\ Rubin Observatory
LSST \citep{Ivezic2019} will constrain these extensions at the
per-cent level, demanding equally precise theoretical predictions.
Full hydrodynamic simulations that simultaneously capture baryonic
physics and non-standard dark-sector models are essential for this
programme.

In this paper we describe the implementation of eleven non-standard
cosmological modules in \curamses\ \citep{Kim2026}, a suite of
algorithmic and GPU-acceleration optimizations for the \ramses\ AMR
code \citep{Teyssier2002}:

\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{CPL dark energy perturbations}
  (Section~\ref{sec:de}): a table-based linear response method for
  the Chevallier--Poloncki--Linder (CPL) parameterisation.

\item \textbf{Massive neutrino linear response}
  (Section~\ref{sec:neutrino}): scale-dependent Poisson equation
  corrections using precomputed \camb\ \citep{Lewis2000} transfer
  function ratios.

\item \textbf{Self-interacting dark matter}
  (Section~\ref{sec:sidm}): cell-based Monte Carlo scattering
  with constant, velocity-dependent, anisotropic, and inelastic
  cross-sections.

\item \textbf{QUMOND and AQUAL} (Section~\ref{sec:mond}):
  Modified Newtonian Dynamics with three operational modes and
  external field effect support.

\item \textbf{$\fR$ Hu--Sawicki gravity}
  (Section~\ref{sec:fR}): chameleon-screened scalar field solved
  via Newton--Gauss--Seidel relaxation.

\item \textbf{nDGP braneworld gravity}
  (Section~\ref{sec:nDGP}): Vainshtein-screened scalar field with
  the nonlinear Laplacian operator.

\item \textbf{Symmetron gravity}
  (Section~\ref{sec:symmetron}): spontaneous symmetry-breaking
  screening with environment-dependent VEV.

\item \textbf{Dilaton gravity}
  (Section~\ref{sec:dilaton}): Damour--Polyakov environmentally
  dependent coupling.

\item \textbf{Cubic Galileon gravity}
  (Section~\ref{sec:galileon}): shift-symmetric scalar field with
  Vainshtein screening, reusing the nDGP operator.

\item \textbf{Coupled dark energy}
  (Section~\ref{sec:coupled_de}): enhanced gravitational constant
  $\Geff = G(1+2\betacde^2)$ for dark matter.

\item \textbf{Early dark energy}
  (Section~\ref{sec:ede}): Doran--Robbers parameterisation
  modifying the background Friedmann equation.
\end{enumerate}

All scalar field models (items 4--9) share a common infrastructure:
the \code{scalar\_gr} array for the field value and the N-GS
red--black relaxation solver with OpenMP parallelism.  Exactly one
scalar field model may be active at a time, while coupled DE and
early DE combine freely with any other model.  All modules compose
with the existing \curamses\ infrastructure --- $k$-section domain
decomposition, Morton key octree, FFTW3 direct Poisson solver, and
OpenMP+MPI hybrid parallelism --- without requiring modifications to
the core AMR data structures.

\section{UNIFIED POISSON SOLVER FRAMEWORK}
\label{sec:poisson_framework}

The cosmological Poisson equation in comoving coordinates reads
\begin{equation}
\nabla^2 \phi = \frac{3}{2}\,\Om\,a\,\delta_{\rm m},
\label{eq:poisson_standard}
\end{equation}
where $\phi$ is the gravitational potential, $a$ is the scale factor,
$\Om$ is the total matter density parameter, and $\delta_{\rm m}$ is
the total matter overdensity.

In the standard $\Lambda$CDM case, all matter is treated as cold and
collisionless: $\delta_{\rm m} = \delta_{\rm cb}$ (CDM + baryons).
When massive neutrinos or clustering dark energy are present, the
Poisson source must include their density perturbations:
\begin{equation}
\nabla^2 \phi = \frac{3}{2}\,a\left[
  \Ocb\,\delta_{\rm cb}
  + \Onu\,\delta_\nu
  + \Ode(a)\,\delta_{\rm DE}
\right],
\label{eq:poisson_full}
\end{equation}
where $\Ocb = \Om - \Onu$, and $\Ode(a) = \Ol\,f_{\rm DE}(a)\,a^3$
is the physical dark energy density in units of the critical density
at the current epoch.

Rather than evolving $\delta_\nu$ and $\delta_{\rm DE}$ as additional
fluid variables, we adopt the \emph{linear response} approach: each
non-standard perturbation is expressed as a scale-dependent multiple of
the CDM+baryon perturbation,
\begin{equation}
\delta_X(k,a) = R_X(k,a)\,\delta_{\rm cb}(k,a),
\label{eq:linear_response}
\end{equation}
where $X \in \{\nu, {\rm DE}\}$ and the response function $R_X(k,a)$
is precomputed from \camb\ transfer functions.  Substituting into
equation~(\ref{eq:poisson_full}) and transforming to Fourier space,
the modified Green's function becomes
\begin{equation}
\hat{\phi}(\mathbf{k}) = G(k)\,\hat{\delta}_{\rm cb}(\mathbf{k})
  \times \underbrace{\left[1 + \frac{\Onu}{\Ocb}\,\Rnu(k,a)\right]}_{\text{neutrino factor}}
  \times \underbrace{\left[1 + \frac{\Ode(a)}{\Ocb}\,\Rde(k,a)\right]}_{\text{DE factor}},
\label{eq:green_unified}
\end{equation}
where $G(k) = -1/k^2$ is the standard Laplacian Green's function
(with appropriate normalisation).  Each correction factor defaults to
unity when the corresponding module is disabled, ensuring bit-wise
recovery of $\Lambda$CDM.

\curamses\ employs a two-level Poisson solver \citep{Guillet2011,Kim2026}:
a multigrid (MG) V-cycle for AMR fine levels and an FFT direct solve
for the uniform base level.  The $k$-dependent corrections in
equation~(\ref{eq:green_unified}) are applied mode-by-mode in the FFT
solver, where Fourier modes are explicitly available.  For the MG
solver, which operates in real space, only $k$-independent corrections
can be applied; these enter through the pre-factor
\begin{equation}
4\pi G_{\rm eff} = \frac{3}{2}\,\Ocb\,a\,\mathcal{S},
\label{eq:fourpi}
\end{equation}
where $\mathcal{S} = 1$ in standard $\Lambda$CDM.  Table~\ref{tab:solver_summary}
summarises how each module modifies the two solver levels.

\section{CPL DARK ENERGY WITH PERTURBATIONS}
\label{sec:de}

\subsection{Background Evolution}
\label{sec:de_background}

The CPL parameterisation \citep{Chevallier2001,Linder2003} models the
dark energy equation of state as
\begin{equation}
w(a) = w_0 + w_a\,(1 - a),
\label{eq:cpl}
\end{equation}
where $w_0$ is the present-day value and $w_a$ captures the
time variation.  The dark energy density evolves as
\begin{equation}
\frac{\rho_{\rm DE}(a)}{\rho_{\rm DE}(1)}
  = a^{-3(1+w_0+w_a)}\,\exp\!\left[-3w_a\,(1-a)\right]
  \equiv f_{\rm DE}(a).
\label{eq:fde}
\end{equation}
For $\Lambda$CDM ($w_0=-1$, $w_a=0$), $f_{\rm DE}(a) = 1$ identically.
The Friedmann equation becomes
\begin{equation}
E^2(a) \equiv \frac{H^2(a)}{H_0^2}
  = \frac{\Om}{a^3} + \frac{\Omega_k}{a^2} + \Ol\,f_{\rm DE}(a),
\label{eq:friedmann}
\end{equation}
which governs the time-stepping and scale factor evolution in
\curamses.

\subsection{Table-Based Linear Response}
\label{sec:de_table}

For arbitrary $\cst$, including the important clustering limit
$\cst \to 0$, the DE perturbation $\delta_{\rm DE}$ cannot be
obtained analytically from $\delta_{\rm cb}$ in a simple closed form.
We therefore precompute an effective response ratio $\Rde(k,a)$ from
\camb\ \citep{Lewis2000} via the \emph{Weyl potential method} and
store it in an ASCII table for use at runtime.

A na\"{\i}ve extraction of $\delta_{\rm DE}/\delta_{\rm m}$ from
\camb\ transfer functions is problematic because \camb\ works in the
\emph{synchronous} gauge, whereas the $N$-body code solves the
Newtonian-gauge Poisson equation $\nabla^2\phi = 4\pi G\,a^2\,
\bar\rho\,\delta$.  At low redshift, the synchronous-gauge
$\delta_{\rm DE}$ can exceed its Newtonian-gauge counterpart by a
factor ${\sim}26$ for $\cst = 0$, leading to a severe
over-correction of the gravitational potential.

Instead, we define an effective response function
\begin{equation}
\Rde(k,a) \equiv \left[
  \frac{\Psi_{\rm W}^{(\rm clust)}(k,a) / \delta_{\rm m}^{(\rm clust)}}
       {\Psi_{\rm W}^{(\rm smooth)}(k,a) / \delta_{\rm m}^{(\rm smooth)}}
  - 1
\right]
\frac{\Om}{\Ode(a)\,a^3}
\label{eq:rde_def}
\end{equation}
where $\Psi_{\rm W}$ is the gauge-invariant Weyl potential returned by
\camb, and the superscripts `clust' and `smooth' refer to two \camb\
runs with $\cst = c_{\rm s,target}^2$ and $\cst = 1$ (non-clustering
DE), respectively.  This ratio isolates the extra gravitational
potential sourced by DE clustering, automatically in the correct
(Newtonian) gauge.

The table generation proceeds as follows.  We run \camb\ twice with
the same CPL parameters $(w_0, w_a)$ but different sound speeds:
the target $\cst$ and a reference $\cst=1$.  For each of 50 scale
factor values $a_i$ (logarithmically spaced from $a=0.01$ to $a=1$)
and 200 wavenumbers $k_j$ (logarithmically spaced from $k=10^{-4}$
to $10\,h\,\mathrm{Mpc}^{-1}$), we extract the Weyl potential and
\begin{equation}
\delta_{\rm m}(k,a) = \frac{\Omega_{\rm cdm}\,\delta_{\rm cdm}
  + \Omega_{\rm b}\,\delta_{\rm b}}{\Om}
\label{eq:delta_m}
\end{equation}
from both runs, compute $\Rde$ via equation~(\ref{eq:rde_def}), and
store $\Rde(k_j, a_i)$ in an ASCII table.

At runtime, \curamses\ reads this table once at the first Poisson
solve.  For each Fourier mode with physical wavenumber
\begin{equation}
k_{\rm phys} = \frac{2\pi}{L_{\rm box}}\,
\sqrt{k_x^2 + k_y^2 + k_z^2}
\quad [h\,\mathrm{Mpc}^{-1}],
\label{eq:kphys}
\end{equation}
the response function $\Rde(k_{\rm phys}, a)$ is obtained by bilinear
interpolation in $(\log_{10}k,\,\log_{10}a)$ space.  The DE correction
factor in equation~(\ref{eq:green_unified}) is then
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{F}_{\rm DE}(k,a)
  = 1 + \frac{\Ode(a)}{\Ocb}\,\Rde(k,a),
\label{eq:de_factor}
\end{equation}
where $\Ode(a) = \Ol\,f_{\rm DE}(a)\,a^3$.

For the important case $\cst = 0$, dark energy clusters at all scales
identically: $\Rde$ is independent of $k$.  This allows the MG solver
to also capture the DE correction via a real-space boost to the
$4\pi G_{\rm eff}$ pre-factor (equation~\ref{eq:fourpi}):
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{S} = 1 + \frac{\Ode(a)}{\Ocb}\,\Rde(k_{\rm ref},a),
\label{eq:fourpi_boost}
\end{equation}
evaluated at $k_{\rm ref} = 10^{-3}\,h\,\mathrm{Mpc}^{-1}$ (any
value suffices since $\Rde$ is $k$-independent).

\subsection{Quasi-Static Helmholtz Fallback}
\label{sec:de_helmholtz}

When no \camb\ table is provided and $\cst > 0$, a quasi-static
approximation is available.  In the sub-horizon, quasi-static limit,
the DE perturbation satisfies a Helmholtz equation that can be
combined with Poisson's equation to yield a modified Green's function
\citep{Bean2004,Sapone2010}:
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{F}_{\rm DE}(k)
  = \frac{\tilde{k}^2 + \kappa^2}
         {\tilde{k}^2 + \kappa^2 + \alpha},
\label{eq:helmholtz_factor}
\end{equation}
where $\tilde{k} = 2\pi\sqrt{k_x^2+k_y^2+k_z^2}$ is the
dimensionless Fourier wavenumber, and
\begin{align}
\kappa^2 &= \frac{a^2\,E^2(a)}{c_{\rm s}^2}
  \left(\frac{L_{\rm box}}{c/H_0}\right)^{\!2},
\label{eq:kappa2}\\
\alpha &= \frac{3}{2}\,\Ol\,f_{\rm DE}(a)\,
  \frac{1+w(a)}{c_{\rm s}^2}
  \left(\frac{L_{\rm box}}{c/H_0}\right)^{\!2}.
\label{eq:alpha}
\end{align}
For $\Lambda$CDM, $1+w=0$ so $\alpha=0$ and $\mathcal{F}_{\rm DE}=1$.
This approach is valid only for $\cst > 0$; for $\cst \to 0$, both
$\kappa^2$ and $\alpha$ diverge, and the table-based method must be
used instead.

\subsection{Namelist Configuration}
\label{sec:de_namelist}

The CPL dark energy module is activated by setting \code{de\_perturb=.true.}
in \code{\&RUN\_PARAMS}.  The equation of state and sound speed are
specified in a dedicated \code{\&CPL\_PARAMS} namelist:
\begin{verbatim}
&CPL_PARAMS
w0 = -0.9
wa = 0.1
cs2_de = 0.0
de_table = 'de_table.dat'
/
\end{verbatim}
The code selects the solver path automatically: table-based if
\code{de\_table} is provided, quasi-static Helmholtz otherwise
(with $\cst > 0$ required).

\section{MASSIVE NEUTRINO LINEAR RESPONSE}
\label{sec:neutrino}

\subsection{Physical Motivation}
\label{sec:nu_motivation}

Massive neutrinos with total mass $\sum m_\nu > 0$ free-stream out of
gravitational potential wells on scales below the neutrino free-streaming
length, suppressing the matter power spectrum by a fraction
$\Delta P/P \approx -8\,\Onu/\Om$ on small scales
\citep{Lesgourgues2006}.  Current constraints from CMB and
large-scale structure data give $\sum m_\nu < 0.12$\,eV at 95\,per\,cent
confidence \citep{PlanckCollaboration2020}, corresponding to
$\Onu \lesssim 0.003$.

The standard approach of representing neutrinos as $N$-body
particles \citep{Brandbyge2008,Viel2010} suffers from severe shot
noise: the high thermal velocities of neutrinos make them
nearly homogeneous, so resolving the small perturbations
$\delta_\nu \ll 1$ requires an impractically large number of
particles.  The linear response method \citep{AliHaimoud2013,Bird2018}
avoids this problem entirely by computing $\delta_\nu$ analytically
from the CDM+baryon perturbation.

\subsection{Transfer Function Table}
\label{sec:nu_table}

We compute the neutrino response function
\begin{equation}
\Rnu(k,a) = \frac{T_\nu(k,a)}{T_{\rm cb}(k,a)}
\label{eq:rnu_def}
\end{equation}
using \camb, where $T_\nu$ and $T_{\rm cb}$ are the neutrino and
CDM+baryon transfer functions, respectively.  The table spans
200 wavenumbers ($10^{-4}$--$10\,h\,\mathrm{Mpc}^{-1}$, log-spaced)
and 50 scale factor values ($0.01$--$1.0$, log-spaced), and is
generated by a Python script \code{generate\_neutrino\_table.py}
included with \curamses.

The default cosmology uses Planck 2018 parameters
\citep{PlanckCollaboration2020}: $\Omega_{\rm b}=0.0486$,
$\Om=0.3089$, $h=0.6774$, $\sum m_\nu=0.06$\,eV (one massive
eigenstate).  For three degenerate neutrinos with $\sum m_\nu=0.06$\,eV,
the density parameter is $\Onu \approx 0.00140$.  At the table
boundaries, $\Rnu$ ranges from $-0.009$ to $1.332$: neutrinos
trace CDM perturbations on large scales ($\Rnu \to 1$) and are
suppressed below the free-streaming scale ($\Rnu \to 0$).

\subsection{Poisson Solver Integration}
\label{sec:nu_poisson}

\subsubsection{FFT base-level solver}

At the base level, where the grid is uniform and an FFT direct solve
is performed, the neutrino correction is applied mode-by-mode after
the forward real-to-complex FFT:
\begin{equation}
\hat{\phi}(\mathbf{k}) = G(k)\,\hat{\delta}_{\rm cb}(\mathbf{k})
  \times \left[1 + \frac{\Onu}{\Ocb}\,\Rnu(k_{\rm phys},a)\right].
\label{eq:nu_fft}
\end{equation}
The physical wavenumber $k_{\rm phys}$ is computed via
equation~(\ref{eq:kphys}).  This correction is applied in both the
distributed (FFTW MPI slab decomposition) and local
(\code{MPI\_ALLREDUCE} + serial FFTW) code paths.

\subsubsection{Multigrid fine-level solver}

The MG solver operates in real space and cannot apply $k$-dependent
corrections directly.  Instead, the Poisson source pre-factor is
modified from $\Om$ to $\Ocb$:
\begin{equation}
4\pi G_{\rm eff} = \frac{3}{2}\,\Ocb\,a\,\mathrm{scale},
\label{eq:fourpi_nu}
\end{equation}
where $\mathrm{scale}$ is the code-to-physical unit conversion.  This
correctly accounts for the fact that only CDM+baryon matter contributes
to the real-space source term; the neutrino contribution, being
$k$-dependent, is captured exclusively through the FFT base-level
correction.  The approximation is valid because neutrino perturbations
are small ($\delta_\nu \ll \delta_{\rm cb}$ on AMR-refined scales) and
the base-level FFT captures the dominant large-scale neutrino effect.

\subsection{Namelist Configuration}

The neutrino module requires \code{use\_neutrino=.true.} in
\code{\&RUN\_PARAMS}, plus a \code{\&NEUTRINO\_PARAMS} block:
\begin{verbatim}
&NEUTRINO_PARAMS
omega_nu = 0.00140
neutrino_table = 'neutrino_table.dat'
/
\end{verbatim}
At startup, the code validates that $\Onu > 0$ and the table file
exists; otherwise the module is disabled with a warning.

\subsection{Consistency with Dark Energy Corrections}
\label{sec:nu_de_compose}

When both neutrino and dark energy modules are active, the correction
factors compose multiplicatively in equation~(\ref{eq:green_unified}).
Each factor is independent and defaults to unity when disabled.  This
design ensures that turning on one module does not interfere with the
other, and the combined effect is the correct linear-order
modification of the gravitational potential.

\section{SELF-INTERACTING DARK MATTER}
\label{sec:sidm}

\subsection{Physical Motivation}
\label{sec:sidm_motivation}

In the CDM paradigm, dark matter is collisionless.
Self-interacting dark matter (SIDM) models introduce a non-zero
scattering cross-section $\sigmam$ that allows DM particles to
exchange momentum and energy, potentially resolving small-scale
tensions with observations \citep{Spergel2000,Tulin2018}.

Astrophysical constraints span several orders of magnitude in
relative velocity: dwarf galaxies probe $v \sim 30$--$100\kms$,
Milky Way-mass haloes $v \sim 200\kms$, and galaxy clusters
$v \sim 1000$--$3000\kms$.  A constant cross-section
$\sigmam \sim 1\,\mathrm{cm^2\,g^{-1}}$ is consistent with
dwarf-scale observations but may be too large for clusters
\citep{Kaplinghat2016}.  Velocity-dependent cross-sections, motivated
by light-mediator models (e.g.\ dark photon exchange), naturally
accommodate these constraints
\citep{Feng2009,Tulin2013,Kaplinghat2016}.

\subsection{Monte Carlo Scattering Algorithm}
\label{sec:sidm_algorithm}

We implement a cell-based Monte Carlo scattering algorithm following
the standard approach used in SPH/mesh codes
\citep{Rocha2013,Robertson2017,Banerjee2020}.  The algorithm operates
on each AMR leaf cell independently, processing all levels from
\code{levelmin} to \code{nlevelmax}.

\subsubsection{Particle collection and pairing}

For each leaf cell (identified by \code{son(ind\_cell)==0}), we
collect all DM particles by traversing the particle linked list
(\code{headp} $\to$ \code{nextp}) and selecting particles with
$\code{idp}>0$ and $\code{tp}\le 0$ (the latter condition includes
both ground-state and excited-state DM in the inelastic model;
see Section~\ref{sec:sidm_inelastic}).  If the number of DM particles
$N_{\rm dm} < N_{\rm min}$ (default 2), the cell is skipped.

The collected particles are randomly shuffled using the Fisher--Yates
algorithm \citep{Knuth1997}, which produces a uniformly random
permutation in $\mathcal{O}(N_{\rm dm})$ operations.  Sequential
pairs are then formed: $(1,2),\,(3,4),\,\ldots$, yielding
$\lfloor N_{\rm dm}/2 \rfloor$ pairs.  If $N_{\rm dm}$ is odd, the
last particle is unpaired and does not scatter.

\subsubsection{Scattering probability}

For each pair $(i,j)$ with masses $m_i$, $m_j$ and velocity vectors
$\mathbf{v}_i$, $\mathbf{v}_j$, the scattering probability is
\begin{equation}
P = \frac{\sigmam(v)\,\bar{m}\,\vrel\,\Delta t}
         {V_{\rm cell}}\,(N_{\rm dm} - 1),
\label{eq:scatter_prob}
\end{equation}
where $\vrel = |\mathbf{v}_i - \mathbf{v}_j|$ is the physical
relative velocity,
$\bar{m} = (m_i + m_j)/2$ is the mean particle mass (in physical
units), $\Delta t$ is the physical timestep, and
$V_{\rm cell} = (\Delta x\,\ell/a)^3$ is the physical cell volume
with $\ell$ the code length unit and $a$ the scale factor.  The factor
$(N_{\rm dm}-1)$ corrects for the fact that each particle can scatter
with any of the other $N_{\rm dm}-1$ particles, not just its assigned
partner.

A scatter event occurs if a uniform random deviate $\mathcal{U}(0,1)
< P$.  If $P > 1$, the probability is saturated and a warning is
issued, indicating that the timestep is too large for the local
conditions.

The unit conversions are:
\begin{align}
\bar{m}_{\rm phys} &= \code{mp}(i)\,\rho_0\,\ell^3, \label{eq:mass_phys}\\
\vrel^{\rm phys} &= \vrel^{\rm code}\,v_0/a, \label{eq:vrel_phys}\\
V_{\rm cell}^{\rm phys} &= (\Delta x\,\ell/a)^3, \label{eq:vcell_phys}\\
\Delta t_{\rm phys} &= \code{dtnew}\,t_0, \label{eq:dt_phys}
\end{align}
where $(\ell, t_0, \rho_0, v_0)$ are the \ramses\ code-to-CGS
conversion factors obtained from the \code{units()} subroutine.

\subsubsection{Velocity update}

When a scatter event occurs, the post-scattering velocities are
computed in the centre-of-mass frame.  The COM velocity is
\begin{equation}
\vcm = \frac{m_i\,\mathbf{v}_i + m_j\,\mathbf{v}_j}{m_i + m_j},
\label{eq:vcm}
\end{equation}
and a new relative velocity direction $\hat{\mathbf{n}}$ is drawn
(see Sections~\ref{sec:sidm_isotropic} and \ref{sec:sidm_anisotropic}
for the angular distribution).  The new velocities are
\begin{align}
\mathbf{v}_i' &= \vcm + \frac{m_j}{m_i+m_j}\,|\vrel'|\,\hat{\mathbf{n}},
\label{eq:vi_new}\\
\mathbf{v}_j' &= \vcm - \frac{m_i}{m_i+m_j}\,|\vrel'|\,\hat{\mathbf{n}},
\label{eq:vj_new}
\end{align}
where $|\vrel'| = |\vrel|$ for elastic scattering.  This
construction guarantees \emph{exact} momentum conservation:
$m_i\mathbf{v}_i'+m_j\mathbf{v}_j' = m_i\mathbf{v}_i+m_j\mathbf{v}_j$
by construction, and exact kinetic energy conservation for equal-mass
elastic scattering.  The velocities are updated in code units by
dividing by $v_0/a$ after the COM-frame calculation.

\subsection{Velocity-Dependent Cross-Sections}
\label{sec:sidm_sigma}

Three cross-section models are supported, selected by the
\code{sidm\_type} parameter:

\subsubsection{Constant}
\begin{equation}
\frac{\sigma}{m} = \sigma_0,
\label{eq:sigma_const}
\end{equation}
where $\sigma_0 \equiv$ \code{sidm\_cross\_section} in units of
$\mathrm{cm^2\,g^{-1}}$.

\subsubsection{Yukawa}
Motivated by Yukawa-type dark force models with a light mediator of
mass $m_\phi$ \citep{Feng2009,Tulin2013}, the transfer cross-section
in the Born regime scales as
\begin{equation}
\frac{\sigma(v)}{m} = \frac{\sigma_0}{\left[1 + (v/v_0)^2\right]^2},
\label{eq:sigma_yukawa}
\end{equation}
where $v_0$ is a reference velocity related to $m_\phi$.  This
produces strong scattering at low velocities (dwarf haloes) and
rapid suppression at high velocities (clusters), in agreement with
observational constraints \citep{Kaplinghat2016}.

\subsubsection{Power-law}
A generic power-law dependence:
\begin{equation}
\frac{\sigma(v)}{m} = \sigma_0\left(\frac{v}{v_0}\right)^n,
\label{eq:sigma_power}
\end{equation}
with a floor at $v = 1\kms$ and a cap at $10^4\,\sigma_0$ to prevent
numerical overflow.  Common choices include $n = -4$ (Born-regime
Yukawa) and $n = -2$.

In all cases, $v$ is the physical relative velocity $\vrel$ of the
pair in $\kms$.

\subsection{Angular Distributions}
\label{sec:sidm_angular}

\subsubsection{Isotropic scattering}
\label{sec:sidm_isotropic}

The default angular distribution is isotropic: the scattered
direction $\hat{\mathbf{n}}$ is drawn uniformly on the unit sphere,
\begin{align}
\cos\theta &= 2\mathcal{R}_1 - 1, \notag\\
\phi &= 2\pi\,\mathcal{R}_2, \notag\\
\hat{\mathbf{n}} &= (\sin\theta\cos\phi,\,
  \sin\theta\sin\phi,\,\cos\theta),
\label{eq:isotropic}
\end{align}
where $\mathcal{R}_1, \mathcal{R}_2 \sim \mathcal{U}(0,1)$.

\subsubsection{Anisotropic (Rutherford-like) scattering}
\label{sec:sidm_anisotropic}

For light-mediator models, the differential cross-section is
forward-peaked.  We implement a regularised Rutherford distribution
\citep{Kahlhoefer2014,Robertson2017}:
\begin{equation}
\frac{\mathrm{d}\sigma}{\mathrm{d}\Omega}
  \propto \frac{1}{(1 - \cos\theta + 2\varepsilon)^2},
\label{eq:rutherford}
\end{equation}
where $\varepsilon$ is a regularisation parameter controlling the
degree of forward peaking ($\varepsilon \to 0$: strongly
forward-peaked; $\varepsilon \to \infty$: isotropic).  The scattering
angle is drawn by CDF inversion:
\begin{equation}
\cos\theta = 1 + 2\varepsilon
  - \frac{1}{b + \mathcal{R}(a - b)},
\label{eq:rutherford_sample}
\end{equation}
where $a = 1/(2\varepsilon)$ and $b = 1/(2+2\varepsilon)$.  The
azimuthal angle $\phi$ is uniform on $[0,2\pi)$.

The scattered direction in the lab frame is obtained by constructing
an orthonormal basis $(\hat{\mathbf{v}}_{\rm rel},\,\hat{\mathbf{e}}_1,\,
\hat{\mathbf{e}}_2)$ aligned with the incident relative velocity and
rotating $\hat{\mathbf{v}}_{\rm rel}$ by $(\theta,\phi)$ in this
basis.

\subsection{Inelastic Scattering (iSIDM)}
\label{sec:sidm_inelastic}

We implement a two-state inelastic SIDM model following
\citet{TuckerSmith2001} and \citet{Vogelsberger2019}.  Dark matter
particles exist in two internal states --- ground ($g$, marked by
\code{tp}$\,=0$) and excited ($e$, marked by \code{tp}$\,=-1$) ---
separated by a mass splitting $\delta$ (specified in keV).

All kinematically allowed scattering channels are implemented, with
equal probability assigned among them:
\begin{enumerate}
\item $g + g \to g + e$ (single up-scatter): requires
  $\frac{1}{2}\mu\,\vrel^2 > \delta$, costs kinetic energy~$\delta$.

\item $g + g \to e + e$ (double up-scatter): requires
  $\frac{1}{2}\mu\,\vrel^2 > 2\delta$, costs~$2\delta$.

\item $e + e \to e + g$ (single down-scatter): always allowed,
  releases~$\delta$.

\item $e + e \to g + g$ (double down-scatter): always allowed,
  releases~$2\delta$.

\item $g + e \to g + g$ (mixed, exothermic): always allowed,
  releases~$\delta$.

\item $g + e \to e + e$ (mixed, endothermic): requires
  $\frac{1}{2}\mu\,\vrel^2 > \delta$, costs~$\delta$.
\end{enumerate}
For each pair, the number of kinematically available inelastic
channels $N_{\rm ch}$ is determined, and one is chosen uniformly at
random.  The post-scattering relative speed is adjusted according to
the net energy change:
$|\vrel'| = \sqrt{2(E_{\rm cm} + \Delta E_{\rm inel})/\mu}$,
where $\Delta E_{\rm inel} = \pm n\delta$ for $n = 1$ or~2
transitions.

At startup, a fraction \code{sidm\_frac\_excited} of DM particles is
randomly assigned to the excited state.  This initialisation is
restart-safe: if excited particles already exist in the restart file,
the initialisation is skipped.

\subsection{Timestep Constraint}
\label{sec:sidm_timestep}

The scattering probability (equation~\ref{eq:scatter_prob}) must
remain below unity for the Monte Carlo algorithm to be valid.  We
enforce this by tracking the maximum probability $P_{\max}$ at each
AMR level during the scattering sweep and imposing a timestep
constraint in the Courant condition:
\begin{equation}
\Delta t_{\rm new} \le \frac{C_{\rm SIDM}}{P_{\max}}\,\Delta t_{\rm old},
\label{eq:sidm_dt}
\end{equation}
where $C_{\rm SIDM}$ (default 0.1) is the SIDM Courant number
specified by \code{sidm\_courant}.  This ensures that the scattering
probability remains below $C_{\rm SIDM}$ at the next step.

\subsection{OpenMP Thread Safety}
\label{sec:sidm_omp}

The scattering algorithm is parallelised over grids using the
\curamses\ \code{pthreadLinkedList} OpenMP dispatch, following the
pattern established for stellar feedback \citep{Kim2026}.  Thread
safety requires careful handling of the random number generator (RNG)
state.  We maintain a per-thread seed array
\code{sidm\_seeds(IRandNumSize, 0:nthreads-1)}, initialised from
\ramses's global \code{localseed} with 100 RNG advances between
consecutive threads to decorrelate the streams.  After the OpenMP
region, \code{localseed} is updated from the last thread's seed
(advanced 100 steps) for deterministic reproducibility across
successive calls.

Thread-local diagnostic counters (scatter counts, momentum and energy
residuals) are accumulated to shared totals via \code{!\$omp critical}
sections.

\subsection{Conservation Diagnostics}
\label{sec:sidm_conservation}

For each scattering event, the code tracks the cumulative changes in
momentum ($\Delta\mathbf{p}$) and kinetic energy ($\Delta E_{\rm k}$),
which should be zero for elastic scattering.  These are reduced across
MPI ranks (\code{MPI\_SUM} for totals, \code{MPI\_MAX} for
worst-case single-event residuals) and reported in the simulation log,
providing runtime verification of the conservation properties.

\subsection{Namelist Configuration}
\label{sec:sidm_namelist}

\subsection{Dissipative SIDM (dSIDM)}
\label{sec:sidm_dissipative}

Following \citet{Shen2021}, we implement a simple dissipative SIDM
model in which a fraction $f_{\rm diss}$ of the centre-of-mass kinetic
energy is lost in each scattering event.  After computing the
post-scatter relative velocity magnitude $|\vrel'|$ (including any
inelastic energy change), we apply
\begin{equation}
|\vrel'| \leftarrow |\vrel'| \sqrt{1 - f_{\rm diss}},
\end{equation}
controlled by the namelist parameter \code{sidm\_fdiss} $\in [0,1)$.
The dissipated energy is tracked cumulatively and reported in the
simulation log.  Setting $f_{\rm diss} = 0$ recovers elastic (or
inelastic) SIDM.

\subsection{DM--Baryon Drag Force (IDM)}
\label{sec:sidm_baryon}

We implement a continuous drag force between dark matter particles and
the baryonic gas, following \citet{Nguyen2025}.  Each DM particle
experiences a deceleration proportional to the local gas density and
relative velocity:
\begin{equation}
\frac{\mathrm{d}\mathbf{v}_{\rm DM}}{\mathrm{d}t}
= -\frac{\sigma_{\rm DM\text{-}b}}{m_{\rm DM}}
  \,\rho_{\rm b}\,|\mathbf{v}_{\rm rel}|\,\mathbf{v}_{\rm rel},
\label{eq:idm_drag}
\end{equation}
where $\mathbf{v}_{\rm rel} = \mathbf{v}_{\rm DM} -
\mathbf{v}_{\rm gas}$ and $\sigma_{\rm DM\text{-}b}/m_{\rm DM}$ is
the DM--baryon cross-section per DM mass.  A velocity-dependent
cross-section $\sigma \propto v^n$ is supported via
\code{sidm\_baryon\_power}.

The velocity kick is capped at 50\% of $|\vrel|$ per timestep to
prevent numerical overshoot.  Momentum is conserved by applying the
opposite impulse to the gas cell:
\begin{equation}
\Delta(\rho\mathbf{v})_{\rm gas}
= -\frac{m_{\rm DM}\,\Delta\mathbf{v}_{\rm DM}}{V_{\rm cell}}.
\end{equation}

The drag is applied at each AMR level after the DM--DM scattering
sweep.  Activation requires \code{sidm=.true.},
\code{sidm\_baryon=.true.}, and \code{hydro=.true.}.

\section{MODIFIED NEWTONIAN DYNAMICS (QUMOND AND AQUAL)}
\label{sec:mond}

\subsection{Physical Motivation}
\label{sec:mond_motivation}

Modified Newtonian Dynamics \citep{Milgrom1983} postulates that the
effective gravitational acceleration deviates from the Newtonian
prediction $\gN$ when $|\gN| \lesssim \azero$, where $\azero \approx
1.2 \times 10^{-8}\,\mathrm{cm\,s^{-2}}$ is a universal acceleration
scale.  MOND successfully reproduces observed galaxy rotation curves,
the baryonic Tully--Fisher relation, and the radial acceleration
relation without invoking dark matter on galactic scales
\citep{Famaey2012,Milgrom2014,McGaugh2016}.

A key requirement for $N$-body implementations is that the modified
gravitational field be derivable from a potential (i.e.\ curl-free), so
that energy is conserved.  The quasi-linear formulation of MOND
(QUMOND; \citealt{Milgrom2010}) achieves this by defining the MOND
potential $\Phi$ through a modified Poisson equation:
\begin{equation}
\nabla^2 \Phi = \nabla \cdot \bigl[\nu(|\nabla\Phi_{\rm N}|/\azero)
\,\nabla\Phi_{\rm N}\bigr],
\label{eq:qumond}
\end{equation}
where $\Phi_{\rm N}$ is the standard Newtonian potential satisfying
$\nabla^2 \Phi_{\rm N} = 4\pi G\rho$, and $\nu(y)$ is an
interpolation function satisfying $\nu \to 1$ for $y \gg 1$
(Newtonian regime) and $\nu \to y^{-1/2}$ for $y \ll 1$ (deep-MOND
regime).

An alternative, more fundamental formulation is AQUAL (AQUAdratic
Lagrangian; \citealt{Bekenstein1984}), which derives from an action
principle.  The AQUAL modified Poisson equation is
\begin{equation}
\nabla \cdot \bigl[\mu(|\nabla\Phi|/\azero)\,\nabla\Phi\bigr]
= 4\pi G\rho,
\label{eq:aqual}
\end{equation}
where $\mu(x)$ is the MOND interpolation function satisfying
$\mu \to 1$ for $x \gg 1$ and $\mu \to x$ for $x \ll 1$.  Unlike
QUMOND, the coefficient $\mu$ depends on the MOND potential $\Phi$
itself, making equation~(\ref{eq:aqual}) nonlinear.  The $\mu$ and
$\nu$ functions are related: for the simple family, $\mu(x) = x/(1+x)$
and $\nu(y) = \tfrac{1}{2} + \tfrac{1}{2}\sqrt{1+4/y}$, where
$y = x\mu(x)$.

\subsection{Interpolation Functions}
\label{sec:mond_nu}

We implement two standard interpolation functions, selected by
\code{mond\_mu\_type}:
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Simple} (\code{mond\_mu\_type=1}): corresponding to
  $\mu(x)=x/(1+x)$, the $\nu$-function is
  \begin{equation}
  \nu(y) = \frac{1}{2} + \frac{1}{2}\sqrt{1 + 4/y}.
  \label{eq:nu_simple}
  \end{equation}

\item \textbf{Standard} (\code{mond\_mu\_type=2}): corresponding to
  $\mu(x)=x/\sqrt{1+x^2}$, the $\nu$-function is
  \begin{equation}
  \nu(y) = \sqrt{\frac{1}{2} + \frac{1}{2}\sqrt{1 + 4/y^2}}.
  \label{eq:nu_standard}
  \end{equation}
\end{itemize}
Here $y \equiv |\gN|/\azero$ with $\gN = -\nabla\Phi_{\rm N}$.  Both
functions are evaluated cell-by-cell from the Newtonian force array
$f_i = -\partial\Phi_{\rm N}/\partial x_i$ that is already computed by
\code{force\_fine}.

For AQUAL (Phase~2), the corresponding $\mu$-functions are:
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Simple}: $\mu(x) = x/(1+x)$, so $\mu - 1 = -1/(1+x)$.
\item \textbf{Standard}: $\mu(x) = x/\sqrt{1+x^2}$, so
  $\mu - 1 = x/\sqrt{1+x^2} - 1$.
\end{itemize}
Note that $\mu - 1 < 0$ always (since $\mu < 1$), whereas
$\nu - 1 > 0$ always (since $\nu > 1$).

\subsection{Phase~0: Algebraic Force Correction}
\label{sec:mond_phase0}

The simplest implementation (\code{mond\_type=0}) applies the QUMOND
correction algebraically:
\begin{equation}
f_i^{\rm MOND} = \nu(|\mathbf{f}|/\azero)\, f_i,
\label{eq:mond_algebraic}
\end{equation}
where $\mathbf{f}$ is the Newtonian force vector.  This is performed
in the subroutine \code{apply\_mond\_force}, called immediately after
\code{gradient\_phi} within \code{force\_fine}.

This approach is computationally inexpensive (a single pass over all
cells with no additional Poisson solve) and sufficient for
spherically symmetric mass distributions where the curl of $\nu\gN$
vanishes by symmetry.  However, for general mass distributions the
resulting field $\nu\gN$ is not curl-free, leading to a
non-conservative force and energy conservation violations.

\subsection{Phase~1: Full QUMOND with Phantom Density}
\label{sec:mond_phase1}

To guarantee a curl-free MOND gravitational field, the full QUMOND
formulation (\code{mond\_type=1}) introduces a \emph{phantom dark
matter density} $\rhoph$ and re-solves the Poisson equation.  Expanding
equation~(\ref{eq:qumond}):
\begin{equation}
\nabla^2 \Phi = \nabla^2 \Phi_{\rm N} + \nabla\cdot\bigl[(\nu-1)
\nabla\Phi_{\rm N}\bigr]
= 4\pi G\rho + 4\pi G\rhoph,
\label{eq:phantom_split}
\end{equation}
so the phantom density is
\begin{equation}
\rhoph = \frac{1}{4\pi G}\,\nabla\cdot\bigl[(\nu-1)
\nabla\Phi_{\rm N}\bigr]
= -\frac{1}{4\pi G}\,\nabla\cdot\bigl[(\nu-1)\,\mathbf{f}\bigr],
\label{eq:rho_phantom}
\end{equation}
where we used $\nabla\Phi_{\rm N} = -\mathbf{f}$.  In cosmological
mode, $4\pi G$ is replaced by $\tfrac{3}{2}\Om a$.

\subsubsection{Divergence computation}

The divergence in equation~(\ref{eq:rho_phantom}) is computed using
second-order centred differences on the AMR grid:
\begin{equation}
\nabla\cdot\bigl[(\nu-1)\mathbf{f}\bigr] \approx
\sum_{d=1}^{3} \frac{h_d^{+} - h_d^{-}}{2\,\Delta x},
\label{eq:div_fd}
\end{equation}
where $h_d^{\pm} = [\nu(|\mathbf{f}|/\azero) - 1]\,f_d$ evaluated at
the right ($+$) and left ($-$) face-neighbours in dimension $d$.  The
neighbour cell indices are obtained from the same \code{ggg}/\code{hhh}
lookup tables used by \code{gradient\_phi} in \code{force\_fine},
ensuring consistent stencil geometry.  At coarse--fine level boundaries
where a face-neighbour grid does not exist at the current refinement
level (\code{igridn}${}=0$), the cell is skipped; this affects only a
small fraction of cells near AMR boundaries.

\subsubsection{Algorithm}

The full QUMOND algorithm within a single AMR level proceeds as:
\begin{enumerate}
\item Solve the standard Poisson equation:
  $\nabla^2\Phi_{\rm N} = 4\pi G\rho$.
\item Compute Newtonian forces:
  $\mathbf{f} = -\nabla\Phi_{\rm N}$ via \code{force\_fine}.
\item Compute phantom density $\rhoph$ from equation~(\ref{eq:rho_phantom})
  and add to $\rho$ in-place (\code{compute\_mond\_phantom\_density}).
\item Exchange boundaries for the modified $\rho$.
\item Re-solve the Poisson equation:
  $\nabla^2\Phi = 4\pi G(\rho + \rhoph)$.
\item Compute MOND forces:
  $\mathbf{f}_{\rm MOND} = -\nabla\Phi$ via \code{force\_fine}.
\end{enumerate}
Steps 3--6 are inserted in \code{amr\_step} between the initial
\code{force\_fine} call and the particle synchronisation
(\code{synchro\_fine}).  The algebraic correction
(\code{apply\_mond\_force}) is automatically disabled when
\code{mond\_type}$\geq 1$.

\subsubsection{Thread safety}

The phantom density computation is fully thread-safe under OpenMP:
each cell's $\rho$ is modified by exactly one thread (the cell index
is determined uniquely by the grid--octant pair in the parallel loop),
while the neighbour force values $\mathbf{f}$ are accessed read-only.
All temporary variables (neighbour indices, $\nu-1$ values, divergence
accumulator) are declared \code{private}.

\subsection{Phase~2: AQUAL Iterative Solver}
\label{sec:mond_phase2}

The AQUAL formulation (\code{mond\_type=2}) solves the nonlinear
modified Poisson equation~(\ref{eq:aqual}) self-consistently via
fixed-point iteration.  Rewriting equation~(\ref{eq:aqual}) as
\begin{equation}
\nabla^2 \Phi = 4\pi G\rho + \nabla\cdot\bigl[(\mu - 1)\,\mathbf{f}\bigr],
\label{eq:aqual_split}
\end{equation}
where $\mathbf{f} = -\nabla\Phi$ and $\mu = \mu(|\mathbf{f}|/\azero)$,
we define an AQUAL phantom density
\begin{equation}
\rhoph^{\rm AQUAL} = +\frac{1}{4\pi G}\,\nabla\cdot\bigl[(\mu - 1)
\,\mathbf{f}\bigr].
\label{eq:rho_phantom_aqual}
\end{equation}
Note the sign difference from the QUMOND phantom density
(equation~\ref{eq:rho_phantom}): the QUMOND term uses $\nu - 1 > 0$
with a minus sign, while the AQUAL term uses $\mu - 1 < 0$ with a
plus sign; both yield positive $\rhoph$ in the deep-MOND regime.

\subsubsection{Algorithm}

The AQUAL iteration proceeds as follows:
\begin{enumerate}
\item Solve the standard Poisson equation to obtain the Newtonian force
  $\mathbf{f}^{(0)} = -\nabla\Phi_{\rm N}$.
\item For $n = 0, 1, \ldots, n_{\rm iter}$:
  \begin{enumerate}
  \item Restore $\rho$ to the baryonic density $\rho_{\rm bary}$.
  \item Compute the AQUAL phantom density
    $\rhoph^{(n)} = +\nabla\cdot[(\mu-1)\mathbf{f}^{(n)}]/(4\pi G)$.
  \item Add $\rhoph^{(n)}$ to $\rho$ and exchange boundaries.
  \item Re-solve the Poisson equation:
    $\nabla^2\Phi^{(n+1)} = 4\pi G(\rho + \rhoph^{(n)})$.
  \item Compute $\mathbf{f}^{(n+1)} = -\nabla\Phi^{(n+1)}$.
  \item Check convergence:
    $\max|\mathbf{f}^{(n+1)} - \mathbf{f}^{(n)}| /
    \max|\mathbf{f}^{(n+1)}| < \epsilon$.
  \end{enumerate}
\item Return the converged force $\mathbf{f}^{(\rm final)}$.
\end{enumerate}
The convergence criterion $\epsilon$ is controlled by
\code{mond\_eps} (default $10^{-6}$).  Convergence is monitored
globally across all MPI ranks via \code{MPI\_ALLREDUCE} with
the \code{MPI\_MAX} operator.

\subsubsection{Implementation}

The iteration is implemented in the subroutine \code{aqual\_iterate},
called from \code{amr\_step} when \code{mond\_type=2}.  The
baryonic density is saved in a temporary array at the start of the
iteration and restored at each step to prevent accumulation of
phantom density.  The existing \code{compute\_mond\_phantom\_density}
routine is reused with a logical flag \code{is\_aqual} that selects
between $\nu - 1$ (QUMOND) and $\mu - 1$ (AQUAL), and applies the
appropriate sign convention.

Each iteration calls the full multigrid (or FFT) Poisson solver and
\code{force\_fine}, which automatically use the GPU-accelerated or
FFTW3 code paths when available.  The multigrid solver overwrites
the $f()$ array internally (using it as scratch for
residual/RHS/mask), so \code{force\_fine} must be called after each
solve to reconstruct the forces.

\subsection{External Field Effect}
\label{sec:mond_efe}

In MOND, an external gravitational field $\mathbf{g}_{\rm ext}$
breaks the internal dynamics of a subsystem, even if it is
uniform (the ``external field effect'' or EFE;
\citealt{Milgrom1983,Bekenstein1984}).  This is relevant for
isolated galaxy simulations embedded in a large-scale tidal field.

We implement the EFE by replacing $\mathbf{f}$ with
$\mathbf{f} + \mathbf{g}_{\rm ext}$ in the argument of the
interpolation function.  The external field is specified via
\code{g\_ext\_mond(1:3)} in CGS units and converted to code
units at each timestep.  The EFE modifies each MOND mode:
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Phase~0}: $f_d^{\rm MOND} = \nu(|\mathbf{f}+
  \mathbf{g}_{\rm ext}|/\azero)\,f_d + (\nu - 1)\,g_{{\rm ext},d}$.
\item \textbf{Phase~1}: the divergence uses $(\nu-1)
  (f_d + g_{{\rm ext},d})$.
\item \textbf{Phase~2}: the divergence uses $(\mu-1)
  (f_d + g_{{\rm ext},d})$.
\end{itemize}
When $\mathbf{g}_{\rm ext} = 0$ (the default), all expressions reduce
to the standard EFE-free forms.  For cosmological periodic boxes the
EFE is automatically zero.

\subsection{Cosmological Considerations}
\label{sec:mond_cosmo}

In cosmological $N$-body simulations, the Poisson equation uses the
density contrast and the force $\mathbf{f}$ represents the peculiar
gravitational acceleration (the perturbation from the homogeneous
Hubble flow).  The MOND correction is therefore applied to $|\mathbf{f}|$
rather than to the total gravitational acceleration.  This ``naive
cosmological QUMOND'' approach is the standard choice for MOND
$N$-body cosmological codes
\citep{Lughausen2015,Candlish2015,Nagesh2021} and is valid in the
quasi-static regime relevant for structure formation.

The critical acceleration $\azero$ is specified in CGS units
($\mathrm{cm\,s^{-2}}$) and converted to code units at each timestep
via
\begin{equation}
\azero^{\rm code} = \azero / (L_{\rm box}/t_{\rm code}^2),
\end{equation}
where $L_{\rm box}$ and $t_{\rm code}$ are the time-dependent code
length and time units returned by the \ramses\ \code{units()} routine.
Since these depend on the scale factor $a$, the code-unit threshold
evolves correctly with cosmic time.

\subsection{Performance Impact}
\label{sec:mond_perf}

Phase~0 (\code{mond\_type=0}) adds negligible overhead: a single pass
over all cells to multiply $\mathbf{f}$ by $\nu$.  Phase~1
(\code{mond\_type=1}) approximately doubles the Poisson solver cost
per level, since the multigrid (or FFT) solve and \code{force\_fine}
are each called twice.  For typical production runs where the Poisson
solver accounts for ${\sim}36$\,per\,cent of the total step time, the
overall cost increase is ${\sim}36$\,per\,cent (i.e.\ the total step
time increases from $1.0\times$ to ${\sim}1.36\times$).

Phase~2 (\code{mond\_type=2}) requires $N_{\rm iter}+1$ Poisson
solves and force computations per level.  With a typical convergence
in 5--10 iterations, the cost is ${\sim}6$--$11\times$ the baseline
Poisson cost.  Using the FFTW3 base-level solver (28.9\,s per
coarse step), Phase~2 with 10 iterations would cost ${\sim}300$\,s
per coarse step.  This is acceptable for isolated galaxy simulations
with moderate box sizes but prohibitive for large cosmological volumes.

\subsection{Namelist Configuration}
\label{sec:mond_namelist}

The MOND module is enabled by setting \code{use\_mond=.true.} in
\code{\&RUN\_PARAMS} and providing the physical parameters in a
dedicated \code{\&MOND\_PARAMS} block:

% 7. f(R) HU-SAWICKI GRAVITY
% ===================================================================
\section{$f(R)$ HU--SAWICKI GRAVITY}
\label{sec:fR}

\subsection{Theory}

$\fR$ gravity \citep{Sotiriou2010} replaces the Einstein--Hilbert
action with $S = \int d^4x\,\sqrt{-g}\,[R + f(R)]/(16\pi G)$, where
$f(R)$ is a nonlinear function of the Ricci scalar.  The
Hu--Sawicki model \citep{HuSawicki2007} is defined by
\begin{equation}
f(R) = -m^2 \frac{c_1 (R/m^2)^n}{c_2(R/m^2)^n + 1},
\label{eq:fR_HS}
\end{equation}
where $m^2 = H_0^2 \Om$ and the ratio $c_1/c_2$ is fixed by the
requirement $f(R_0) \approx -2\Lambda$.  The scalar degree of freedom
$f_R \equiv df/dR$ obeys a Klein--Gordon equation that, in the
quasi-static limit, reduces to
\begin{equation}
\nabla^2 f_R = -\frac{a^2}{3}\bigl[R(f_R) - \bar{R}\bigr]
  + \frac{a^2}{3}\,8\pi G\,\delta\rho,
\label{eq:fR_eom}
\end{equation}
where $\bar{R}$ is the background Ricci scalar and $\delta\rho$ is the
matter overdensity.  The Hu--Sawicki inversion
$R(f_R) = \bar{R}_0\,[-n\,\fRz / f_R]^{1/(n+1)}$ maps the field
value back to the curvature.  In high-density regions, the effective
mass of $f_R$ becomes large, suppressing deviations from GR ---
the \emph{chameleon screening mechanism}.

\subsection{Solver}

We solve equation~(\ref{eq:fR_eom}) on each AMR level using
Newton--Gauss--Seidel (N-GS) relaxation with red--black ordering.
The field $f_R$ is stored in the \code{scalar\_gr} array, initialised
to the background value $\bar{f}_R(a)$ on the first step and
warm-started from the previous step thereafter.  The Jacobian of the
residual is
\begin{equation}
\frac{\partial F}{\partial f_R}\bigg|_c
  = -\frac{6}{\Delta x^2}
    - \frac{a^2}{3}\frac{dR}{df_R},
\end{equation}
which includes both the standard Laplacian stencil and the nonlinear
$R(f_R)$ term.  The fifth force is
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{F}_5 = -\tfrac{1}{2}\,\nabla f_R,
\end{equation}
computed via the shared \code{compute\_fifth\_force} routine.
Convergence is monitored by
$\max|F| / \max|S| < \epsilon$ with default $\epsilon = 10^{-6}$.

\subsection{Namelist Configuration}

The $\fR$ module is controlled by \code{use\_fR=.true.} in
\code{\&RUN\_PARAMS} and the dedicated \code{\&FR\_PARAMS} block:
\code{fR0} (amplitude, $< 0$; default $-10^{-6}$),
\code{fR\_n} (Hu--Sawicki index; default 1),
\code{n\_iter\_fR} (max iterations; default 20),
\code{fR\_eps} (convergence threshold; default $10^{-6}$).

% 8. nDGP GRAVITY
% ===================================================================
\section{nDGP BRANEWORLD GRAVITY}
\label{sec:nDGP}

\subsection{Theory}

The Dvali--Gabadadze--Porrati (DGP) model \citep{DGP2000} embeds our
4D spacetime in a 5D bulk, with a crossover scale $r_c$ below which
gravity is 4-dimensional.  In the quasi-static limit, the scalar
degree of freedom $\varphi$ (the brane-bending mode) satisfies
\begin{equation}
\nabla^2\varphi + \frac{r_c^2}{3\beta a^2}
\bigl[(\nabla^2\varphi)^2 - (\nabla_i\nabla_j\varphi)^2\bigr]
  = \frac{\Om\,a}{\beta}\,\delta,
\label{eq:nDGP_eom}
\end{equation}
where $\beta(a) = 1 + 2 H r_c (1 + \dot{H}/(3H^2))$ and
$\delta = \delta\rho/\bar\rho$.  The nonlinear derivative terms
provide \emph{Vainshtein screening}: near massive objects the
second-order operators dominate, suppressing the scalar force.

The normal branch (nDGP; $\epsilon = +1$) does not self-accelerate and
requires an explicit cosmological constant, but provides a useful
testbed for Vainshtein screening.  The self-accelerating branch
($\epsilon = -1$) suffers from ghost instabilities but is included
for completeness.

\subsection{Solver}

We solve equation~(\ref{eq:nDGP_eom}) using Newton--Gauss--Seidel
relaxation, identical in structure to the $\fR$ solver but with the
additional Vainshtein bilinear term.  The diagonal approximation
\citep{Li2013} drops off-diagonal second derivatives
($\partial_i\partial_j\varphi$ with $i \neq j$), which is accurate to
the level of the grid resolution.  The Jacobian is
\begin{equation}
J = -\frac{6}{\Delta x^2}
  + \frac{r_c^2}{3\beta a^2}\cdot 2\nabla^2\varphi\cdot
    \Bigl(-\frac{4}{\Delta x^2}\Bigr).
\end{equation}
The fifth force is $\mathbf{F}_5 = -[1/(2\beta)]\,\nabla\varphi$,
computed via \code{compute\_fifth\_force}.

\subsection{Namelist Configuration}

Controlled by \code{use\_nDGP=.true.} in \code{\&RUN\_PARAMS} and
\code{\&NDGP\_PARAMS}:
\code{omega\_rc} ($\Omega_{r_c} = 1/(4r_c^2 H_0^2)$; default 0.25),
\code{nDGP\_branch} ($+1$ normal, $-1$ self-accelerating),
\code{n\_iter\_nDGP} (default 20),
\code{nDGP\_eps} (default $10^{-6}$).

% 9. SYMMETRON GRAVITY
% ===================================================================
\section{SYMMETRON GRAVITY}
\label{sec:symmetron}

\subsection{Theory}

The Symmetron model \citep{Hinterbichler2010,Hinterbichler2011}
features a scalar field $\phi$ with a $\mathbb{Z}_2$-symmetric
potential
\begin{equation}
V(\phi) = -\frac{\mu^2}{2}\phi^2 + \frac{\lambda}{4}\phi^4
  + V_0,
\end{equation}
and a universal coupling to matter
$A(\phi) = 1 + \phi^2/(2M^2)$.
The effective potential in a region of density $\rho$ is
\begin{equation}
V_{\rm eff}(\phi)
  = \frac{1}{2}\Bigl(\frac{\rho}{\rho_{\rm ssb}} - 1\Bigr)
    \frac{\mu^2}{2}\phi^2
  + \frac{\lambda}{4}\phi^4,
\end{equation}
where $\rho_{\rm ssb} = \mu^2 M^2$ is the critical density.  When
$\rho > \rho_{\rm ssb}$, the effective mass-squared is positive and the
field sits at $\phi = 0$ (symmetry restored), suppressing the fifth
force.  When $\rho < \rho_{\rm ssb}$, symmetry is spontaneously broken
and $\phi \neq 0$ generates a fifth force --- the
\emph{symmetron screening mechanism}.

In terms of the normalised field $\chi = \phi/\phi_0$ and the
Compton wavelength $L$, the quasi-static equation of motion is
\begin{equation}
\nabla^2\chi = \frac{a^2}{2L^2}
  \biggl[\Bigl(\frac{\rho}{\rho_{\rm ssb}}
    - \frac{a_{\rm ssb}^3}{a^3}\Bigr)\chi + \chi^3\biggr].
\label{eq:symmetron_eom}
\end{equation}

\subsection{Fifth Force}

The Symmetron fifth force is \emph{field-dependent}:
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{F}_5 = -6\,\Om\,\beta_{\rm s}^2\,
  \frac{a_{\rm ssb}}{a}\,\chi\,\nabla\chi,
\label{eq:symmetron_force}
\end{equation}
which differs from the $\fR$ and nDGP cases where the force is a
simple gradient of the scalar field.  A dedicated
\code{compute\_fifth\_force\_symmetron} routine implements the
$\chi\,\nabla\chi$ product.

\subsection{Solver}

The equation is solved by N-GS relaxation.  Denoting the
right-hand side coefficients as $m_{\rm eff}^2 = (a^2/2L^2)
[(\rho/\rho_{\rm ssb}) - (a_{\rm ssb}/a)^3]$ and
$\lambda_{\rm eff} = a^2/(2L^2)$, the residual and Jacobian are
\begin{align}
F &= \nabla^2\chi - m_{\rm eff}^2\,\chi
  - \lambda_{\rm eff}\,\chi^3, \\
J &= -6/\Delta x^2 - m_{\rm eff}^2 - 3\lambda_{\rm eff}\,\chi^2.
\end{align}
The Compton wavelength $L$ is converted from Mpc/$h$ to code units
using $L_{\rm code} = L / L_{\rm box}$.

\subsection{Namelist Configuration}

Controlled by \code{use\_symmetron=.true.} and
\code{\&SYMMETRON\_PARAMS}:
\code{a\_ssb} (SSB scale factor; default 0.5),
\code{beta\_symmetron} (coupling; default 1.0),
\code{L\_symmetron} (range in Mpc/$h$; default 1.0),
\code{n\_iter\_symmetron} (default 20),
\code{symmetron\_eps} (default $10^{-6}$).

\section{DILATON GRAVITY}
\label{sec:dilaton}

\subsection{Theory}

The environmentally dependent Dilaton model
\citep{Brax2010,Brax2011} employs the Damour--Polyakov mechanism in
which the coupling function $A(\phi_D) = 1 + A_2\,\phi_D^2/2$ has a
minimum at the cosmological field value.  The quasi-static field
equation has the same structure as the Symmetron:
\begin{equation}
\nabla^2\phi_D = \frac{a^2}{2L_D^2}
  \biggl[\Bigl(\frac{\rho}{\rho_0}
    - \frac{a_0^3}{a^3}\Bigr)\phi_D
  + \xi_2\,\phi_D^3\biggr],
\label{eq:dilaton_eom}
\end{equation}
where $L_D$ is the Dilaton Compton wavelength, $a_0$ is the
transition scale factor, and $\xi_2$ parameterises the cubic
self-interaction (set to unity in our implementation).

\subsection{Fifth Force}

The Dilaton fifth force is also field-dependent:
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{F}_5 = -2\,\beta_D^2\,\phi_D\,\nabla\phi_D,
\label{eq:dilaton_force}
\end{equation}
implemented in a dedicated \code{compute\_fifth\_force\_dilaton}
routine, structurally identical to the Symmetron force routine but
with different coupling coefficients.

\subsection{Solver and Namelist}

The solver is identical in structure to the Symmetron solver with
$(a_{\rm ssb}, L_{\rm sym}) \to (a_0, L_D)$.  Parameters are set
via \code{use\_dilaton=.true.} and \code{\&DILATON\_PARAMS}:
\code{beta\_dilaton} (default 1.0),
\code{L\_dilaton} (Mpc/$h$; default 1.0),
\code{a0\_dilaton} (default 0.5),
\code{n\_iter\_dilaton} (default 20),
\code{dilaton\_eps} (default $10^{-6}$).

\section{CUBIC GALILEON GRAVITY}
\label{sec:galileon}

\subsection{Theory}

The cubic Galileon \citep{Nicolis2009,Deffayet2009a} is a scalar field
theory with second-order equations of motion and a shift symmetry
$\phi \to \phi + c$.  The Lagrangian is
$\mathcal{L} = c_2\,(\partial\phi)^2 + (c_3/\Lambda^3)\,
\Box\phi\,(\partial\phi)^2$, and the quasi-static field equation is
\begin{equation}
\nabla^2\phi_G + \frac{c_3}{c_2 H_0^2 a^2}
\bigl[(\nabla^2\phi_G)^2
  - (\nabla_i\nabla_j\phi_G)^2\bigr]
= \frac{\Om\,a}{\betaG}\,\delta,
\label{eq:galileon_eom}
\end{equation}
where $\betaG(a) = c_2/(6\,c_3\,H)$ and $H$ is the Hubble parameter.
This has the same Vainshtein operator as the nDGP equation
(\ref{eq:nDGP_eom}), with $r_c^2/(3\beta)$ replaced by
$c_3/(c_2\,H_0^2)$.

\subsection{Solver}

We reuse the nDGP Gauss--Seidel infrastructure with substituted
coefficients:
$\mathrm{coeff}_G = c_3/(c_2\,a^2)$ and source
$= \Om\,a\,\delta/\betaG$.  The fifth force is
$\mathbf{F}_5 = -[1/(2\betaG)]\,\nabla\phi_G$, computed via the
shared \code{compute\_fifth\_force} routine.

\subsection{Namelist Configuration}

Controlled by \code{use\_galileon=.true.} and
\code{\&GALILEON\_PARAMS}:
\code{c2\_galileon} (kinetic coefficient; default $-1.0$),
\code{c3\_galileon} (cubic coefficient; default 1.0),
\code{n\_iter\_galileon} (default 20),
\code{galileon\_eps} (default $10^{-6}$).

\section{COUPLED DARK ENERGY}
\label{sec:coupled_de}

\subsection{Theory}

Coupled Dark Energy (CDE) models
\citep{Amendola2000,Amendola2004,Pettorino2008} introduce a direct
interaction between dark energy and dark matter in the Einstein frame.
For a constant coupling $\betacde$, the effective gravitational
constant experienced by dark matter particles is enhanced:
\begin{equation}
\Geff = G\,(1 + 2\betacde^2).
\label{eq:Geff_cde}
\end{equation}
Baryons remain uncoupled and feel only the standard gravitational
force.  For simplicity, our implementation applies the enhancement
to all matter (DM + baryons), which is exact when baryons are a
subdominant component and provides a useful approximation otherwise.

\subsection{Implementation}

After the standard gradient of the gravitational potential is computed
in \code{force\_fine}, the \code{apply\_coupled\_de\_force} subroutine
multiplies the force array by $(1+2\betacde^2)$:
\begin{equation}
f_d \to (1 + 2\betacde^2)\,f_d, \quad d = 1,2,3.
\end{equation}
No scalar field is required; the enhancement is a simple
post-processing step applied at the same point as the MOND algebraic
correction.

\subsection{Namelist Configuration}

Controlled by \code{use\_coupled\_de=.true.} in
\code{\&RUN\_PARAMS} and \code{\&COUPLED\_DE\_PARAMS}:
\code{beta\_cde} (coupling constant; default 0.1).
Setting $\betacde = 0$ recovers standard gravity.
This module can be used simultaneously with any scalar field model
or with early dark energy.


% ===================================================================
% 13. EARLY DARK ENERGY
% ===================================================================
\section{EARLY DARK ENERGY}
\label{sec:ede}

\subsection{Theory}

Early Dark Energy (EDE) models
\citep{DoranRobbers2006,Poulin2019,Hill2020} introduce a transient
dark energy component at early times that modifies the expansion
history.  We implement the Doran--Robbers parameterisation in which
the EDE energy density fraction is
\begin{equation}
f_{\rm ede}(a) = \frac{(a_c/a)^{3(1+w_{\rm ede})}}
  {1 + (a/a_c)^{3(1+w_{\rm ede})}},
\label{eq:f_ede}
\end{equation}
where $a_c = 1/(1+z_{\rm ede})$ is the critical scale factor
and $w_{\rm ede}$ is the EDE equation of state.  For
$a \ll a_c$, $f_{\rm ede} \to 1$, contributing a constant
$\Oede$ to the Friedmann equation; for $a \gg a_c$,
$f_{\rm ede}$ decays as a power law and EDE becomes negligible.

\subsection{Implementation}

The EDE contribution enters the Friedmann equation through the
\code{dadtau} and \code{dadt} functions in \code{init\_time.f90}:
\begin{equation}
H^2(a) = H_0^2\Bigl[\frac{\Om}{a^3} + \Ol\,f_{\rm DE}(a)
  + \Omega_k\,a^{-2} + \Oede\,f_{\rm ede}(a)\Bigr].
\label{eq:friedmann_ede}
\end{equation}
No modification to the force calculation is needed; the effect
propagates entirely through the modified expansion history, which
changes the conformal time--scale factor mapping and thus the
cosmological time stepping.

\subsection{Namelist Configuration}

Controlled by \code{use\_ede=.true.} in \code{\&RUN\_PARAMS} and
\code{\&EDE\_PARAMS}:
\code{omega\_ede} (EDE fraction; default 0.04),
\code{z\_ede} (injection redshift; default 3000),
\code{w\_ede} (EDE equation of state; default 1/3).
Setting $\Oede = 0$ recovers the standard $\Lambda$CDM expansion.
This module can be used simultaneously with any other model.

\section{VERIFICATION}
\label{sec:verification}

We verify each module using cosmological restart tests from a
Horizon Run~5 \citep{Lee2021} snapshot at $a = 0.164$ (output\_00005,
step~241), run with 12 MPI ranks.  The primary diagnostic is the
energy conservation error
\begin{equation}
e_{\rm cons} = \frac{|E_{\rm tot} - E_{\rm tot,0}|}{|E_{\rm tot,0}|},
\label{eq:econs}
\end{equation}
where $E_{\rm tot}$ is the total (kinetic + potential + thermal)
energy.

\subsection{$\Lambda$CDM Recovery}
\label{sec:verify_lcdm}

Each module must reproduce the baseline $\Lambda$CDM result when
nominally enabled but set to produce no physical effect:

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Neutrino}: \code{use\_neutrino=.true.},
  \code{omega\_nu=0}.  The code sets $\Onu/\Ocb = 0$, so the neutrino
  factor in equation~(\ref{eq:green_unified}) is identically unity.
  Result: $e_{\rm cons} = 6.71\times10^{-4}$ at step~242,
  $6.74\times10^{-4}$ at step~243 --- bit-identical to the reference
  run.

\item \textbf{Dark energy}: \code{de\_perturb=.true.} with
  $w_0=-1$, $w_a=0$.  Since $1+w=0$, $\alpha=0$
  (equation~\ref{eq:alpha}), so $\mathcal{F}_{\rm DE}=1$.  Result:
  bit-identical to baseline.

\item \textbf{SIDM}: \code{sidm=.false.}.  The scattering routine is
  not called.  Result: bit-identical to baseline.

\item \textbf{MOND}: \code{use\_mond=.true.} with $\azero$ set to an
  extremely small value (${\sim}10^{-30}\,\mathrm{cm\,s^{-2}}$),
  placing all cells deep in the Newtonian regime ($\nu \approx 1$,
  $\mu \approx 1$).
  All three modes (\code{mond\_type=0}, 1, and 2) recover the
  baseline forces.  Setting \code{use\_mond=.false.} is
  bit-identical to the reference run.

\item \textbf{$\fR$ Hu--Sawicki}:
  \code{use\_fR=.true.} with $|\fRz| \to 0$ (e.g.\
  $\fRz = -10^{-10}$).  The chameleon mass becomes extremely large,
  screening all cells.  The scalar field stays near the background
  value and the fifth force is negligible.

\item \textbf{nDGP}:
  \code{use\_nDGP=.true.} with $\Omega_{r_c} \to \infty$.  In
  this limit $\beta \to \infty$ and the fifth force
  $\propto 1/\beta \to 0$.

\item \textbf{Symmetron}:
  \code{use\_symmetron=.true.} with
  $\beta_{\rm s} \to 0$ ($10^{-10}$).  The fifth force prefactor
  $\propto \beta_{\rm s}^2$ vanishes.

\item \textbf{Dilaton}:
  \code{use\_dilaton=.true.} with
  $\beta_D \to 0$ ($10^{-10}$).  Identical argument.

\item \textbf{Galileon}:
  \code{use\_galileon=.true.} with $c_3 \to 0$ ($10^{-10}$).
  The Vainshtein term vanishes and $\betaG \to \infty$, so
  the fifth force $\propto 1/\betaG \to 0$.

\item \textbf{Coupled DE}:
  \code{use\_coupled\_de=.true.} with $\betacde = 0$.
  $\Geff = G(1+0) = G$, identical to standard gravity.

\item \textbf{Early DE}:
  \code{use\_ede=.true.} with $\Oede = 0$.
  The EDE term in the Friedmann equation vanishes identically,
  recovering standard $\Lambda$CDM expansion.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{SIDM Conservation}
\label{sec:verify_sidm}

For elastic constant-cross-section SIDM with
$\sigmam = 1\,\mathrm{cm^2\,g^{-1}}$, the cumulative momentum and
kinetic energy conservation residuals over 3 coarse steps are:
\begin{align}
|\Delta \mathbf{p}| / |\mathbf{p}_{\rm tot}| &\sim 10^{-15}, \\
|\Delta E_{\rm k}| / |E_{\rm k,tot}| &\sim 10^{-15},
\end{align}
consistent with machine-precision round-off.  The overall energy
conservation error is $e_{\rm cons} = 7.57\times10^{-4}$, comparable
to the $\Lambda$CDM baseline.

\subsection{SIDM Cross-Section Scaling}
\label{sec:verify_scaling}

Increasing $\sigmam$ from 1 to $10\,\mathrm{cm^2\,g^{-1}}$ produces
a proportional increase in the total number of scattering events
(factor ${\sim}10\times$), confirming the linear dependence of
equation~(\ref{eq:scatter_prob}) on $\sigmam$.

\subsection{OpenMP Reproducibility}
\label{sec:verify_omp}

The deterministic per-thread seed initialisation
(Section~\ref{sec:sidm_omp}) ensures that the total scatter count and
per-event diagnostics are identical between OMP${}=1$ and OMP${}=5$
runs, verifying that the threading does not introduce spurious
stochasticity.

\subsection{MOND Phase Consistency}
\label{sec:verify_mond}

For a spherically symmetric mass distribution, the curl of
$\nu\gN$ vanishes, so Phase~0 (algebraic) and Phase~1 (phantom
density) should produce identical forces.  We verify this by
comparing the force field at a high-density peak region in the
test snapshot: the relative difference $|\mathbf{f}_{\rm Phase\,0} -
\mathbf{f}_{\rm Phase\,1}|/|\mathbf{f}_{\rm Phase\,1}|$ is at the
$10^{-3}$ level near the peak (where the geometry is approximately
spherical) and reaches a few per~cent in asymmetric regions, as
expected from the non-vanishing curl field that Phase~1 correctly
removes.

For Phase~2 (AQUAL), the converged solution should agree with
QUMOND in the curl-free limit (spherical symmetry).  In general
mass distributions the two formulations differ because AQUAL solves
for a self-consistent potential while QUMOND uses the Newtonian
potential as input.  The AQUAL iteration convergence is monitored
at each step via the relative force change
$\max|\Delta\mathbf{f}|/\max|\mathbf{f}|$.

\subsection{Neutrino Power Spectrum Suppression}
\label{sec:verify_nu_pk}

With $\Onu = 0.00140$ ($\sum m_\nu = 0.06$\,eV), the expected
small-scale power spectrum suppression is
$\Delta P/P \approx -8\,\Onu/\Om \approx -3.6$\,per\,cent.
A quantitative comparison with \camb\ linear theory predictions
requires a larger simulation volume and will be presented in a
forthcoming application paper.

\subsection{Dark Energy Power Spectrum Validation}
\label{sec:verify_de_pk}

We validate the DE perturbation module by comparing matter power
spectra from gravity-only $N$-body simulations against \camb\ linear
theory predictions for four cosmological models:
$\Lambda$CDM ($w_0=-1, w_a=0$),
$w$CDM ($w_0=-0.8, w_a=0$),
CPL thaw ($w_0=-0.9, w_a=0.2$),
and CPL phantom ($w_0=-1.1, w_a=-0.2$).
All runs use $N=256^3$ particles in a $L=500\,h^{-1}\,\mathrm{Mpc}$
box with $\cst=0$ and matching initial conditions from \textsc{music}
\citep{Hahn2011}, evolved from $z=17$ to $z=0$ with six output
redshifts ($z=5, 2, 1, 0.5, 0.2, 0$).  In-situ power spectra are
computed via an NGP grid assignment and FFTW3 FFT at each output.

Fig.~\ref{fig:de_pk_ratio} shows the ratio
$P_{\rm sim}(k) / P_{\rm CAMB}(k)$ for each model.  In the linear
regime ($k < 0.1\,h\,\mathrm{Mpc}^{-1}$), all four models yield
ratios of ${\sim}0.96$, consistent with the expected finite-resolution
and finite-volume effects of a $256^3$ gravity-only simulation.
The wCDM and CPL-thaw models show a 1--2\,per\,cent enhancement
relative to $\Lambda$CDM, reflecting the additional gravitational
potential from DE perturbations captured by the Weyl-potential
$\Rde(k,a)$ table.  For the phantom model, DE perturbations are
disabled ($\code{de\_perturb=F}$) since the Weyl method requires
non-phantom equations of state; the background expansion history alone
correctly reproduces the \camb\ predictions.

\begin{figure*}
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{fig_de_pk_ratio.pdf}
\caption{Ratio of $N$-body matter power spectrum to \camb\ linear
  prediction for four DE models.  Each panel shows one model with six
  output redshifts ($z=0$--5, colour-coded).  The grey band marks
  $\pm 5$\,per\,cent.  All models converge to $P_{\rm sim}/P_{\rm
  CAMB} \approx 0.96$ in the linear regime, consistent with
  finite-resolution effects.}
\label{fig:de_pk_ratio}
\end{figure*}

\section{CONCLUSIONS}
\label{sec:conclusions}

We have presented eleven non-standard cosmological modules for the
\curamses\ AMR code, spanning three broad categories.

\textbf{Poisson solver extensions:}
(i)~CPL dark energy perturbations via a table-based linear response
method using precomputed \camb\ Weyl-potential ratios, valid for
arbitrary sound speed including the clustering limit $\cst = 0$;
(ii)~massive neutrino linear response via scale-dependent
$\Rnu(k,a)$ corrections.

\textbf{Dark matter phenomenology:}
(iii)~SIDM Monte Carlo scattering with constant, velocity-dependent,
anisotropic, and inelastic cross-sections, with machine-precision
conservation;
(iv)~QUMOND and AQUAL (three modes: algebraic, phantom density,
iterative) with external field effect.

\textbf{Scalar field modified gravity:}
(v)~$\fR$ Hu--Sawicki with chameleon screening;
(vi)~nDGP braneworld with Vainshtein screening;
(vii)~Symmetron with spontaneous symmetry-breaking screening;
(viii)~Dilaton with Damour--Polyakov environmentally dependent coupling;
(ix)~cubic Galileon with Vainshtein screening.  All five share a
common Newton--Gauss--Seidel solver infrastructure with red--black
ordering and OpenMP parallelism.

\textbf{Force and background modifications:}
(x)~coupled dark energy with $\Geff = G(1+2\betacde^2)$;
(xi)~early dark energy in the Doran--Robbers parameterisation,
modifying the Friedmann equation at high redshift.

Each module is self-contained, controlled by dedicated namelist
blocks, and verified to recover the $\Lambda$CDM baseline when
disabled.  The six scalar field gravity models (MOND, $\fR$, nDGP,
Symmetron, Dilaton, Galileon) are mutually exclusive, while coupled
DE and early DE can combine freely with any model.

Together with the algorithmic optimizations described in
\citet{Kim2026} --- $k$-section domain decomposition, Morton key
octree, spatial hash feedback, and hybrid CPU/GPU dispatch ---
\curamses\ now provides a comprehensive platform for precision
cosmological simulations that span the standard model and its
extensions across all major theoretical frameworks: dynamical dark
energy, massive neutrinos, self-interacting dark matter, modified
gravity, and modified expansion history.

Future work will apply these modules to constrained-realisation
simulations targeting DESI and Euclid survey volumes, enabling
direct comparison of $\Lambda$CDM, $w_0w_a$CDM, $\nu\Lambda$CDM,
SIDM, MOND, $\fR$, nDGP, Symmetron, Dilaton, and Galileon
predictions for the halo mass function, galaxy rotation curves,
galaxy clustering, and weak lensing observables.

\section{CAMB TABLE FORMAT}
\label{app:table_format}

Both the neutrino and dark energy response tables use the same ASCII
format:
\begin{verbatim}
# Header comments (lines starting with #)
nk na
k_1 k_2 ... k_nk       [h/Mpc]
a_1 a_2 ... a_na
R(1,1) R(2,1) ... R(nk,1)
R(1,2) R(2,2) ... R(nk,2)
...
R(1,na) R(2,na) ... R(nk,na)
\end{verbatim}
where $R(i,j) = R_X(k_i, a_j)$ is the response function.  The
$k$-values are in units of $h\,\mathrm{Mpc}^{-1}$ and the $a$-values
are dimensionless scale factors.  Both are stored in log-spacing but
written in linear values; the code takes $\log_{10}$ internally for
interpolation.

The generation scripts \code{generate\_neutrino\_table.py} and
\code{generate\_de\_table.py} (in \code{misc/}) produce tables in
this format using \camb\ v1.6+.  The DE table uses the Weyl-potential
method (Section~\ref{sec:de_table}) to extract $\Rde$ in the
Newtonian gauge.  Typical generation time is ${\sim}30$\,s per table.
